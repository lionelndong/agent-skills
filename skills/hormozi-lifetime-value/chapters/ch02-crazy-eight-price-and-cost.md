# Chapter 2: The Crazy Eight + Margin Levers (#1 Increase Prices, #2 Decrease Costs)

## Core Idea
The **Crazy Eight** are the eight ways to make a customer worth more. Hormozi built them by breaking the "too broad" maxim — *"there are only two ways to make a customer more valuable… you can increase average order value, or increase the number of times they buy"* — into eight applicable chunks so anyone can become **"fluent in monetization."** The first two — **price and cost** — move *gross profit* directly and so come first.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The Crazy Eight** (Hormozi's authoritative list, exact wording from the page):
  1. Raise prices
  2. Lower the cost of delivering the thing
  3. Upsell Frequency: Get them to buy more again later
  4. Upsell Quantity: Get them to buy more now
  5. Upsell Quality: Get them to buy a premium version
  6. Downsell Quantity: Get them to buy fewer things rather than nothing
  7. Downsell Quality: Get them to buy lower-cost things rather than nothing
  8. Cross-Sell: Get them to buy a different thing on top
  > **Faithfulness note — a real discrepancy in the source:** the body section *headers* number these differently than the summary list above. Body order is: **#1 Increase Prices · #2 Decrease Costs · #3 Increase # of Purchases · #4 Cross-Sell Something Different · #5 Sell More (Quantity) · #6 Sell Better (Quality) · #7 Downsell Fewer (Quantity) · #8 Downsell Lower Quality.** Both numberings are Hormozi's; preserved as-is, not reconciled. Chapters here follow the **body headers**.
- **#1 Increase Prices** — *"You charge more money for the same thing."* Pricing affects gross profit more than any of the eight, so start with it. **The leverage:** *"If you take a 10% profit business, raise the prices by 20%, and keep sales the same… you don't grow the business by 20%... you triple it."* All the extra "drops straight to the bottom line."
  - **How to find the price sweet spot**: maximize **`Sales conversion rate × lifetime gross profit`**. *"The price that gets the most people to buy with the highest gross profit is the price that maximizes profit."* The goal isn't to sell the most stuff — it's to make the most money.
  - **Test cadence**: test prices **every quarter** (Hormozi cites a Profitwell study linking profitability to pricing-test frequency). He once bought a company, doubled the price, changed nothing else, and tripled it.
- **#2 Decrease Costs** — *"You decrease your costs to deliver the same thing."* This raises gross profit too, but unlike price (infinite upside) **costs can only go down to zero**. The thinking makes the business more profitable AND more scalable. Nine tactics (services-focused, transferable):
  1. **Increase the ratio of employees to customers** (rep handles 10 instead of 5 → costs halved).
  2. **Offshore talent** (same skills, lower cost-of-living countries → cut costs 80%+; "free and anyone can do it").
  3. **Sell more similar customers to productize delivery** (same problems → templates + automation → faster, more consistent, more profitable).
  4. **Done For You → Done With You** ("I'll do it for you" → "I'll help you do it"; raises customer-to-employee ratio; profitable if price only dips slightly).
  5. **Cap usage** (e.g. cap revisions; charge per use thereafter).
  6. **Lifetime → annual** (don't service a one-time transaction indefinitely).
  7. **In-person → remote** (fewer travel/sourcing costs for you and the customer).
  8. **Cut meeting times** (delete all recurring meetings each quarter, add back only what's needed, at half the time).
  9. **Buy in bulk & prepay** (lock in 10–20% vendor discounts; "a guaranteed return on investment").

## Key Concepts
- **Gross profit is the lever**: both #1 and #2 act on gross profit, the most powerful term in LTGP.
- **Break-even conversion rate**: the conversion rate at which the new higher price equals the old price's profit; above it = a winner.
- **Productize**: standardize delivery (templates/automation) so similar customers cost less to serve.
- **Conversion × LTGP**: the single quantity price optimization maximizes.

## Mental Models
- **Test price more than anything else** — *"I run pricing tests more than any other thing in business"*; the right price "is usually higher than you think."
- **Price has unlimited upside; cost is capped at zero** — chase price first, then trim cost.
- **Start low, then go up** — make sales first to confirm demand and "run some water through the pipes to see where the leaks are."

## Worked Example: Pricing a 10%-margin business + the "start low" protocol
- A business runs **10% profit**. Raise prices **20%**, hold sales steady → the extra 20% is nearly all margin, so profit roughly **triples** (the original 10 points plus ~20 new points of margin).
- **Start-low protocol**: launch at a price low enough to make sales and confirm demand. Once "things are humming," **nudge the price up 20% every ~10 sales** until you see a *dramatic* drop in sales — then return to the prior sweet spot. If you priced someone too high before realizing it, **add bonuses** or **refund the difference** (Hormozi has done both). *"Testing price is expensive for a few months. But the only thing more expensive is not testing price at all and having a business that's under-monetized for life."*

## Anti-patterns
- **Optimizing for units sold, not money made**: the lowest price often sells the most stuff but makes the least money.
- **Never testing price**: leaves money on the table permanently; the costliest mistake of the eight.
- **Cutting cost in ways that gut the offer**: decreasing cost is fine *to deliver the same thing* — don't degrade what the customer receives (that's a *downsell*, #7/#8, and only for unqualified buyers).
- **Endless scope (no usage cap)**: customers "endlessly drain resources" without a revision/usage cap.

## Key Takeaways
1. Run the Crazy Eight in your head on every product until you're "fluent in monetization."
2. Do **price first** — it hits gross profit hardest; a 20% raise on a 10% business can triple profit.
3. Optimize price by **conversion rate × LTGP**, not by volume; test every quarter.
4. Start low to validate demand, then nudge **+20% every ~10 sales** to the sweet spot.
5. Cut delivery cost via ratio, offshoring, productizing, DFY→DWY, caps, annualizing, remote, fewer meetings, and bulk prepay — without degrading the same deliverable.

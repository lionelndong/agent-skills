# Chapter 1: What Is LTV? (Definition, Why It Matters, How To Calculate It)

## Core Idea
**"Lifetime value (LTV) is the gross profit collected over the lifespan of a customer"** — total money from a customer minus everything it costs to deliver. If advertising is the machine that makes a business grow, **LTV is the fuel**: the owner who makes a customer more valuable than the competition can outbid everyone for attention and "get all the customers to yourself."

## Frameworks Introduced
- **LTV = Lifetime Gross Profit (LTGP) = Customer Lifetime Value (CLV)**: Hormozi's exact words — *"how much total money you make from a customer minus everything it costs you to deliver it. I sometimes call it lifetime gross profit (LTGP). Some places refer to it as customer lifetime value (CLV). Just know, it's the same idea."*
- **The 3-Step LTGP Calculation**: a "back of napkin" method to establish a baseline before you try to raise it.
  - **Step One — Gross Profit**: *"what's leftover from a purchase after you deliver the goods or service."* NOT net profit (which is after all expenses). You use gross profit to pay the rest of your bills.
  - **Step Two — Average transactions OR churn**: figure the average number of transactions a customer makes over their lifetime; for a recurring business, figure **churn** instead.
  - **Step Three — Multiply or divide**: product/transactional business → `Gross Profit × avg transactions = LTGP`. Recurring business → `Gross Profit ÷ Churn = LTGP`.
- **Churn**: *"the percentage of customers that leave between time periods."* `Churn = people who left ÷ original amount`. Critical rule Hormozi flags: **signing up new customers in the period does not affect churn** — only the original cohort's departures count.

## Key Concepts
- **Gross Profit**: revenue minus the cost to deliver that specific thing (not all overhead).
- **Gross Profit %**: gross profit ÷ revenue (e.g. $24,000 ÷ $30,000 = 80%).
- **Lifetime / lifespan**: the full span a customer keeps buying; lifetime transactions *always increase as a business ages* because old customers keep coming back.
- **Money math**: Hormozi's term — *"I loved money math because the more I did, the more money I made."* The skill that makes you rich is being able to count your money.
- **The arms race**: *"he who can make his customer more valuable than his competition, wins."* Cost to acquire can only hit zero, but revenue per customer "can go infinitely high."

## Mental Models
- **Think of LTV as fuel, advertising as the machine** — a better machine with no fuel goes nowhere; more fuel lets you outspend rivals on every auction-based attention channel.
- **Buy/fix businesses "back to front"** — Hormozi prefers businesses that *get* customers but fail to *monetize* them, because raising LTV lets you pour more into an already-working ad machine.
- **Establish a baseline before optimizing** — you can't increase LTGP until you've calculated it; "you may be surprised that some products you spend a lot of time on don't make you as much profit as you thought."

## Worked Example: The two LTGP calculations
**Physical product (transactional → multiply):**
- Sell a widget for $100; costs $20 to manufacture + ship → Gross Profit = $100 − $20 = **$80**.
- Average transactions per customer = 4.
- `LTGP = $80 × 4 = $360`.

**Service (recurring → divide by churn):**
- 10 clients per rep; each client pays $3,000/mo → $30,000/mo revenue per rep. Rep costs $6,000/mo.
- Gross Profit = $30,000 − $6,000 = $24,000 → **80% gross profit**. Per-customer GP = $3,000 × 80% = **$2,400**.
- Churn = 5% (lost 5 of an original 100).
- `LTGP = $2,400 ÷ 5% = $48,000`.

**The opening deal story** (May 2020): a company exploded but had only a front-end product — customers were "one and done," no recurring revenue, one marketing channel ("sitting ducks"). Founders wanted to sell something they were passionate about but unrelated to the core; Hormozi opposed it — *"If customers like your stuff, they want to buy more stuff like it!"* They settled the disagreement by **surveying the customers** ("the business serves the customer, not the other way around"). The customers overwhelmingly wanted the closer, complementary offer. Implemented as an upsell, it **2.2x'd LTV per customer** and let them **5x advertising profitably** — revenue went from a few million per month to a few million per week.

## Anti-patterns
- **Confusing gross profit with net profit**: net is after *all* expenses; LTV uses *gross*. Getting this wrong corrupts every downstream number.
- **Miscounting churn by netting in new signups**: "People get this twisted." New customers joining don't reduce the churn of the original cohort.
- **Selling something "too far" from the core**: the passion-project upsell that "felt 'too far' from the core product" — a marketing, branding, and sales nightmare that doesn't solve the customer's *next* problem.
- **Never testing / never calculating**: an under-monetized business "for life."

## Key Takeaways
1. LTV = LTGP = CLV — gross profit over a customer's lifespan; memorize the one definition.
2. Two formulas only: transactional → `GP × transactions`; recurring → `GP ÷ churn`.
3. Churn is original-cohort departures ÷ original count; new signups are irrelevant to it.
4. Raise LTV so you can outspend competitors for attention — "those who can spend the most, get the most."
5. Solve the customer's *next* problem with something *close* to what they bought; don't chase passion projects far from the core.

# Chapter 4: The Downsells & Putting It All Together (#7 Downsell Quantity, #8 Downsell Quality)

## Core Idea
The last two of the Crazy Eight catch the customers who would otherwise buy **nothing**: offer a smaller amount or a lower-quality version *only to people who don't qualify for your main offer*. **"If it means this or nothing, then this beats nothing!"** Done right, you raise revenue **per visitor** even while making those extra sales at a lower price.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **#7 Downsell Fewer (Lower Quantity)** — *"You sell a smaller amount of the same thing."* The quantity upsell in reverse: **fewer of the same thing**, a **smaller version**, or **less frequent 'doses'.**
  - Why it raises LTV: pricing factors in **conversion rate**, so capturing buyers who'd otherwise convert at $0 lifts revenue *per person who walks in the door*.
  - **The golden rule of downsells**: *"I forbid my sales team from selling a qualified person a downsell."* Only downsell people who **don't qualify** for your other offers — this avoids **cannibalizing** the main offer while scooping the extra cash. (Examples: 12 months → 3 months upfront; two burgers → one; monthly → every other month rather than nothing; 1 hour → 30 min = 0.5x.)
- **#8 Downsell Lower Quality** — *"You downsell something that gets the same result for the customer as your main offer but with a lower quality experience."* It might take longer, carry higher risk, or add hassle. Mechanically: **"take the list for a quality upsell and reverse it"** — offer a less valuable version of what you sell. (Slower response; less meeting availability; fewer locations/days/hours; less reschedule flexibility; more junior employees; higher customer-to-employee ratio; less convenient communication; more recorded/less live; more remote/less in-person; DFY→DWY→DIY; less personalization; **no/worse guarantee**. Physical: lower-quality ingredients/materials; worse or zero warranty; longer wait times.)
- **Putting It All Together** — *"If you make more than anyone else does from the same customer then you can spend more than anyone else to get them."* The Crazy Eight are "tried and true"; use them *whenever you need to increase LTV — which is all the time.* Hormozi's close: write down the action steps for each; "my highest converting upsells… usually come from getting into the zone and following a process I know works."

## Key Concepts
- **Cannibalization**: when a buyer who'd have paid full price takes the cheaper downsell instead — the loss to avoid by gating downsells to unqualified buyers only.
- **Revenue per visitor**: the metric a downsell improves — total cash collected across everyone who "walks in the door," not the price of a single sale.
- **Qualified vs unqualified buyer**: only the unqualified get downsells; the qualified must buy the main offer.
- **Operational drag**: downsells "incur little operational drag since you already do or make the stuff."

## Mental Models
- **"This beats nothing"** — a smaller/worse sale to a non-buyer is pure upside *if* it doesn't poach a real buyer.
- **Reverse the quality list** — every premium dimension in #6 becomes a downgrade dimension in #8.
- **The LTV arms race** — *"LTV is the arms race of getting customers. Those who can spend the most, get the most. Those who can't, get left behind."*

## Worked Example: Why a lower price can make more money per visitor
Imagine **four people** hit your sales team / checkout. Compare per-visitor yield before vs after adding a downsell:
- **Without a downsell**, only those who buy the main offer pay anything; the rest yield $0.
- **With a downsell**, buyers who *can't* afford / don't *qualify* for the main offer take the smaller or lower-quality version instead of leaving with nothing.
- Net effect in Hormozi's framing: **"we make 50% more per person who walks in the door… even though we're making the additional sales at a lower price."**
- **The catch he names explicitly**: *"you lose money on downsells when people who would've bought the $5 thing now opt for the $2.50 thing."* Hence the rule — downsell **only** the unqualified, never a qualified buyer.

**Quantity downsell (home service, 1x/mo):** 12 months → **3 months upfront**; one visit **every other month** rather than nothing; 1 hour → **30 min (0.5x)**.

## Anti-patterns
- **Downselling a qualified buyer**: the cardinal sin — it cannibalizes the main offer and *loses* money.
- **Treating a downsell as a discount**: it's a *smaller or lower-quality deliverable*, offered to non-qualifiers — not a price cut on the full thing.
- **Building bespoke downsells**: keep them to "the stuff you already do or make" so they carry little operational drag.
- **Waiting for inspiration / skipping the action steps**: the conclusion's whole point — work the framework as a process.

## Key Takeaways
1. Downsells turn a "no" into a smaller "yes" — *this beats nothing* — and raise revenue **per visitor**.
2. **Only downsell unqualified buyers**; never a qualified one (avoids cannibalization).
3. #7 = fewer / smaller / less-frequent of the same; #8 = same result, lower-quality experience.
4. Build #8 by **reversing the #6 premium list**; keep downsells to what you already produce (low drag).
5. Whoever makes the most per customer can spend the most to acquire — run the Crazy Eight "all the time."

# Chapter 2: Execution & Results — Quality > Quantity

## Core Idea
When you narrow to your highest-value avatar and re-engineer the buying journey around them, you get **both more leads and more valuable ones** — because your messaging becomes tailored and you weed out the unqualified up front.

## Worked Example: The Gym Launch Avatar
After surveying and sorting for the top 20%, Hormozi found his best customers shared:
- **Demographics**: right-leaning/conservative, married, 25–45, male, gym owner, US-based.
- **Business Requirements**: signed lease, 1+ employee minimum, **$10,000+/mo revenue** minimum at start, **30+ existing clients**.
- **Aspirations**: $1M+ gym, not work so much, open more locations.
- **Buying Reasons**: not enough leads, "bad market," bad pricing, can't find good employees.

**The buying-process finding**: **78% of top customers had consumed at least TWO pieces of long-form content** before purchasing. So the team **injected two long-form, high-value content pieces into every lead's journey** (genuine value-in-advance, not disguised sales pitches), increased total content output, and armed the sales team with an "all-time greatest hits" list to hand-select 2–3 pieces per prospect — forcing prospects through the same path that produced the best customers.

## Key Concepts
- **The 70x comparison** — a competitor served the *same vertical* and made the *same number of sales*, yet made **70x less profit**. The difference: they "accepted anyone with a pulse and a credit card" (high churn, high CAC, low retention, generic advice) while Hormozi selectively pursued and catered to the highest-value customers (higher retention, gross margins, premium pricing, repeat business). *"Same market. Different customer segmentation. Monstrously different results."*
- **Quality > Quantity** — every time they removed qualification steps, lead **volume rose but money fell**. Fix: **merge marketing and sales into one acquisition department** so neither blames the other; both focus on closing lots of *valuable* deals.
- **The value trade** — *"I would rather pay $5,000 to acquire $45,000 than pay $1,000 to acquire $5,000"* (even at 5x the cost). Avg. competitor LTV ≈ **$6,000–$8,000**; Acquisition.com's ≈ **$45,000+** — and the margin difference is far larger than the 6–8x LTV gap.

## Mental Models
- *Knowing your ideal buyer journey forces patience* — see the business holistically, not as a widget to sell to as many people as possible (that's how "small newbie entrepreneurs think").
- *Short-term revenue dip, long-term profitability* — narrowing may cost revenue during the change, but unless you're quitting, the long-term call wins.

## How to Use This Chapter (3 actions)
1. **Survey** your existing customers. 2. Use the data to decide which characteristics are **leading indicators of high-value customers**. 3. **Change your advertising messaging** and **re-engineer a sales process** for them specifically.

## Key Takeaways
1. Find what your top 20% share, then advertise only to them and rebuild the sale around their proven path.
2. Same market + better customer segmentation can mean *many multiples* more profit (the 70x gap).
3. Growing a business = selling more customers OR making them worth more — picking the right avatar does both.

# Chapter 3: How to Start, Grow, then Pivot Your Own Brand

## Core Idea
Branding is "the deliberate pairing of things (your thing plus what your ideal customer has)," and good branding is the deliberate pairing of your thing with something *good*. To start, grow, or pivot a bad brand into a good one, run four steps — then keep adjusting. "It takes 20 years to build a reputation and 5 minutes to ruin it" (Buffett). "My brand didn't magically appear — it was deliberate."

## Frameworks Introduced

- **The 4-Step Branding Process**:
  1. **Figure out who your ideal customer is.**
  2. **Figure out what they like.**
  3. **Associate your stuff with those things.**
  4. **Optimize those associations for max profit.**
- **The Ideal-Avatar Criteria (4 boxes)** — pick a market that is: **Growing** (a rising tide grows you even if you stay the same size) · **Has enough money** (so you can charge a lot) · **Easy to target** ("If my customers exist, but I can't find them… does it matter?") · **In Pain** (they desperately want what you sell; competition proves demand and "most business owners aren't tough to beat"). Narrow the avatar to "provide more value per person."
- **Associate via 3 actions** (Step 3): **make content** geared to the audience → **distribute it** → **iterate** based on their responses. Give away videos, blogs, articles, booklets, PDFs that solve their problems so "who do they think of when that happens? Our business."
- **Levels of Authority: The Branding Cycle** — three camps of authority, in increasing power:
  1. **What We Say** — me advertising myself (Content, Ads, Outreach). The *least* influential.
  2. **What Other People Say** — affiliates and other customers; "matters way more than what you tell them."
  3. **What They Experience Themselves** — a person's own experience "has, by far, the most authority." It governs what they tell others and your advertising over the long haul.
  - **Good branding** (virtuous cycle): advertise → first sale → deliver on promises → strong positive association → they buy again and tell others.
  - **Bad branding** (vicious cycle): advertise → first sale → *fail* to deliver → strong negative association → never buy again + discourage others; "each customer chipping away at your reputation."
- **Brand Goes Both Ways** — brand influences whether they buy; the product then influences how they see the brand next time. "Your advertising has a stronger influence on your brand in the short term. Your product has a stronger influence on your brand in the long term." Referrals are two-sided: a bad product makes them resent the referrer; "if a porn star recommends an accounting firm, I may assume it's an accounting firm for porn stars."
- **The 5 Directions to Optimize (Step 4)** — to grow the addressable market, move: **Up Market** (larger customers) · **Down Market** (smaller) · **Adjacent** (similar-but-different) · **Broader** (the whole category) · **Narrower** (a slice of your existing market). Each move requires associating with what the *new* avatar likes — so your brand must change too.
- **The Bouquet Metaphor** — "On their own, flowers are not a bouquet, just as products, values, experiences, people… on their own are not a brand." What you include, from where, and how much = the brand you create. **Narrow** = a bouquet of only red roses (talk about one specific thing). **Broaden** = bring in all sorts of flowers. **Adjacent** = red roses → yellow roses (still roses, different type). Never add **sticks and weeds** (bad pairings) that "distort the clarity of our bouquet."
- **Brand Pivots = Net Changes in Audience** — "When you make brand changes, you really make brand bets." Only two outcomes: gain more than you lose (good trade) or lose more than you gain (bad trade). "One-for-one swaps only happen on paper." Goal: net positive on every move.

## Worked Example: The Assisted-Living Law Firm
A business-law firm can't profitably serve everyone, so it narrows its avatar by the four criteria → **Assisted Living center owners** (Growing ✓, Money ✓, Easy to find via ads targeting older demographics ✓, Pain — "Do aging populations have legal issues? Do they ever!" ✓).
- **Step 2 — what they like**: more money, more customers, more profit, a sellable business, help recruiting/retaining nurses, fewer lawsuits, fewer taxes.
- **Step 3 — associate**: make content *from the owner's perspective* — e.g. "here are things that lowered costs and improved quality of life for residents," how to depreciate investments to save money, floor plans that "house more residents per square foot." Owners react "this stuff really makes my life better" and think of the firm.
- **Step 4 — directions**: Up = multi-location owners; Down = at-home/mobile care; Adjacent = rehab facilities; Broader = all senior living (hospice, retirement); Narrower = assisted living with 100–500 residents.

## Worked Example: Hormozi's Own Pivot (closet → productions)
He started making only business content; as he grew he touched other topics and "views followed. But… it was the wrong views." He **recentered** on only business and "more business owners paid attention and had positive associations again." Going "from making rant videos in my closet to full on productions about business," he lost the few who only wanted closet videos but gained far more business owners who preferred the better quality — a good trade. "Don't let the five mean comments stop you from gaining the 500 new people who like the new thing."

## Mental Models
- **Authority climbs**: engineer the move from *what you say* → *what others say* → *what they experience*; the last one is what actually compounds.
- **Prune the bouquet**: "remove things your ideal customers don't like and add the things they do" — branding is gardening, not a one-time build.
- **Recover from mistakes by overwhelming, not erasing**: "We don't try and eliminate the DUI… we try to overwhelm it with way more of the stuff that the majority of our people actually like" (Kanye keeps selling after canceled events because "over successive pairings the good stuff minimizes the bad stuff").
- **Default growth = do more of what works, do it better, show it to more people** (low-risk); *change* is a higher-risk/higher-reward bet.

## Anti-patterns
- **Letting your brand happen by accident** — "They take every podcast. They sell any customer. They talk about whatever's trending. And… no one has any idea what they're about."
- **Chasing wider reach with the wrong audience** — broader content pulled Hormozi toward "wantrepreneurs," not buyers; reach without the right *direction* is the wrong reach.
- **Adding one bad pairing to a strong brand** — "One bad pairing can absolutely hurt a brand." The rotten flower makes "this bouquet sucks now."
- **Quitting before the payoff** — "Branding has higher returns than direct response advertising if you measure over a longer period of time." Final step is **Patience** — "Wait five years and don't give up."
- **Charging premium without delivering** — "Your premium brand had better be delivering something special, or it's not going to get the business" (and won't *keep* it).

## Key Takeaways
1. Run the 4 steps in order: **who → what they like → associate → optimize**; then never stop adjusting.
2. Pick an avatar that is **growing, monied, easy to find, and in pain**.
3. Build authority up the cycle: **What We Say → What Other People Say → What They Experience** (the last wins long-term).
4. Grow/pivot in one of **5 directions**, treating each as a *bet* you must win net (gain > loss); keep the bouquet clean.
5. Fix mistakes by **overwhelming** them with liked pairings; then be patient — brand compounds over years.

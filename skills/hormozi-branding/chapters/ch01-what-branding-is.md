# Chapter 1: What Branding Is (and Is NOT)

## Core Idea
**"Branding means pairing two or more things together."** It happens all the time — consumers constantly associate you with what you say, do, appear next to, and look like. The job is to do it on purpose and do it well. **"Good branding means pairing your business with something a majority of your ideal audience likes."** This isn't about colors, logos, feelings, or presence. "This is about making money."

## Frameworks Introduced

- **Good / Bad / No Branding** — the three outcomes of any pairing.
  - **Good branding**: pair your business with something a *majority of your ideal audience likes* → they like and buy.
  - **Bad branding**: pair with something a majority of your audience *dislikes* → they hate it and don't buy.
  - **No branding**: exposure produces normal behavior — "You're practically invisible."
- **Branding vs. Advertising** — "Advertising is letting people know about your stuff. Branding is what you let them know in the advertisement." Advertising lets people know the cow belongs to *someone*; branding lets them know it belongs to *you* (and the relative value of you). "You don't want them to buy products like yours, you want them to buy your products." You can have great advertising + poor branding, or vice versa, in any combination.
- **The Three Brand Measurements** — objective measures so you know how you're doing:
  - **Reach** — the output of advertising: how many people know about your stuff. "A brand can't exist without it, so reach is the primary element of a brand."
  - **Influence** — "the chance it changes behavior when they see it."
  - **Direction** — how behavior changes: **towards** or **away**. Depends on the target audience (the same thing pulls one audience toward and another away).
- **The Eight Brand Positions** — reach (high/low) × influence (high/low) × direction (towards/away) = 8 positions. One brand can occupy any position depending on who you ask.
- **Learning = same condition, new behavior** — the litmus test for all content (including this playbook): if it changes your behavior, you learned; if not, it was a waste of time.

## Worked Example: The Branded Cow
The first entrepreneurs to use branding to make money were cattle herders — they seared a logo into the side of a cow.
- **No brand**: you see a cow with nothing on it. "Hello, cow." "Moo." Just a cow. You leave it alone.
- **Good branding**: the cow has a brand you recognize — your neighbor Bill's. *If you like Bill*, you return the cow to him.
- **Bad branding**: *if you hate Bill*, the cow "may stay lost in the wilderness forever and become lunch for your family… in the form of delicious burgers."
- **Advanced note** — a branded cow whose brand you *don't recognize*: you treat it like any branded animal (it belongs to *someone*). So there's still a difference between a no-logo cow and an unrecognized brand.

Either way — for better or worse — "the brand affected what you did. It affected your behavior."

## Worked Example: Kendall Jenner x Pepsi
Pepsi ran an ad of Kendall Jenner handing a police officer a Pepsi during a protest. Immediate backlash; they pulled it within six days. **Good advertising, bad branding**: lots of people found out (good advertising), but a lot of people hated the pairing (bad branding) → fewer people bought → a net loss. The pairing had "very little to do with Jenner, and everything to do with protests" — they paired with something the majority don't find "refreshing and joyful." Jenner could likely have done a *different* Pepsi ad that succeeded. Is there a product where Jenner + protest nets money? Yes — "It just isn't Pepsi."

## Mental Models
- Use **reach / influence / direction** to diagnose any brand — yours or a competitor's — instead of arguing about whether it's "good."
- Think of **direction as audience-dependent**: politicians have *strong* brands (high influence) but opposite directions for different groups. "High influence. Both strong, just different directions."
- Measure your own brand **with money**: "Good branding means your audience likes and buys. Bad branding means the people in your ideal audience hate it and… don't."

## Anti-patterns
- **Treating brand as logos, colors, feelings, presence, or intuition** — none of that tells you what to *do*. "If you don't know what to do, nothing's going to change."
- **Believing "there's no such thing as bad publicity"** — there is. "Good beats bad. Bad beats none. Lots of good beats everything." Any publicity beats none, but good publicity beats bad.
- **Thinking you have to be polarizing** — you don't. Taylor Swift, Mother Teresa, and Apple are strong, widely-positive brands. "Selling polarizes people" on its own; *deliberately* trying to polarize interferes with business. "Just focus on getting people to buy your stuff."
- **Optimizing for "everyone"** — Pepsi's audience was everyone, which made the bad pairing maximally costly.

## Key Takeaways
1. Branding = pairing two or more things; *good* branding = pairing with what a majority of your **ideal** audience likes.
2. Advertising creates reach (do people know about you); branding is the pairing — measured by **influence** and **direction**.
3. For a business, score every pairing by one question: did it make more money or not?
4. You don't need controversy — "Make promises that serve a large audience, and keep them."

# Stage 8: SPECIALIZE

## Core Idea
With 100–249 employees you're a **"President"** with four layers of management under you. **No one person can be good at everything anymore** — you need specialists. The job: stop "everyone does everything" and **create dedicated teams of specialists**, each focused on what they're best at. Goal: **create dedicated people and teams for specific tasks.**

## Stage Card (verbatim)
- **Your Role**: President
- **Headcount**: 100 to 249
- **Bottom Line (the constraint)**: No one is good enough to know everything
- **Graduate by**: Create dedicated people and teams for specific tasks

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Hyper-specialization** (the core of "Specialize") — break big jobs into smaller specialized pieces, make sure everyone knows their specific role, find people who are really good at those pieces, and create systems to help specialists work together. (Some salespeople sell only certain products; some service reps handle only certain customers; some tech/finance people handle only certain tasks.)
- **Build your brand (not just sell)** — advertising is getting expensive and less effective ("shouting in a crowded room"). Focus on **building your brand** (like Nike or Apple): create a clear, simple message about who you are; **give away valuable free stuff to build trust**; get **associated with other trusted brands or people**.
- **Best leads to best closers** — your best salespeople aren't closing as much; **send your best leads to your best closers** and use automatic tracking to measure everything.
- **Trim & turn features into products** — your product is a "Swiss Army knife" with too many features confusing customers (a TV remote with 100 buttons). Survey customers to find which features they actually use, remove the rest, and **turn some removed features into separate products** you can sell.

## Key Concepts
- **Standardize hiring + founder final check** — recruiters are inconsistent (hurting culture); create one standard selection process and have the **founder do a final check on important hires**.
- **One clear path for multi-product customers** — different customers get conflicting info; create one clear path and make smart recommendations about what else to buy.
- **Specialized software + IT request system** — departments need different software and overwhelm IT; get specialized software per department, a system for IT requests, and clear rules on what IT will/won't do.
- **Money: stop paying retail** — renegotiate prices with vendors, put extra cash in interest-earning accounts, get specialists for tax/finance tasks, and run regular internal audits.

## Mental Models
- **Hospital, not general practice** — you don't want a general doctor doing heart surgery; you want a heart specialist who focuses only on hearts.
- **High school → university** — teachers teach multiple subjects (generalists) vs. professors who specialize in one thing. Specialization is what creates **outsized returns to capitalize on** with the next big bet (Stage 9).

## Anti-patterns
- **"Jack of all trades"** — being okay at everything stops working at this size.
- **Feature bloat** — too many features confuse customers; trim and re-package.
- **Inconsistent recruiting** — unstandardized hiring damages culture.

## Key Takeaways
1. The constraint is that **no one is good enough to know everything** — build **specialist teams**.
2. As paid ads decay, shift to **brand-building**: clear message, free value, association with trusted brands.
3. Route **best leads to best closers**; standardize hiring with a **founder final check**.
4. **Trim product features** (turn some into separate products) and stop paying retail on vendors/cash.

## Connects To
- **Stage 7 (Categorize)**: categorized work is what you now assign to specialists.
- **Stage 9 (Capitalize)**: hyper-specialization creates the outsized returns to capitalize on with the next big bet.
- **Stage 4 (Prioritize)**: brand/clarity and "say no" themes scale up here.

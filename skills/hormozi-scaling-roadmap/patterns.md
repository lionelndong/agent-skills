# Patterns — $100M Scaling Roadmap

The roadmap's reusable plays. Each is a constraint-driven move tied to a stage. Apply when your business hits the matching symptom — not by calendar.

## The Stage Diagnosis Pattern (the master pattern)
**When to use**: any time, to locate yourself on the roadmap.
**How**: read each **stage card** — Role, Headcount, Bottom Line (constraint), Graduate By — and find the one whose **Bottom Line** matches your current pain. That stage's "Graduate By" is your single objective.
**Trade-offs**: headcount bands are signals, not rules; trust the *constraint* over the employee count if they disagree.

## Free-First Validation (Stage 0)
**When**: pre-revenue, unsure anyone wants it.
**How**: give the thing away free to people you know; ask what they think and what would make it better; protect yourself with an entity + business bank account.
**Trade-offs**: no money yet — that's intentional; the output is feedback and proof, not cash.

## The V1 Product (Stage 1)
**When**: people like the free version but it's "not good enough to pay for."
**How**: build a "Version 1" — good enough that people will pay, not perfect; sell using free-customer results as proof; collect real payments and track them.
**Trade-offs**: shipping a V1 risks quality complaints; perfectionism risks never making the first sale (worse).

## Fix Things in an Unscalable Way (Stage 2)
**When**: demand is inconsistent and quality wobbles.
**How**: do whatever it takes to delight each customer (extra service, immediate fixes, after-hours effort). Be **valuable, not scalable yet**.
**Trade-offs**: deliberately inefficient; you trade margin/time for reputation and learning.

## The Rule of 100 + Double Down (Stage 2)
**When**: feast-or-famine customer flow.
**How**: spend **100 minutes daily on marketing**, every day; test channels, then **stick with the one that works** and pour volume in — don't diversify early.
**Trade-offs**: concentration risk on one channel (addressed later at Stage 7), but focus wins at this size.

## Named Sales/Service Scripts (Stage 2)
**When**: sales and service are ad-hoc.
**How**: convert leads with the **"CLOSER" method**; handle upset customers with the **"Angry Boat" method**; capture testimonials with the **"Epiphany Script"**.
**Trade-offs**: scripts need practice until natural; rote delivery undercuts them.

## Fix the ONE Biggest Problem First (Stage 3)
**When**: too many complaints, too little time (overwhelmed solo owner).
**How**: rank complaints, fix the **#1** really well, then move to the next. Write down how tasks are done (SOPs) and train the team.
**Trade-offs**: other problems wait; that's the price of actually resolving the worst one.

## Niche Down + Say "No" (Stage 4)
**When**: "trying to be everything to everyone"; product pulled in many directions.
**How**: pick a specific best-customer type, reshape the product for them, **raise prices**, and say **no** to poor-fit customers/projects. Start measuring lead→sale, satisfaction, response time.
**Trade-offs**: you turn down money short-term to build something bigger long-term.

## Second Premium Product (Stage 5)
**When**: low **LTV:CAC** — customers buy once and have nothing else to buy (and churn).
**How**: listen to what best customers want next; build a **second, higher-priced** product; **start small and test with your best customers**; keep the first product running smoothly.
**Trade-offs**: balancing act — splitting focus risks the cash cow; done right, the new LTV funds ads + better hires.

## "Better" Beats "More" (Stage 6)
**When**: more people but everything's inefficient.
**How**: train current people better, improve current products, serve current customers better, use current tools better — **before** adding new ones. Run the loop: find → why → better way → test → train → monitor; make changes stick.
**Trade-offs**: optimization needs patience and upfront investment with delayed payoff; people resist change.

## Sort Into Categories + Add a Second Channel (Stage 7)
**When**: every system overwhelmed; "organized chaos"; single acquisition channel.
**How**: triage by sorting **employees, applicants, data, customers, money, leads** into categories with systems for each (cloud data, **ATS**, **HRIS**, quarterly departmental budgets). Add a **second acquisition channel** (e.g. referral program); add **lead scoring** and prevention-focused service.
**Trade-offs**: organizing takes time away from growth, but removes single points of failure.

## Hyper-Specialization + Brand-Building (Stage 8)
**When**: "no one is good enough to know everything"; ads getting expensive/ineffective.
**How**: break big jobs into specialized pieces and staff dedicated specialists; standardize hiring with a **founder final check**; route **best leads to best closers**; trim "Swiss Army knife" features (turn some into separate products); shift to **brand-building** (clear message, free value, association with trusted brands); stop paying retail (renegotiate vendors, earn interest on idle cash).
**Trade-offs**: specialization adds coordination overhead (needs systems to connect specialists); brand payoff is slower than direct-response.

## Make a Big Bet (Stage 9)
**When**: plateaued; unsure where next growth comes from; cash cows can't be safely changed.
**How**: make a **big bet** — **M&A** (buy a company) or **R&D** (build something new) — big enough to move the needle (1-year+ payoff). Protect the cash cows; do **strategic workforce planning** and **founder-led recruiting**; get **audited books**, talk to **investment bankers**, possibly raise capital.
**Trade-offs**: big bets are risky and slow; small bets won't move a company this size.

## Cross-Stage Recurring Patterns
- **One thing at a time** — Stage 3 ("fix the ONE biggest problem"), Stage 6 ("one improvement at a time"), Stage 7 (specific teams on specific features).
- **The 6-area scan** — Product, Marketing, Sales, Customer Service, Technology/Tech, People/HR & Money — re-examined at every stage at higher complexity.
- **Protect the business / formalize money** — entity & bookkeeping (Stage 0) → payroll (Stage 3) → P&L + insurance (Stage 4) → budget/forecast (Stage 5) → balance sheet + quarterly taxes (Stage 6) → departmental budgets (Stage 7) → audited books + bankers (Stage 9).

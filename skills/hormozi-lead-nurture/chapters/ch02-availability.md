# Chapter 2: Pillar I — Availability

## Core Idea
"If leads cannot schedule, they do not show." Availability — **how many appointment slots you have and how far out leads can schedule them** — was the *single biggest* lever on show rate in Vlad's data, "an almost perfect correlation with throughput." On an absolute basis, businesses with the most time slots had the most schedules, shows, and purchases.

## Why it works / failure mode
If you don't have availability when leads do, they either (a) **don't schedule at all** (nobody shows, nobody buys) or (b) **schedule at a bad time** — which is worse: high chance of skipping/"ghosting," lower chance of buying if they do show (they're managing scheduling annoyances), and they steal a slot from someone who would have shown and bought. The fix easily produces **20–40%, sometimes 200%+** increases in shown appointments.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The Availability Tactics (4)** — expand the surface area for booking:
  1. **Take Appointments More Days Per Week** — "You pay rent/payroll/insurance seven days a week. You might as well make money seven days a week." Most people work 5 days; booking 7 makes you **40% more available** to accept money.
  2. **Take Appointments More Hours Per Day** — "Be available to sell when your customers are available to buy." For nationwide selling, **6am–6pm PST** covers the highest % of US sales relative to hours (go beyond if you can).
  3. **Give Leads More Flexible Appointment Times** — offer **4 scheduling options per hour** (12:00, 12:15, 12:30, 12:45) instead of one or two. Keep appointment *length* the same; just give freedom over the start time.
  4. **Have Inbound, Outbound, and Self-Scheduling Options** — you call leads; leads call you; leads book themselves online. **Do all three.** "The more ways you give people to book, the more people will book."
  - When to use: any appointment-driven business; do this *first* (biggest lever).
  - How: widen days → hours → slots-per-hour → booking channels.

- **Header–Headline–Subheadline** — the top of a self-scheduling page. **Header = "Attention [Avatar]"** (confirms they're in the right place); headline + subheadline tell them the next thing to do and why.

- **Friction control (two-way)** — *Remove* friction to get more bookings; *Add* friction when you have too many bad appointments.

## Key Concepts
- **Availability** — slots × how far out leads can book.
- **Diminishing returns** — gains taper at the upper quartiles of availability, but large increases are still there at the top.
- **Excess capacity** — the deliberate slack (extra reps / open slots) that makes high availability and speed possible.
- **Self-scheduler** — an online booking page leads use without a human.
- **15-minute qualifying call** — splitting a short qualify call from the close call lets one scheduler serve many slot times.

## Online Scheduler Tips
1. **Header confirms they're in the right place**, then tell them what to do next and why (Header → Headline → Subheadline).
2. Make **available dates/times obvious the moment the page loads** — mobile and desktop.
3. **Eliminate as many steps as possible**; don't re-ask info leads already gave (tech that pre-fills known fields creates huge schedule-rate gains). "Do not underestimate this."

## Worked Example: Add Friction To Kill Bad Appointments
When the calendar fills with *low-quality* bookings, deliberately **add friction** so only qualified people book:
- Add a **video** to watch before the scheduler appears.
- Add a **sales letter** above the scheduler they must read first.
- Add the **price** to the page so people know what they're in for.
- **Delay when the scheduler appears** until they're sufficiently qualified.
These reduce appointment *quantity* and raise *quality*.

## Mental Models
- "I can't be open more hours" really means **"it's not worth it for me right now"** — until you count the money you burn making it hard to buy. Ask "What would it actually cost?" and "How much do I burn by *not* doing this?"
- **Rip off the Band-Aid**: "First, it sucks. Then, it works. Then I kick myself for not doing it sooner."
- It's **not about cost, it's about ROI** — more schedules, more shows, more sales. Good trade.

## Anti-patterns
- **Booking only on the business's convenience** (every 30/60 min, weekdays 9–5) — that's when customers are "AT WORK MAKING MONEY," not buying.
- **One booking channel only** — leaves bookings on the table.
- **Adding friction when the problem is too *few* appointments** (and vice-versa) — match the friction lever to the actual problem.

## Key Takeaways
1. Availability is the **#1 lever** on show rate — fix it first.
2. Widen all three dimensions: **more days, more hours, more slots per hour** (aim 4/hour).
3. Offer **inbound + outbound + self-scheduling**; strip steps from the self-scheduler.
4. Friction is a dial: **remove** it for volume, **add** it for quality.

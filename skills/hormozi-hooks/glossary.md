# Glossary — $100M Playbook: Hooks

**70-20-10 Innovation Rule** — Google's resource-allocation strategy (Page & Brin), applied to hooks: 70% proven winners, 20% winner-adjacent models, 10% wild experiments. (Ch 3)

**Avatar** — The specific target person whose identity a Label hook speaks to. (Ch 2)

**Bad hook** — A hook people never notice; if nobody notices the ad, nobody buys. (Ch 1)

**Call Out** — The first part of a hook; grabs attention so the prospect thinks "This is for me," in order to deliver the promise. (Ch 1)

**Cocktail Party Effect** — The scientific name for why a call out works: even amid lots of noise, a single thing (like your name) catches and holds attention. (Ch 1)

**Commands** — A verbal hook type: a direct command or suggestion ("Read this if you're tired of being broke"). (Ch 2)

**Condition for Value** — The second part of a hook (the hook itself); promises that consuming/doing the thing yields value. (Ch 1)

**Conditionals** — A verbal hook type: a scenario/condition leading to a result, learning, or command. (Ch 2)

**Core Business (70%)** — The 70-20-10 bucket for "more of what works"; proven hooks that stabilize performance. (Ch 3)

**Emerging Business (20%)** — The 70-20-10 bucket for promising, less-proven "Winner Adjacent" hooks. (Ch 3)

**Exclamations / Provocative Statements** — A verbal hook type expressing strong emotion ("Ahhhhh… This is the blueprint…"). (Ch 2)

**Explicit promise** — A stated value condition: "If [target] [condition], they will [reward/value]." (Ch 1)

**Good hook** — A hook that makes people notice and pay attention; "a good hook makes a sale" (sells attention). (Ch 1)

**Hook** — Whatever gets the attention of your audience; what people see and/or hear first. (Ch 1)

**Hook Checklist** — The run-procedure: first time (harvest 50, use all), every time after (70% top-5 / 20% permutations / 10% new). (Ch 4)

**Hook Spreadsheet** — A swipe library: one tab per platform; columns name, hook, views, link; reviewed before every batch. (Ch 3)

**Implicit promise** — Value shown rather than stated (a mom shows a well-behaved kid, then "Look how I did it"). (Ch 1)

**Labels** — A verbal hook type: words the avatar identifies with ("Local business owners, I have a gift for you"). (Ch 2)

**"Let the data do the teaching"** — Improve without a teacher by making more like your top 10% and less like your bottom 10%, repeated. (Ch 3)

**Lists or Steps** — A verbal hook type: numbered lists ("the 28 ways to stay poor"). (Ch 2)

**"More of what works"** — The 70% core bucket: reuse hooks already proven in your own or others' content. (Ch 3)

**Narratives** — A verbal hook type: stories and anecdotes ("One day I was in the back and this old lady comes in…"). (Ch 2)

**Nonverbal hook** — A sound or visual that grabs attention (catching a banana, a dropped tray, a champagne-flute *cling*). (Ch 2)

**Open question** — A Question hook that asks a choice ("Which would you rather be?"). (Ch 2)

**Outlier** — A high-performing piece of content (found via tools or platform search) worth modeling. (Ch 3)

**Questions** — A verbal hook type, split into Yes questions and Open questions. (Ch 2)

**Specific enough / broad enough** — Design rule: specific enough to get the right people, broad enough to get as many as possible. (Ch 1)

**Statements** — A verbal hook type: a declarative line ("The smartest thing you can do today…"). (Ch 2)

**The Two Parts of a Hook** — Call Out + Condition for Value; every hook has both. (Ch 1)

**Verbal hook** — Words that get attention; eight types (Labels, Questions, Conditionals, Commands, Statements, Lists/Steps, Narratives, Exclamations). (Ch 2)

**Whisper-Tease-Shout Method** — A named email cadence (built up across a launch sequence) referenced in a subject-line hook. (Ch 4)

**Winner Adjacent** — Thinking about the 20% bucket: model a hook concept that won in another niche. (Ch 3)

**Yes question** — A Question hook designed to elicit "yes" ("Would you pay $1,000 to have the business of your dreams in 30 days?"). (Ch 2)

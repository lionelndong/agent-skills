## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

### What a Hook is
**"A hook is whatever gets the attention of your audience."** It's whatever people see and/or hear first — the first few seconds of a video, the first thing you hear, the first line or image. "If nobody notices your ad, then nobody buys your stuff. Nothing else matters. Your entire business hinges on these first few seconds." Master hooks and you can **5x, 10x, or 100x** the people who pay attention — changing nothing else. *(David Ogilvy: "After you've written your headline, you've spent eighty cents of your advertising dollar." → "If you have ten hours to advertise, focus your first eight hours on the hooks.")*

### The Two Parts of a Hook (how hooks work)
1. **Call Out** — gets the prospect to say *"This is for me."* Happens verbally and nonverbally. Works via the **Cocktail Party Effect**: in a noisy room, one thing (your name) still pierces through — the call out harnesses that to cut through the noise.
2. **Condition for Value** — *the hook itself.* Promises value: consume/do this → get that. **Explicit** ("If [MOMS] [WATCH THIS] [THEY'LL KNOW HOW TO GET KIDS TO BEHAVE]" — target/condition/reward) or **implicit** (show the result, then "Look how I did it"). People consume "if the cost of consuming is less than the benefit."
- Design rule: **specific enough to get the right people, broad enough to get as many as possible.**

### Types of Hooks — Verbal & Nonverbal (the 8 verbal types)
"Anything that gets someone to notice your ad is a hook." Two families: **verbal** (words) and **nonverbal** (sounds/visuals); combine them when the platform allows.
**8 verbal types** (rewrite ONE message as all of them for instant variety):
1. **Labels** — words the avatar identifies with ("Local business owners, I have a gift for you").
2. **Questions** — *Yes* ("Would you pay $1,000 to have the business of your dreams in 30 days?") and *Open* ("Which would you rather be?").
3. **Conditionals** — scenario → result/learning/command ("If you're working all the time and your business isn't growing, you're working on the wrong sh*t").
4. **Commands** — "Read this if you're tired of being broke."
5. **Statements** — "The smartest thing you can do today…" / "How to get ahead of 99% of people."
6. **Lists or Steps** — "the 28 ways to stay poor."
7. **Narratives** — stories/anecdotes ("One day I was in the back and this old lady comes in piss angry").
8. **Exclamations / Provocative Statements** — "Ahhhhh… this is the blueprint to becoming a millionaire."
**Nonverbal**: a sound/visual that signals an *embarrassing disaster* (dropped tray) or *important news* (champagne-flute *cling*) — both make people need to know what's next (e.g., catching a banana on camera).

### The Process — Google's 70-20-10 Innovation Rule
Allocate hook effort every batch: **70% Core** ("more of what works" — proven hooks; stabilizes performance) · **20% Emerging** ("Winner Adjacent" — model outliers from other niches) · **10% Big New Ideas** (wild new formats; document losers, graduate winners into the 70%). Build a **Hook Spreadsheet** (one tab per platform; columns name/hook/views/link) and review it before every batch. **The hook dictates the content, not the other way around.**

### Testing & Volume rules
- **Ads**: record ~**10 hooks** per 1 ad. **Short content**: reuse each winning tweet as a short, then expand the hook. **Long content**: record **3–4 hooks**, pick best in post. **Email**: **split-test** every campaign; log winners.
- **Let the data do the teaching** (cold start): make a bunch → keep your **top 10%**, cut your **bottom 10%** → repeat forever.

### The Hook Checklist (run procedure)
- **First time through**: study others' top performers → isolate the hooks → write your favorite **50** → use them all.
- **Every time after**: take the top **5** (top 10%) → **70%** of next batch from them → **20%** permutations (visuals + words) → **10%** completely new.
- **Every good hook contains**: a **call out** + an **implication of value**.

### The #1 anti-pattern
**Don't write the hook about yourself — "they don't care."** Dean Graziosi's Larry King ad bombed because the opening flattered Dean; rewriting only the first 1–2 sentences to make the *viewer* feel important ("Have you ever looked in the mirror and thought 'I should be further ahead by now?'") turned it into a monster that sold millions. Nail the hook or nothing else matters.

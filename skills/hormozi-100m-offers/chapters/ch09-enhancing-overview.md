# Chapter 11: Enhancing the Offer — Scarcity, Urgency, Bonuses, Guarantees & Naming (Overview)

## Core Idea
Once the core offer exists, five **enhancers** make it dramatically more desirable **without changing the offer itself**, by shifting the supply-and-demand curve in your favor. "The 'perfect profit combination' is lots of demand, and very little supply, or perceived supply."

## Framework: The Five Enhancers (each is distinct — keep all five)
1. **Scarcity** — decrease supply to raise prices (and indirectly increase demand via perceived exclusiveness).
2. **Urgency** — increase demand by decreasing the prospect's action threshold (a function of *time*).
3. **Bonuses** — increase demand and perceived exclusivity by stacking value.
4. **Guarantees** — increase demand by reversing risk.
5. **Naming** — re-stimulate demand and expand awareness to the target audience.

## Key Concepts — The Delicate Dance of Desire
- **All marketing influences the supply/demand curve** — artificially raise demand (persuasion) and lower perceived supply (scarcity) so you sell the same product for more, in higher volume, over a longer horizon.
- **Desire comes from not getting what you want** (Naval Ravikant: "Desire is a contract you make with yourself to be unhappy until you get what you want"). To increase desire, *delay* satisfying it — sell fewer units than you can.
- **Demand is fractal / non-linear (80/20)** — roughly one fifth of prospects will pay ~5× the price. Skimming the top can earn more than selling the whole pyramid cheaply, while leaving pent-up demand.
- **Hormozi's Law**: *"The longer you delay the ask, the bigger the ask you can make."* / "The longer the runway, the bigger the plane that can take off."

## Worked Example: Arnold's Fundraiser ("When demand increases, cut supply")
Ben Newman raised the ticket from $15,000 → **$25,000** *and cut the number of tickets* on George's advice. Result: an extra ~$1M raised before the event even started; the night raised ~$5.4M from only 100 people ($54,000/head). Items auctioned as one-of-a-kind ("if you missed it, you'd never get another chance"), with Arnold adding bonuses when bidding climbed. "People want what they can't have. People want what other people want. People want things only a select few have access to."

## Mental Models
- *Keep supply (and satisfaction of desire) under the demand you can generate* — "the real key to never going hungry."
- *Don't pull the trigger too early* — satisfy all demand and "you kill your golden goose"; each successive promotion then sells less.

## Anti-patterns
- Always generating *new* demand to scrape another quick sale (the sad state of a business that satisfies all desire each time).
- Treating the enhancers as interchangeable — they are five separate levers.

## Key Takeaways
1. Enhancers raise desire and price without altering the core offer.
2. Manage the gap: high demand, low (perceived) supply.
3. Apply Scarcity, Urgency, Bonuses, Guarantees, and Naming — each in turn.

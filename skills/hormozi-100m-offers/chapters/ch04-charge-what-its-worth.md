# Chapter 5: Pricing — Charge What It's Worth

## Core Idea
"The reason people buy anything is to get a deal." They buy when perceived **value** exceeds **price**. Your job is to widen that gap by **raising value and raising price** — never by discounting. "There is no strategic benefit to being the second cheapest in the marketplace, but there is for being the most expensive." (Dan Kennedy)

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Price-to-Value Discrepancy**: the gap between what they pay (price) and what they get (value). "The moment the value they receive dips below what they are paying, they stop buying." Widen the gap by increasing value, not cutting price (think "buying $100,000 of value for $10,000" — money at a discount).
- **The Virtuous Cycle of Price** (raise prices →):
  - ↑ clients' emotional investment
  - ↑ clients' perceived value
  - ↑ clients' results (they're invested)
  - Attract the **best** clients (easiest to satisfy, cost less to fulfill)
  - **Multiply margin** → invest in systems, people, experience, scale.
- **The Vicious Cycle of Price** (lower prices →): ↓ emotional investment, ↓ perceived value, ↓ results, attract the **worst** clients ("never satisfied until your service is free"), destroy margin. "Your world sucks . . . your service probably sucks."

## Key Concepts
- **Those who pay the most, pay the most attention** — price it so it "stings a little"; the sting focuses investment and drives results.
- **Higher price → higher perceived value (literally)** — the blind wine taste test: identical wine rated by price. Raising price can *increase* the value the consumer experiences.
- **Category of one / monopoly profits** — be so much more expensive that the prospect thinks "there must be something entirely different going on here."
- **Profit is oxygen** — it fuels growth, more impact, more people served.

## Worked Example: The $42,000 Gym Lords Conversation
Hormozi's father questions $42,000/yr pricing. Hormozi reframes it through value:
- "If I made you **$239,000** extra this year, would you pay me $42,000?" (avg. topline increase over 11 months)
- Work required: ~15 hrs/week. Time to result: 11 months. Pay up front: **nothing** — pay as you make the money.
- The father: "Oh . . . well then, yeah, I would do it." → "And that's why they do it, too."
Pricing logic in his own market: low-price competitors charged $500/mo, one high competitor $5,000; Hormozi entered at **$16,000** (3× the highest, 32× the lowest) for a 16-week done-with-you intensive, then upsold 35% into **$42,000/yr**.

## Mental Models
- *Don't price off the market* — those competitors are broke. Pricing at the market = pricing for market efficiency = barely above water.
- *Conviction beats skepticism* — to charge premium prices you must "outwork your self-doubt" so your delivery is certain.
- *Charging premium is also a moral choice* — only margin lets you truly over-deliver and serve customers best.

## Anti-patterns
- The default pricing process: look at marketplace → average → go slightly below → add "a little more" → land at "more for less."
- Competing on price without a 10×-lower cost structure — "a one-way road to destruction."

## Key Takeaways
1. Raise price **only after** sufficiently raising value — keep it a steal.
2. Premium pricing starts the virtuous cycle; discounting starts the vicious one.
3. "99 percent of businesses need to raise their prices to grow, not lower them."

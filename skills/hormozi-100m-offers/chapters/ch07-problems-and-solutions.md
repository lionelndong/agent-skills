# Chapter 9: Creating Your Grand Slam Offer, Part I — Problems & Solutions

## Core Idea
Build the offer by selling the destination, not the vehicle ("I wasn't selling the plane flight. I was selling the vacation."). Identify the dream outcome, exhaustively list every problem between the prospect and that outcome, then convert each problem into a solution.

## Frameworks Introduced — The First Three Steps
- **Step #1: Identify Dream Outcome** — the big result with a decreased time delay (e.g., "Lose 20 lbs in 6 weeks"). It must be them arriving at their destination and what they want to experience.
- **Step #2: List Problems** — for each thing the customer must do, think "in insane detail" about what happens immediately before and after, and every reason they couldn't do it or keep doing it. The more problems, the better (more problems = more to solve).
- **Step #3: Solutions List** — reverse each problem into solution-oriented language. "Simply adding 'how to' then reversing the problem" gives a checklist of exactly what you must deliver. You **will solve every problem**.

## Key Concept: Problems Map to the Four Value Drivers
Every problem is really one of the four value drivers in disguise:
1. **Dream Outcome** — "This will not be financially worth it."
2. **Likelihood of Achievement** — "It won't work *for me specifically*; I won't stick with it; external factors will get in the way." (The most unique, service-specific bucket.)
3. **Effort & Sacrifice** — "This will be too hard, confusing; I won't like it; I'll suck at it."
4. **Time** — "This will take too much time / too long to work / won't be convenient."

## Worked Example: Weight-Loss Problem → Solution
For the first action "Buying healthy food / grocery shopping," list the problems, then reverse each:
| Problem | Solution wording |
|---|---|
| …is hard, confusing, I'll suck at it | *How to make buying healthy food easy and enjoyable, so anyone can do it (especially busy moms!)* |
| …takes too much time | *How to buy healthy food quickly* |
| …is expensive | *How to buy healthy food for less than your current grocery bill* |
| …is unsustainable | *How to make buying healthy food take less effort than buying unhealthy food* |
| …my family's needs get in the way | *How to buy healthy food for you and your family at the same time* |
| …undoable if I travel | *How to get healthy food when traveling* |
Repeat for every core action (cooking, eating, exercising…). One worked grid can yield 16 core problems with 2–4 sub-problems each — "32 to 64 problems total."

## Mental Models
- *The more problems you think of, the more problems you get to solve* — "this is the best news ever."
- *Just get all the problems down, then turn them into solutions* — "Don't be fancy" (Brooke Castillo's 90-day relationship-course list made the same way).

## Anti-patterns
- Selling the membership/mechanism instead of the dream outcome.
- Listing only a few problems — "if only one of these needs is missing in a solution, it can cause someone not to buy."

## Key Takeaways
1. Sell the vacation, not the flight — lead with the dream outcome.
2. Exhaustively enumerate problems in the sequence the customer hits them.
3. Turn every problem into a named solution; commit to solving all of them.

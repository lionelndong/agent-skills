# Chapter 4: Pricing — Finding the Right Market (A Starving Crowd)

## Core Idea
"A starving crowd" beats everything. If demand is large and desperate, "you can be mediocre at business, have a terrible offer, and have no ability to persuade people, and you can still make money." We don't create demand — we **channel** it.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Four Indicators of a Great Market** (what Hormozi looks for):
  1. **Massive Pain** — they must *desperately need* it, not merely want it. *"The pain is the pitch."*
  2. **Purchasing Power** — the audience can afford what you charge (the unemployed-résumé-coach failure).
  3. **Easy to Target** — reachable via associations, lists, groups, channels they gather in.
  4. **Growing** — a tailwind, not a headwind (the shrinking-newspapers failure).
- **The Three Markets** (that always exist): **Health, Wealth, Relationships** — enduring desires with perpetual pain when lacking. Find a growing, affordable, targetable sub-group within one.
- **Order of Importance — Three Levers on Success**: **Starving Crowd (Market) > Offer Strength > Persuasion Skills.** A "great" on a higher lever overpowers everything below it; a "bad" market stops the equation.

## Key Concepts
- **Normal market** — growing at the marketplace rate with common unmet needs in health/wealth/relationships. You can make crazy money in a normal market; you just can't be in a *bad* (shrinking) one.
- **Niche slap** — Hormozi's term for hopping niche-to-niche instead of committing. "Don't make me niche slap you."
- **Riches are in the niches** — niching lets you charge dramatically more for nearly the same product.

## Worked Example: Niche Pricing (Dan Kennedy)
The *same* time-management course, re-niched, escalates in price:
- Generic "Time Management" course → ~**$19**
- "Time Management for Sales Professionals" → ~**$99**
- "Time Management for B2B Outbound Sales Reps" → ~**$499** (a 25× increase)
- "Time Management for B2B Outbound Power Tools & Gardening Sales Reps" → **$1,000–$2,000** ("This is made exactly for me").
Core content barely changes; specificity + resonant messaging create the value. (Same logic: generic weight-loss program $19 vs. a program marketed only to shift-nurses $1,997.)

## Mental Models
- *Don't be romantic about your audience* — serve people who can pay you what you're worth.
- *Pick, then commit.* You'll fail until you succeed; switching markets restarts the clock.
- *When to broaden (author note):* under ~$10M/yr, niching usually makes more money; broaden only when your TAM (total addressable market) caps you.

## Anti-patterns
- Selling "ice to eskimos" / fighting a shrinking market (newspapers declining 25%/yr).
- Quitting after one failed offer and blaming the market ("this is a bad market") when the offer was the problem.
- Personalizing failure: "If your offer doesn't work, it doesn't mean *you* suck. It means your *offer* sucks."

## Key Takeaways
1. Market matters most — score candidates on Pain, Purchasing Power, Targeting, Growth.
2. Stay within Health, Wealth, or Relationships; niche down inside it.
3. Commit to the niche long enough to iterate; "if you try one hundred offers, I promise you will succeed."

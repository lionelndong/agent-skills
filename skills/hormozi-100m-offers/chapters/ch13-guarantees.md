# Chapter 15: Enhancing the Offer — Guarantees

## Core Idea
"The single greatest objection for any product or service being sold is… risk." A guarantee **reverses risk** — the #1 way to increase an offer's conversion. Jason Fladlien saw conversion 2–4× simply by changing the quality of the guarantee.

## Frameworks Introduced — The Four Types of Guarantees
1. **Unconditional** — the strongest; "a trial where they pay first then see if they like it" (no-questions-asked / satisfaction). Gets the most buyers; expect some refunds. Best for lower-ticket; risky as ticket/fulfillment cost rises.
2. **Conditional** — includes "terms and conditions"; get creative. Aim for **"better than money back."** Tie the conditions to the key actions required for success (so the guarantee also drives results). Ideally everyone qualifies but, having gotten the result, doesn't want it.
3. **Anti-Guarantee** — "all sales are final." Own it with a creative, believable **"reason why"** (a damaging admission / real exposure: "our product is so exclusive and powerful that once used it cannot be unused"). Attracts serious, motivated buyers.
4. **Implied Guarantee** — performance-based: revshare, profit-share, triggers, ratchets, bonuses ("if I don't perform, I don't get paid"). Hormozi's favorite alignment — but needs measurable outcomes and trust/control over collection.

## The Anatomy of a Guarantee with Teeth
- A guarantee's power is a **conditional statement**: *"If you do not get X result in Y time period, we will Z."*
- Without the "or what" (the Z), it sounds weak. **Bad:** "We'll get you 20 clients guaranteed." **Better:** "You'll get 20 clients in your first 30 days, or we give you your money back + your advertising dollars spent with us."
- **Always hit your guarantee hard**, even if you don't have one — "say it boldly and give the reason why."
- **Name your guarantee** something memorable (his "Club a Baby Seal Guarantee").
- **Stack guarantees** — e.g., an unconditional 30-day + a conditional triple-your-money-back 90-day; or two sequential conditional outcomes ("$10k by 60 days, $30k by 90 days, as long as you do 1, 2, 3").

## The Math (don't be emotional)
A stronger guarantee usually nets more even if refunds rise:
- 100 sales, 5 refunds (5%) = 95 net.
- Guarantee: 130 sales, 13 refunds (10%) = 117 net.
- 117 / 95 = **1.23× (23% more)** — straight to the bottom line. For a guarantee to *not* be worth it, the sales increase would have to be 100% offset by refunds.

## Worked Example: Conditional "Service Guarantee" (his favorite)
*"We keep working with you free of charge until you achieve X."* It guarantees the outcome while eliminating the **time** risk — you're never at risk of losing the money, only the outcome. Add conditions tied to success actions (attend calls, report data, etc.). In practice, almost no one collects: either you happily keep working with a clearly committed client, or they're so close to the goal it gets dropped. (Other catalogued variants: Outsized Refund, Modified Service, Credit-Based, Personal Service, Hotel+Airfare, Wage-Payment, Release of Service, Delayed Second Payment, First Outcome.)

## Key Concepts & Cautions
- **Guarantees are enhancers, not a cure** — they can't fix a poor product or sales team; misused, they "backfire into lots of refunds."
- **Guarantee buyers can be bad customers** — people who buy *only* for the guarantee may not do the work; tying the guarantee to required actions helps.
- **High-cost services warning** — prefer conditional or anti-guarantees when a refund means eating *both* the refund and the fulfillment cost.
- **Reverse their specific fears** — "What are they most afraid of?" Turn time, emotion, and outside costs into the guarantee.

## Mental Models
- *Reversing risk is the number one conversion lever* — spend as much time on the guarantee as on the deliverables.
- *Start with service-based / performance guarantees* — makes all sales final (no refund fear) and commits you to results.

## Anti-patterns
- A vague guarantee with no "or what" (the diluted thing "most marketers do").
- Using a guarantee to paper over a weak product or team.

## Key Takeaways
1. Four types: Unconditional, Conditional, Anti-Guarantee, Implied — and they stack.
2. Structure it "If not X in Y, then Z," make it specific, name it, hit it boldly.
3. Do the math: stronger guarantees usually raise net sales even as refunds rise.

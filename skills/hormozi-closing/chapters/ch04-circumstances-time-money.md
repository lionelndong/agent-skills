# Chapter 4: When They Blame Circumstances — Time & Money

## Core Idea
**Circumstances blame = "I avoid buying your thing because of stuff out of my control."** The fix: help them see they *choose* to let circumstances decide for them — which is good news, because it means **they have the power to change it**, shifting decision-making power from the circumstances back to them. Circumstances split into **Time, Money, and Fit**.

> **Repeated for emphasis:** assume every reply is prefaced by "totally understand / totally get it / that's a great point" before you transition into the close. Validate, then reframe — never disagree.

## Frameworks Introduced

### Time closes — "people make time for stuff either way"
What to do: (1) help them see where their hours go; (2) discuss how to use them better. "Starting while busy is actually the best time to start."
- **"Better To Start When You're Busy"** — "Life is always going to have busy seasons; if you can't figure out how to do it during busy seasons, then this isn't going to last."
- **"You're Gonna Get Busy Again"** — if you only start when free, you'll quit the moment you're busy; learn it busy and "you'll stick with it no matter what life throws at you." *(Nudge: "this is geared to give you time back, not cost you time.")*
- **"It's Not About Timing, It's About Priorities"** — "What are you doing that's more important than this right now? … either you're never going to have time, or something needs to become less important so this can be more important."
- **The "Smartphone Close"** — Hormozi's wife pulled his screen time (20 hrs/week on social media): "I just found you some time." "Bezos, you, and me all had the same 24 hours… it's all about how we allocate them."
- **The "When/Then" Close** — "I'll do it when I have more time" is a false premise: it's like "When I get healthy, then I'll go to the doctor." It flips the order.

### Money closes — Money blame comes in **four flavors**
1. **Not enough value** — they have the money but don't see the value. **Immediately translate "price too high" into "value too low."** Shift focus from the price they pay to the value they get.
2. **Can't afford it** (far rarer) — appeal to **resourcefulness, not resources**.
3. **Other people do it for less** — ask why they didn't buy from them already; highlight the difference in delivery.
4. **I'd buy it for less** (haggling) — **offer to charge more** to end the haggle.

**(1) Not Enough Value:**
- **"It's Good That It's A Lot"** — a big investment means they'll take it seriously ("if this were just $1, it might be easy to overlook"); the real risk is "walking out the door… and staying where you are."
- **"Good Things Aren't Cheap — Cheap Things Aren't Good"** — "How many free PDFs have you downloaded and made no more money?"
- **"Would You Even Believe Me If It Were Less?"** — "Would you even believe it could do what I say if it were one third the price? … Then what are we talking about?"
- **"It's Not A Lot When You Think About It"** — "Hypothetically, if this got you an extra $10,000/mo, would it be worth it? Then it's not the price, it's whether you believe you can do it." (incl. "If all it does is XXX, would it be worth it?")
- **"You're Gonna Spend The Money Either Way"** — "In the next 12 months you're gonna spend this amount either way. All we're doing is deciding where it's going."
- **"You Pay The Price Either Way"** — "with years of your own life to figure it out, or a payment today… We just pay with the currency we value least. Your time or your money."
- **"Some Now or More Later"** and **"Future Favor"** — the cost only increases as you put it off; do future-you a favor.

**(2) Can't Afford It:**
- **"Resourcefulness Not Resources"** — the only difference between a self-made millionaire who had nothing and someone who has nothing is **how resourceful they were** (Phil Knight/Nike: "You need to pay my payroll for me"). *(Nudge: "If you needed life-saving surgery, would you find a way to pay for it? So the question is, is saving yourself from the life you're living worth it?")*
- **"Had It Worse And Done Better"** / **"Everyone Starts At Zero."**

**(3) Other People Do It For Less:**
- **"Cheap Comparison Close"** — "If we were the same price, which one would you pick? *(they say you)* Why?" Let them list your advantages, then: "So now you understand why we aren't the same price, right?"
- **"Good Fast Cheap"** and **"Cheap Or What You Need"** — "you can buy something cheap, or what you need."

**(4) Haggling:**
- **The "We Could Do It For More" Close** — "Uhh… we could do it for more (ha)." "If someone ever asks for a discount, this works insanely well."
- **"It's Not, Not The Money"** — "If this were free, would you do it? *(yes)* … So it's not about the money" — strips away every non-price objection so you isolate and dismantle the real one.

## Key Concepts
- **STAR (qualification, Author Note)** — sell only qualified prospects: **S**ituation (problem to solve), **T**iming (time to solve it), **A**uthority (authority to decide), **R**esources (money to spend). Unqualified prospects "are not a sales problem — they're an advertising problem."
- **"Price too high" = "value too low"** — the central money reframe.
- **Resourcefulness > resources** — the core "can't afford it" lever.

## Mental Models
- **You pay either way** — every prospect spends the cost in *some* currency (money now, or years of their life); your job is to let them "pay with the currency they value least."
- **Translate price → value instantly** — never argue the price; argue the value gap.
- **Haggling is a trap** — "as soon as you say you'll do it for less, you give up all appeals to your product's value. This takes you from closing to haggling. Gross."

## Worked Example: The "It's Not, Not The Money" isolation
Prospect: "It's not the money, I just don't think I'd use it."
Rep: "If this were free, would you do it?"
Prospect: "Yeah, I guess."
Rep: "So it's not actually about the money. OK — well, either way you might as well lock in the savings now, then we can push out your start date as far as you like. Because if you like a discount now, you'll like one later."
— Once they admit they'd take it free, **every objection unrelated to price collapses**, leaving one real obstacle to dismantle.

## Anti-patterns
- **Discounting to win the sale** (Rule 11) — never change price on the spot.
- **Arguing the price is fair** — reframe to value instead; "price too high" is "value too low."
- **Selling an unqualified (no-STAR) prospect** — that's an advertising failure, not a close to attempt.

## Key Takeaways
1. Circumstances = "out of my control" → show them they're *choosing* it.
2. Time: the best time to start is *now*, busy — it's priorities, not timing.
3. Money has four flavors; the master move is **"price too high" → "value too low."**
4. Can't afford it → **resourcefulness, not resources**. Haggling → offer to charge *more*.

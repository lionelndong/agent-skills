# Chapter 2: Blame (The Onion of Blame) + My Rules of Closing

## Core Idea
**Blame is what robs people of their decision-making power.** When people avoid a decision they could make, they blame "stuff that *looks* reasonable but… doesn't make much sense." Closing = patiently peeling back those layers — addressing the decision-avoidance falsehoods — until the prospect has their power back and can decide.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The Onion of Blame** — every objection sorts into **three sources of blame** (seek to understand, don't memorize):
  1. **Circumstances (External)** — "I must get what I want, when I want it. I must not get what I don't want. Or else… XYZ circumstance is to blame."
  2. **Other People** — "I must get treated fairly and kindly. Or else… XYZ person is to blame."
  3. **Self** — "I must be perfect, I must do well, I must get what I want, or else my XYZ character trait is to blame."
  - When to use: as soon as a prospect says anything that isn't yes. Identify which layer is talking.
  - How: peel back layers one at a time; **expect people to swap objections** — "when that happens, it means you're closing correctly."
- **The objection map (what it is → how it presents):**
  - **Circumstances** → "I'm too busy," "It's too expensive," "It's not for me." Splits into **Time / Money / Fit**.
  - **Other People** → "I need to talk to my [spouse]," "I've been burned before." Splits into **Authority/Permission** and **Trust/Skepticism**.
  - **Self** → "I need to think about it," "It's not for me." Splits into **Fear** and **Avoidance**.
- **Designed for "after the prospect says no."** "The better you are at marketing, the more concerns have been resolved ahead of time." Worse marketing → your sales process must be more thorough.

## Key Concepts
- **Obstacle / Objection** — the "power-stealing stuff people blame"; nobody says "I'm suffering from a distorted reality, please educate me."
- **Swapping objections** — moving from one blame to another; a *signal of progress*, not failure.
- **Nudge** — a one-liner (not a full overcome) used "when someone is right on the edge" to tip a deal over.
- **Stacking closes** — hitting the same objection from another angle/analogy if the first doesn't land; "each will take us one step closer to a sale."
- **Kind, not nice** — "Nice guys don't want to offend. Kind people want to genuinely help… and sometimes that means calling them on their BS… in a tactful way."

## Mental Models
- **Follow the finger of blame** — "if a person avoids a decision because they don't have power… where does the power go? Follow the finger of blame." Whatever they're pointing at is the chain enslaving them.
- **Selling is a transference of belief over a bridge of trust** — you can only build trust if you genuinely want to help; **belief and trust are a continuum, not binaries** ("it's not *whether* they believe you, it's *how much*").
- **You sit on the same side of the table** — you're not competing with the prospect. "You can never convince someone. We can only guide them… to convince themselves."
- **The person who cares the most about the prospect wins the sale** — keep the prospect, not the sale, as the priority; "it completely divorces you from needing to make a sale."

## Worked Example: The Rules Drilled Into the Team
A practitioner-ready slice of the **28 Rules of Closing** (the ones to commit to memory):
- **Rule 1 — When in doubt, repeat back what they said** (buys thinking time + active-listening points).
- **Rule 2 — Acknowledge or agree, but never disagree** (disagreement breeds hostility); transition with "totally agree," "that's a great point," "thank you for bringing that up."
- **Rule 3 — Before getting real, get permission**: *"Can I be a coach instead of a friend for a second?"*
- **Rule 9 — Yellows are golds; be a trashman**: practice on unqualified leads — "the more you close the better you get." Less-qualified prospects are a *bigger* challenge → better practice.
- **Rule 10 — Don't assume objections mean they won't buy**: "It's expensive" is often an observation, not an objection — sometimes "you're just there for the show."
- **Rule 11 — Never change your price on the spot** to get a sale (a "sad, dark, and ethically questionable path").
- **Rule 13 — Only ask for the sale when you think you've got 'em**; otherwise keep probing.
- **Rule 15 — Selling happens before you ask for the sale; closing happens after.**
- **Rule 17 — Expect and plan for no. It's not failure; it's expected.**
- **Rule 26 — Once the prospect says yes, shut up** ("the rule of law" — anything more can disrupt what's working).
- **Rule 27 — Record all your sales** to learn from them ("champions watch game tape").

## Key Takeaways
1. Every objection is one of three blames: **Circumstances, Other People, Self**.
2. People often blame more than one thing — **expect swaps**; peel the onion patiently.
3. Repeat back → acknowledge (never disagree) → reframe; **get permission before getting real**.
4. You only need **two or three closes per obstacle** — "get good at closing, not become an encyclopedia of closes."
5. Keep the prospect (not the sale) as the priority; **volume negates luck**.

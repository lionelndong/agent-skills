# Chapter 3: All-Purpose Closes (the 80/20)

## Core Idea
**These are the 20% of closes that work 80% of the time.** Use them to respond to **anything that's not yes**. They "cut through a lot of smoke and mirrors and force prospects to wake up and decide" — or to surface a *real* obstacle you can then overcome with the blame-specific closes.

## Frameworks Introduced (the named All-Purpose Closes)
- **The "What's Your Main Concern" Close** — *"What's your main concern? What are you afraid of happening?"*
  - When to use: any stall, especially "I need to think about it." Hormozi's "most used phrase to build rapport."
  - How: if they give fluff, escalate to "what are you afraid of happening?"; if still stuck, spell out the absurd worst case ("…so you're afraid of spending money and me stealing it and spending it in Vegas?") — they laugh, then give a truer answer.
- **The "Reason" Close** — *"The reason you are telling yourself not to do this is the reason you should do it."*
  - The biggest chain you're holding is the thing enslaving you; break it and power returns. Works on Money, Time, Authority, Avoidance (e.g., Money: "the fact that you don't have money is the very reason you need to do this more than anyone").
- **The "Hypothetical" Close** — *"If this were perfect, would you do it? Then what's the difference between perfect and what we've got?"*
  - If they name a small detail, fix it and close. If they can't name anything: "it sounds like it's nothing to do with the program; it's something else. What are you afraid of happening?" "This works astonishingly well."
- **The "Zoom Out" Close** — *"You want [X]. We sell [X]. Do you think overanalyzing the details might be the reason you haven't made it happen yet?"* Reframe the decision as: does this make it **more likely** you hit your goal than doing it on your own?
- **The "1 to 10" Close** — "On a scale of 1 to 10… where do you see yourself?" If **10** → "Great. Let's rock and roll." If **9 or less** → "What would it take to get you to a 10?" → handle it → "Feeling 10ish now?"
- **The "Best Case Worst Case" Close** — frame the options as all risk-free except one (because of the guarantee). "Only one of them has a true guarantee of *not* getting you where you want, which is walking out the door." (Has a no-guarantee variant: "you take no risk but you guarantee you don't get what you want — by not starting.")
- **The "Card Not On Me" Close** — *"Do you have your phone on you? Pull up your banking app, go to statements, find the account number top right. I'll Google the routing number while you do that."* Handles "I forgot my wallet / don't have my card."

## Key Concepts
- **Nudge** — a one-liner woven into many closes to tip an edge-case over.
- **The ID gesture (process to avoid the wallet objection)** — get verbal agreement, then **ask for their ID** (forces the wallet out), fill the contract from it, chit-chat about the photo to fill time, then: *"I'll trade you for the card you wanna use."* Other cards are now in plain sight.
- **ACH (Author Note)** — take ACH payments: lower costs, fewer expirations/chargebacks, and you can "always sign up a new client, even if they don't have their wallet."

## Mental Models
- Treat the first objection as **smoke** — the all-purpose closes either dissolve it or force a real obstacle to the surface.
- **Worst-case-out-loud** — naming an absurd worst case makes the prospect laugh, which breaks the avoidance spell and returns honesty to the conversation.
- **Make the "do nothing" option explicitly costly** — Best Case/Worst Case reframes walking out as the *only* guaranteed loss.

## Worked Example: The "1 to 10" sequence end-to-end
Rep: "On a scale of 1 to 10, where 1 is not interested at all and 10 is ready to start right now — where are you?"
Prospect: "Probably a 7."
Rep: "What would it take to get you to a 10?"
Prospect: "I'd want to be sure the meal plan fits my schedule."
Rep: "No big deal. I think we can take care of that for you. *[handles it]* … Feeling 10ish now?"
— The number externalizes the hesitation, then the follow-up converts the gap into a *single, nameable* obstacle you close on.

## Anti-patterns
- **Asking for the sale before diffusing concerns** — "It's easier to handle problems before you ask for money than after."
- **Accepting "I need to think about it" at face value** — always convert it into a named concern.

## Key Takeaways
1. Lead with the 80/20 closes; they expose the real obstacle.
2. "What's your main concern / what are you afraid of happening?" is the workhorse.
3. The Reason Close flips the very excuse into the reason to buy.
4. Use the **ID/wallet process** so payment-method objections never come up.

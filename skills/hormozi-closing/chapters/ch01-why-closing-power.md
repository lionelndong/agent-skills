# Chapter 1: Why Is Closing So Important? + Power

## Core Idea
**Closing — the most important part of selling — starts *after* you've asked the prospect to buy.** "If you don't close, you don't make money." The job of closing isn't to get the prospect to buy; it's to **give the prospect enough power to overcome decision avoidance** and make a genuine decision either way.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Advertising Splits Your Audience Into Three Buckets** — the foundational map of who you're selling to:
  1. **YESes (Laydowns)** — the advertising already closed them; "you just have to show up to accept payment." *Goal: Don't mess it up.*
  2. **NOs (Unqualified)** — a bad fit; the advertising pulled in the wrong person ("people in Ohio who opt in to a gym offer in Florida").
  3. **MAYBEs (On the fence)** — value what you sell but have reservations. *Most* interested people land here. **"This is why we train."**
  - **"The money is in the maybes."** Closing determines who captures bucket three.
- **Power** — the missing ingredient in most buying decisions. People on the fence "have too little power to influence their own decision" so they waffle ("maybe yes / maybe no" = neither). **Closing gives people the power to decide.**
  - When to use: every time a prospect avoids deciding rather than says a true "no."
  - How (giving decision-making power = three things): 1) get a good understanding of what they want (earlier in the conversation); 2) **put what they want next to the options they have to get there**; 3) make sure they come to a genuine decision.
- **"No" vs. "I avoid deciding"** — both fail to produce a purchase and *look* identical, but are different. The costly mistake is sweeping avoidant maybes into the "no" bucket. Closing is how you tell them apart.

## Key Concepts
- **Laydown** — a YES who buys regardless; advertising did the closing.
- **Unqualified** — a prospect who is a genuine bad fit (an advertising problem, not a sales problem).
- **On the fence** — a qualified MAYBE with reservations; the target of all closing.
- **Decision avoidance** — the waffling state ("maybe yes, maybe no") closing exists to break.
- **Bigger pie** — better ads reach more people → more maybes → "closing gets you a bigger slice of it."

## Mental Models
- **Champions close.** Like top red-zone offenses, golfers in putting range, and Federer (won 54% of points but 80% of matches by winning *the points that mattered*) — winners score the points that matter. The ask "puts the entire deal for money on the line."
- Think of better advertising as **making the pie bigger** (more maybes); closing is **taking a bigger slice**.
- **You win either way.** Close properly → best case they buy and live happily ever after; worst case they don't, and you dodge "the cancerous and costly bullet of unqualified purchases." The only failure is leaving someone on the **avoidance treadmill**.

## Anti-patterns
- **Treating a purchase as a binary (buy / not buy)** — it's three buckets, not two; this "horrendous mistake costs businesses so much money."
- **Believing buyers "would never" buy at a certain price or without a salesperson** — people buy cars and property online; it's "just a smaller pool."
- **Letting a maybe stay on the avoidance treadmill** — "you let them down. Everyone's time is wasted, nobody benefits, and the prospect is worse off."

## Worked Example: Gym Launch, January 2017
Hormozi went **$412/working-hour into debt** ($3,300/day across six gym launches) on a $100k business credit card — his last move. The ads worked but **nobody bought**: sales reps reported "2 for 14," "0 for 18." Reps were hearing "needs to talk to their husband," "needs to think about it," "too expensive" — and couldn't handle them. The realization: *the reps weren't lazy or dumb — they didn't know how to sell.* He built a playbook, drilled one objection at a time (stalls first, then price), went from red to black, and broke even by month's end. That training turned into a business that processed **$6.8M** over the next 13 months. "Sales teams routinely double close rates with effective closing."

## Key Takeaways
1. Closing starts *after* the ask; selling happens before it.
2. Advertising makes three buckets — YES / NO / MAYBE — and the money is in the maybes.
3. The goal of closing is **power to decide**, not the sale itself; done right you win either way.
4. A genuine "no" and decision avoidance look the same — closing separates them.

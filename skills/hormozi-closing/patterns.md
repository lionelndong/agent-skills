# Patterns — $100M Playbook: Closing

## Pattern: Diagnose the blame (the Onion of Blame)
**When to use**: The instant a prospect says anything that isn't yes.
**How**: Sort the objection into one of three layers — **Circumstances** (Time/Money/Fit), **Other People** (Authority/Permission, Trust), **Self** (Fear/Avoidance, Preferences). Peel one layer at a time; expect objection swaps (a sign you're closing correctly).
**Trade-offs**: Seek to understand, not memorize; mis-labeling the layer sends you to the wrong close.

## Pattern: The validate-then-reframe frame (the universal wrapper)
**When to use**: Before *every* close.
**How**: Repeat back what they said → acknowledge ("totally understand / that's a great point / thank you for bringing that up") → deliver a reframe. Never disagree. Get permission before getting real ("Can I be a coach instead of a friend for a second?").
**Trade-offs**: Disagreement breeds hostility; this costs a beat of time but keeps you on the same side of the table.

## Pattern: Lead with the All-Purpose (80/20) Closes
**When to use**: Any non-yes, before you know the real obstacle.
**How**: What's Your Main Concern → Reason → Hypothetical → Zoom Out → 1 to 10 → Best Case Worst Case → Card Not On Me. They dissolve smoke or force a nameable obstacle.
**Trade-offs**: They're general-purpose; once a specific blame surfaces, switch to the targeted close.

## Pattern: What's Your Main Concern
**When to use**: Stalls, "I need to think about it."
**How**: "What's your main concern? What are you afraid of happening?" If fluff → escalate; if stuck → name the absurd worst case so they laugh and get honest.
**Trade-offs**: Builds rapport but only opens the door — you still must overcome what surfaces.

## Pattern: The Reason Close (flip the excuse)
**When to use**: Money, Time, Authority, Avoidance objections.
**How**: "The reason you're telling yourself not to do this is the reason you should." Identify the chain enslaving them; break it and power returns.
**Trade-offs**: Powerful but pointed — needs rapport/tonality or it feels like a trick.

## Pattern: Translate "price too high" → "value too low"
**When to use**: Money / "it's too expensive" (the *not enough value* flavor).
**How**: Immediately shift focus from the price they pay to the value they get. Stack: It's Good That It's A Lot · Good Things Aren't Cheap · Would You Even Believe Me If It Were Less · It's Not A Lot When You Think About It · You Pay/Spend The Money Either Way.
**Trade-offs**: Never argue the price is fair; argue the value gap. Don't discount (Rule 11).

## Pattern: Resourcefulness Not Resources
**When to use**: A genuine "I can't afford it" (the far rarer flavor).
**How**: "The only difference between a self-made millionaire who had nothing and someone who has nothing is how resourceful they were" (Phil Knight/Nike). Nudge: "If you needed life-saving surgery, would you find the money?"
**Trade-offs**: Only for qualified prospects (STAR); don't sell someone genuinely beyond their means.

## Pattern: Handle the cheaper competitor
**When to use**: "The guy down the street does it for less."
**How**: Cheap Comparison Close — "If we were the same price, which would you pick? Why?" Let them list your advantages, then highlight the difference in delivery.
**Trade-offs**: Ask why they didn't already buy from the cheaper option — that exposes the real reason.

## Pattern: End the haggle by charging more
**When to use**: "Can you do it for less?"
**How**: "Uhh… we could do it for more (ha)." Optionally quote a *higher* price. Forces a decision.
**Trade-offs**: "As soon as you say you'll do it for less, you give up all appeals to your value… Gross." Never haggle.

## Pattern: Isolate price with "It's Not, Not The Money"
**When to use**: "It's not the money, it's [some other thing]."
**How**: "If this were free, would you do it?" (yes) → "So it's not about the money." Every non-price objection collapses, leaving one obstacle to dismantle.
**Trade-offs**: Then you must actually overcome the surfaced obstacle.

## Pattern: Time → priorities, and "start busy"
**When to use**: "I'm too busy / I'll start when I have time."
**How**: Better To Start When You're Busy · You're Gonna Get Busy Again · It's Not About Timing It's About Priorities · Smartphone Close · When/Then. Show where their hours go, then reframe to priorities.
**Trade-offs**: Requires they engage with the time audit; pushy delivery backfires.

## Pattern: Decision-Maker sequence (Other People → Authority)
**When to use**: "I have to talk to my spouse/partner."
**How**: Prevent it by qualifying decision makers onto the call. Else run: (1) What if they say no? (2) What would they object to? (3) If they know you're unhappy, why would they stop you? (4) Ask for **support, not permission**. Add role reversal + Three-Day No-Sweat (delay payment 24–72 hrs).
**Trade-offs**: "Best for selling cheap stuff relative to the buying power of the prospect." If it materially affects the household, you genuinely need buy-in — don't force it.

## Pattern: Don't Let It Burn You Twice (Other People → Trust)
**When to use**: "I've been burned before."
**How**: "It burned you once with the poor investment; it'll burn you twice if it stops you making a good one." Use humor/analogy (bad eighth-grade boyfriend → not all coaches suck). Rapport and tonality carry it.
**Trade-offs**: Without big rapport "you will flop."

## Pattern: Isolate And Solve (Self → Preferences)
**When to use**: "This isn't right for me / I want it my way."
**How**: Always start: "What specifically would make it for you vs. not for you?" Then: We Vote With Our Dollars · Pain Of Change · You Gotta Change To Change · Change The Variables–Change The Outcome.
**Trade-offs**: Their preferences likely caused the problem; make them choose result vs. preference.

## Pattern: The Mechanic / Surgeon Secretary Close
**When to use**: A hyper-specific question you can't answer on the spot.
**How**: "Answering that would be like a mechanic diagnosing your car over the phone before it's in the shop." The real answer comes only after you know them better. Run from anyone who *does* answer.
**Trade-offs**: Don't bluff a specific answer; defer credibly instead.

## Pattern: Cost-of-inaction closes (Self → Rushed/Avoidance)
**When to use**: "Let me think about it / I need more information."
**How**: What Would Make It A No · How Much Has Not Deciding Cost You · Another Year Of Almost · It Doesn't Take Time It Takes Information · Gameplan Close · This Isn't A Fast Decision.
**Trade-offs**: Frame around *not deciding* as the costly choice; avoid manufactured urgency ("I don't want you to feel an ounce of urgency, but…").

## Pattern: Guarantee Closes
**When to use**: Only when you offer a guarantee.
**How**: Both Options Are Risk Free · Decadere (decide = "cut off"; which future are you cutting off?) · Closer Or Further ("directionally right"). Make walking out the only guaranteed loss.
**Trade-offs**: Hollow without a real guarantee behind them.

## Pattern: The ID/wallet process (kill the payment-method objection)
**When to use**: Before asking for the card, to pre-empt "forgot my wallet."
**How**: Get verbal agreement → ask for their ID (forces wallet out) → fill the contract from it → chit-chat about the photo → "I'll trade you for the card you wanna use." Backstop with ACH and the Card Not On Me close.
**Trade-offs**: Take ACH so a missing card never kills a sale.

## Pattern: Practice on everyone (Yellows are golds)
**When to use**: Skill-building, ongoing.
**How**: Close even unqualified leads — "the more you close the better you get." Volume negates luck. Record every sale and improve one thing at a time.
**Trade-offs**: Don't confuse practice with a real sale to a no-STAR prospect; qualify before *selling*.

# Glossary — The New Secret Chapter (Section A: Attract)

**Advertisement Length (friction lever)** — Lengthening ads/copy/videos before the CTA; the time commitment alone adds friction → fewer but more valuable clicks. (Ch 3)

**Attraction (Section A: Attract)** — Getting people to engage who otherwise wouldn't, using free & discount promotions wrapped around a core premium offer. (Ch 1)

**Avatar** — The specific customer whose deep desires, fears, and everyday struggles your copy must name precisely ("cleaning the bathrooms yet again," not "working hard"). (Ch 2)

**Bargain Hoppers** — Discount-seekers with no apparent intent to buy the main thing; should be treated as qualified leads, not customers, and qualified via offer structure (the "Groupon issue"). (Ch 4)

**CAC** — Cost of acquiring a customer; learning to profit from free offers lowers it and raises ROI. (Ch 3)

**Card On File** — The captured payment method from a first (discounted) transaction that makes later upsells frictionless. (Ch 4)

**Discount Offer** — A massive (50%+) reduction on a *component* of your offering ("a piece of the thing"), used to acquire and transact with a customer compliantly and cheaply. (Ch 4)

**Forced Consumption (friction lever)** — Making a prospect consume sales material (e.g. a 40-minute video) before any CTA appears; pre-indoctrinates; Hormozi's favorite quality lever. (Ch 3)

**Free Money Math** — Comparing campaigns by *absolute qualified leads*, not percent qualified; higher raw volume + friction often beats a "cleaner-feeling" low-volume campaign. (Ch 3)

**Free Offer** — "Value in advance" / "something for nothing"; the highest-volume, lowest-cost front end; "the most powerful offer of all time." (Ch 3)

**Friction** — Anything making an offer harder to take (qualifications, info requirements, steps, forced consumption, length); raises lead quality at the cost of volume. (Ch 1, Ch 3)

**Front End** — The first offer prospects encounter; kept "as low as possible" when upsells sit behind it. (Ch 1)

**Get Flow → Monetize Flow → Then Add Friction** — The master sequence: generate demand first, then monetize, then add friction — "in that order." (Ch 1, Ch 4)

**Grand Slam Offer** — The valuable core offer (from $100M Offers / Vol I) that a free/discount wrapper makes more attractive; never altered by the promotion. (Ch 1)

**Infinite Returns (Power of)** — Pricing upside is uncapped while the downside floors at $0; lets premium offers out-earn high-volume competitors. (Ch 2)

**Lazy Whales** — Otherwise-qualified, high-value prospects you can lose if you add *too much* friction. (Ch 3)

**Marginal Discount** — A 5–25%-off discount; "not enough to drive real behavior," just cuts margin — avoid. (Ch 4)

**Massive Discount** — 50%+ off; the level that drives action "from a population that wouldn't otherwise act." (Ch 4)

**Penny Gap** — Dr. Dan Ariely's finding that ~9x more people take a free item than one priced at a penny; the landslide power of $0 over $0.01. (Ch 3)

**Perceived Value Discrepancy** — The value-vs-price gap that, on its own, drives action; shared mechanism of free and discount offers. (Ch 4)

**Pre-indoctrination** — Using forced consumption so only educated, primed prospects reach the call to action. (Ch 3)

**Premium Offer** — Selling the client the best outcome available at high ticket, presented standalone (no wrapper); "just different," not better/worse than other offer types. (Ch 2)

**Promotional Wrapper** — Free/discount packaging around the core offer; "wrapping paper, not the gift" — changes attractiveness, not value. The point is to *enhance* the Grand Slam Offer, not change it. (Ch 1)

**Skimming ("the cream, not the milk")** — Premium marketing pays a premium for the best customers rather than acquiring everyone. (Ch 2)

**Splintering** — Breaking the offer into tiny pieces so you discount only one core *component*, not "the whole farm." (Ch 4)

**Two-Step Sale** — A discounted first step (captures card + trust, kills no-shows) leading to an expensive second-step sale; "probably the biggest benefit" of discounts. (Ch 4)

**Why? (answering the)** — A too-good free/discount offer triggers disbelief, so you must always answer *why* it's so good (e.g. "$100 → $1,000 back," nobody called). (Ch 3)

# Section A: Discount Promotions

## Core Idea
"Free" and "discount" operate very similarly from a money-model view — both "create a **perceived value discrepancy** that on its own can drive action." Hormozi is "not a big believer in 'marginal' discounts (5–25% off)" — they just cut margin. Use **massive discounts (50% or more)** that "drive action from a population that wouldn't otherwise act." A discount offer is usually a **"piece of the thing," not the "entire thing."**

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Massive Discounts Only (50%+)**: marginal discounts (5–25%) don't move real behavior; the discount "has to get people who wouldn't otherwise respond — to respond."
- **The Four Ways To Display Discounts**: "People will respond differently to the same discount displayed differently." There are four common, tested display formats you test against your own offer; "the price of the offer many times informs what will make the most sense." (Demonstrated on an "ultimate lemonade bundle" — 3 bottles/day for $30/day — promoted four different ways; "the numbers don't matter," the *display* does.)
- **The Two-Step Sale** ("probably the biggest benefit"): use a discounted front end to **collect the card over the phone** and complete a low-cost first transaction, which (a) eliminates no-shows, (b) lets you charge penalties to enforce steps, and (c) makes the later, more-expensive upsell "smooth as buttaaa" via the card on file. **Ad → Opt in → Phone Call for promo, set appt → Shows up for promo appt → Gets value → Schedules follow-up → Shows up & is sold the big offer.**
- **Splinter The Offer**: never discount your *core* offer (it trains customers to buy only on discount). Instead "splinter" the offer into tiny pieces and discount **one core component** — "a sliver away as value to get the card on file," not "the whole farm."

## Key Concepts
- **Discount offer** — a massive (50%+) price reduction on a *component* of your offering, used to acquire and transact with a customer cheaply.
- **Perceived value discrepancy** — the gap between value and price that, on its own, drives action (shared by free and discount).
- **Two-step sale** — discounted first step (gets card + trust) → expensive second step (the real sale).
- **Card on file** — the captured payment method that makes upsells frictionless ("do you want to use the card on file?").
- **Bargain hoppers** — discount-seekers; treat as **qualified leads**, not customers, and qualify them via offer structure.
- **Splintering** — breaking the offer into pieces so only a small component is discounted.

## Mental Models
- **A discount buyer is a qualified lead, not a customer** — "We shouldn't see people as customers if they buy the discount. We should see them as qualified leads." (The Groupon "issue" was businesses that couldn't structure offers to qualify buyers into core customers.)
- **People show up for what they pay for** — even a small charge yields ~85–90%+ show rates, "entirely eliminating no-shows."
- **Eliminate the card-out moment** — Amazon one-click, Disney money wristbands: removing the "take your card out" friction sells more. Use the card on file to do the same.
- **Match the right tool to the job** — discount over free when you need compliance, some cash, or a two-step.

## The Pros & Cons (taught as a lemonade-stand dialogue)
**Pros:** (1) **Lots of cheap leads within the law** — where "creative offers around free" are forbidden, discounts let you "advertise compliantly" and still get volume (use in heavily regulated industries/states/countries). (2) **You actually collect some money** — helps liquidate acquisition costs, "but we will never build our business to use this money as the real way we are liquidating our costs." (3) **People come in expecting to spend some money** — "more of a mental benefit," easing limiting beliefs in owners/staff. (4) **The Two-Step Sale** (the biggest benefit) — eliminates no-shows; enforce steps with penalties; paves seamless upsells. (5) **Discount offers make upsells smooth** — card on file → frictionless next sale.

**Cons:** (1) **Giving Away The Farm** — discounting the *core* offer trains customers to buy only at discount ("no bueno"); fix by splintering and discounting only a component. (The exception: a pricing model that "wildly increase[s] prices during the 'regular season' and live[s] off the discounting," like clothing retailers — still a double-edged sword.) (2) **Bargain Hoppers** — like freebie-seekers; resolve with intelligent offer structure that auto-qualifies prospects into core customers.

## Worked Example: The $19 → $2,100 Two-Step Sale
Give away a **heavy metals test consultation for $19** to capture a lead and a card over the phone; then upsell a **$2,100 10-week lemonade detox plan** when you meet:

**Ad → Opt in → Phone call for the $19 promo, set appointment → Shows up for the $19 appointment → Gets value → Schedules a follow-up appointment → Shows up at the second appointment and is sold the $2,100 program.**

Because the card is already on file, the upsell sidesteps "I forgot my card at home" and the "take your card out" hesitation — "you would be amazed at how many more people will buy when they don't have to take their card out."

## Anti-patterns
- **Marginal discounts (5–25% off)** — too small to drive behavior; just erodes margin.
- **Discounting the core offer** — trains customers to wait for sales ("Giving Away The Farm").
- **Treating discount buyers as your business model** — the cash "won't amount to much"; don't fund marketing on it.
- **Discounting something prospects don't understand** — a discount needs a known reference price; works best for "well-understood" services (dentists, chiropractors, gyms, haircuts).

## Key Takeaways
1. Use **massive (50%+) discounts on a splintered component**, not marginal discounts on the core offer.
2. The discount's real power is the **two-step sale**: capture the card cheaply, eliminate no-shows, then upsell frictionlessly.
3. Discounts win when **free is non-compliant**, when staff need the "they're here to spend" mindset, or for **well-understood** services with known price ranges.
4. See discount buyers as **qualified leads**; structure the offer so they self-qualify into core customers (avoid the Groupon trap).

## Attract Section Conclusion: Brass Tacks
"If you are not getting enough response, you probably need to make your front end more appealing" — give people who otherwise wouldn't respond a reason to. Enhancing your Grand Slam Offer with a free or discount front end is the fastest way, whether finding your first acquisition channel or adding another. **Always: Generate Flow → Monetize Flow → Increase Friction. In that order.** Fundamentally, the process works only because you **know your business and product better than your customers do** — all businesses capitalize on an information advantage, making customers aware of the problems ahead and monetizing through upsells.

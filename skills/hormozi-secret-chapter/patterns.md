# Patterns — The New Secret Chapter (Section A: Attract)

## Pattern: Get Flow → Monetize Flow → Then Add Friction
**When to use**: Entering any new market or adding any new acquisition channel.
**How**: Lead with a free or massively discounted front end to generate demand/lead flow → start transacting and monetizing → *then* add friction to raise quality and monetize more aggressively. Keep the front end as low as possible if you can layer upsells behind it.
**Trade-offs**: Generating demand first feels slow/unprofitable early ("cart before the horse" is the temptation), but flow is the real constraint — solve it before profit.

## Pattern: The Promotional Wrapper
**When to use**: Cold markets where prospects need a reason to move toward you (answer "what's in it for me?").
**How**: Keep the Grand Slam Offer (the gift) intact; change only the *wrapping* — present it free or discounted to make it more inherently attractive. Free/discount are wrappers around the core premium offer.
**Trade-offs**: A promotion must *enhance* the offer, never change/water it down; the value inside stays the same.

## Pattern: Premium Offer (standalone Grand Slam Offer)
**When to use**: You have something very valuable AND a selling process that demonstrates it; you want max profit per sale at low volume.
**How**: Present the high-ticket Grand Slam Offer on its own (no discount/trial). Buy traffic on a "$25,000/hr budget"; pack the offer with bonuses, creative guarantees, and premium support so it still feels like a steal. Often best as the **second** offer after a free/discount first offer demonstrated value.
**Trade-offs**: Highest cost per lead, slowest break-even, least efficient for market capture; needs cash, strong copy (avatar-specific), and a proven sales process. If new/low-volume/high-CAC, wrap it in free or discount first.

## Pattern: The 9.5x Premium Math
**When to use**: Deciding between a high-volume cheap offer and a low-volume premium offer on the same traffic.
**How**: Take the audience a cheap offer would close (e.g. 190 @ $50 profit) and offer them the premium (~$10k) instead; expect ~5% close → 9.5 sales × $9,500 = $90,250 vs. $9,500. ≈ 9.5x more profit with ~10 clients instead of 190.
**Trade-offs**: Requires the premium trifecta (valuable offer, avatar copy, sales process); volume/eyeballs needed per bite is far higher.

## Pattern: Free Front End (maximize volume)
**When to use**: You need the most leads per eyeball / lowest cost per lead, small or local markets, or to *test* whether anyone wants your thing.
**How**: Offer real value for free ("value in advance"); harness the **penny gap** (free ≫ $0.01). Read failures diagnostically: don't-want / don't-believe / wrong-pond. Then add friction to skim the cream.
**Trade-offs**: Close rate & ticket are unchanged vs. premium (split-test proven) — only lead cost/volume change (often 5x cheaper leads); volume can overwhelm ops and attract non-buyers, both fixed with friction/offer design.

## Pattern: Friction Dial (5 levers)
**When to use**: Free/cheap front end is producing too much or too-low-quality volume.
**How**: Add, in any combination — (1) **Qualifications** (state requirements; repeat everywhere), (2) **Information requirement & question type** (more/heavier fields; open-ended > multiple-choice in friction), (3) **Number of steps** (each sheds prospects), (4) **Forced consumption** (watch a long video before any CTA; best when eyeballs are cheap), (5) **Advertisement length** (longer pre-CTA content). Each cuts volume and raises quality.
**Trade-offs**: Find the sweet spot — "weed out the weirdos" without losing "lazy whales"; too much friction kills otherwise-qualified high-value leads.

## Pattern: Answer "Why?"
**When to use**: Any free/discount offer good enough to trigger disbelief.
**How**: Pair the crazy offer with a credible reason it exists, so prospects believe it (an unbelievable offer gets ignored — "$100 → $1,000 back," nobody called).
**Trade-offs**: Without a "why," the better the offer, the *less* it converts.

## Pattern: Discount Offer (compliant, cash-collecting front end)
**When to use**: Free is non-compliant (regulated industry/state/country), you want some up-front cash, staff need a "they're here to spend" mindset, or the service is **well-understood** (dentists, chiropractors, gyms, haircuts) with a known reference price.
**How**: Use a **massive (50%+)** discount on a **splintered component**, not a marginal discount on the core offer. Collect the card over the phone; treat buyers as qualified leads.
**Trade-offs**: Cash "won't amount to much" — don't fund marketing on it; discounting the core offer trains buyers to wait for sales; a discount on something prospects can't price-reference doesn't land.

## Pattern: The Two-Step Sale
**When to use**: You need multiple steps completed before a bigger sale, want to kill no-shows, and want a frictionless upsell.
**How**: Step 1 — discounted offer captures the card + builds trust (set the follow-up appointment; charge a penalty if they no-show). Step 2 — deliver value, then upsell the expensive offer using the **card on file** ("want to use the card on file?"). Flow: Ad → Opt in → promo call/appt → shows up → gets value → schedules follow-up → shows up → sold. Example: $19 heavy-metals consult → $2,100 10-week detox.
**Trade-offs**: People show up for what they pay for (~85–90%+); eliminating the "card-out" moment (à la Amazon one-click, Disney wristbands) sells more.

## Pattern: Splinter The Offer (don't give away the farm)
**When to use**: You want a discount front end without training customers to buy only on discount.
**How**: Break the offer into tiny pieces; discount only **one core component** ("a sliver") to get the card on file and enable upsells — never the whole core offer.
**Trade-offs**: The exception is a "high regular price, live off discounting" model (clothing retailers) — still a double-edged sword.

# Glossary — $100M Playbook: Retention (Churn Checklist)

All terms are Hormozi's, scoped to this playbook.

- **ACA Framework** — the regular check-in reach-out script: **A**cknowledge (what you've seen them do), **C**omplement, **A**sk a question. Used 1-on-1 every 2–3 weeks between surveys. (Ch 3)
- **Activation Point** — a leading indicator of retention: "Every customer that does (X thing) or gets (Y result) stays longer than customers who don't." The single highest-priority thing to find. (Ch 3)
- **Annual Payment Option** — letting/requiring customers prepay a year so they stay a year; default offer "buy 10 months, get 2 free" (16% off). (Ch 3)
- **Ascension / Ascend** — the 4th customer-journey milestone: getting an existing customer to buy more. Just-purchased customers are the least likely to churn. (Ch 3)
- **Big Head, Long Tail** — pricing structure: large one-time upfront (education/setup) priced to its value + small recurring priced to consumable value; kept on one sale to preserve the anchor. (Ch 3)
- **Carrots > Sticks** — onboarding guideline: incentives beat penalties. (Ch 3)
- **Churn** — customers who leave over a period; `leavers ÷ original customer pool`. Counts only the first-timepoint pool. (Ch 2)
- **Churn Checklist** — the 9-step cross-industry retention system (the core of this playbook). (Ch 3)
- **Common Factors Analysis** — "find the stuff they have in common" across many experts/customers to reveal the real driver and eliminate bias. (Ch 1, Ch 3)
- **Community Linking** — connecting members to each other (not to you) via events, intros, podcasts, and elevating micro-celebrities. (Ch 3)
- **Customer Journey (Four Milestones)** — Activate → Testimonial → Refer → Ascend. (Ch 3)
- **DoD Problem-Solving Process** — ask experts (plural), get all answers, find common threads. (Ch 1)
- **Exit Interview** — talking to a leaving customer before they leave (set as an onboarding expectation); saves ~half of cancellations. The gym-era version of the cancellation call. (Ch 1)
- **The 5 Horsemen of Retention** — the gym answer: track attendance, reach out 2x/week, handwritten cards, member events, exit interviews. (Ch 1)
- **Founder Rate** — an owner/business-owner discount (e.g. 50% off) to convert more people and keep them longer. (Ch 3)
- **Leaky Bucket Business** — one that loses customers as fast as it gets them; "hardly a business at all." (Ch 1)
- **Major Churn Points** — the months customers tend to leave; place unlockables/incentives just past them. (Ch 3)
- **Micro-celebrities** — community members you elevate by publicly praising their specific expertise. (Ch 3)
- **Onboarding** — teaching customers how to hit the activation point. Custom > generic, personal > group, live > recorded, carrots > sticks, some beats none. (Ch 3)
- **Overwhelm** — "the #1 reason for churn"; countered by delivering the core 2–3 things ("less but better"). (Ch 2)
- **Retained %** — the inverse of churn (95% retained = 5% churn). (Ch 2)
- **Save with a Redo** — cancellation save: "Let me give it another shot and I'll make it right." (Ch 3)
- **Save with an Upsell** — cancellation save: credit their payment toward the higher program they should've been in. (Ch 3)
- **Shaking the Three** — the deliberate early churn spike when you start contacting customers (people who'd quit anyway + already-churned-but-still-billed). Expect Month-1 churn up, then down. (Ch 1)
- **Some Beats None** — the most important onboarding rule: doing *something* always lowers churn. (Ch 3)
- **Structural Churn** — high churn from serving VSMBs that go out of business; "that's okay" — still decrease it (Shopify, Skool). (Ch 2)
- **Three Strikes** — the rule for bad customers: correct, then remove after three bad behaviors. (Ch 3)
- **Usage Churn** — still subscribed but no longer using the product; a leading indicator of cancellation. (Ch 3)
- **Value per Second, Not Seconds of Value** — deliver density of value, not volume of stuff. (Ch 2)
- **VSMB** — very small business owner (a high-structural-churn customer type). (Ch 2)
- **Who > What** — serving better customers beats improving the product; same work, far more value on a bigger base. (Ch 3)

# Chapter 1: The 5 Horsemen of Retention

## Core Idea
A "leaky bucket business" — one that loses customers as fast as it gets them — is "hardly a business at all." To fix gym churn, Hormozi interviewed the gym owners with **< 3% month-over-month churn** for the last 6 months ("the churn-reducing experts"), ran a **common factors analysis**, and the 5 factors they all shared became **The 5 Horsemen of Retention**.

## Frameworks Introduced

- **The DoD Problem-Solving Process** (how he *found* the answer): 1) Ask experts (plural) about the problem, 2) Get all their answers, 3) Find common threads ("common factors analysis"). "Talking to people who already know your problem gets you solutions faster than doing it all on your own." Overlap every expert's list; the factors that "pop up each time" are the ones to solve for.
  - When to use: any complex retention/operations problem where practitioners already exist.
  - How: research the people already winning at the metric → interview each on *everything* they do → keep only the common factors.

- **The 5 Horsemen of Retention** (the gym answer — keep all five, in his words):
  1. **Track attendance** — if a member attends ≥3x/week they stick; ≤2x/week they churn. The drop to two sessions is the rescue signal.
  2. **Reach out 2x per week** — praise their participation/progress AND solve their little problems. Do both.
  3. **Handwritten cards** — make them yourself; send on signup, at referral asks (3/6/12-month milestones), holidays, birthdays. "There's never a bad time to send a handwritten card."
  4. **Member events** — on an internal calendar, "regular to you and random to them" (every 21, 42, or 63 days). Handwritten invites. Reduces churn AND gets referrals.
  5. **Exit interviews** — talk to leavers *before* they leave; saves ~half of phone/email cancellations. Set the expectation at onboarding that an exit interview is required; use the cancellation notice as the trigger. "Realistically you'd cut churn by 25% (assuming half show). That's a respectable 33% increase in LTV."

## Key Concepts
- **Leaky bucket business** — get behind by even a couple customers/month and the business dies; "a scary way to live."
- **Common factors analysis** — "Find the stuff they have in common" across many experts to reveal the core problem and eliminate bias.
- **Shaking the three** — the deliberate early churn spike you cause when you start contacting customers (see Anti-patterns / Worked Example).
- **Attendance as a leading indicator** — behavior (sessions/week) predicts cancellation before it happens.

## Mental Models
- Use the **drop-to-two-sessions** trigger as your "reach out now" alarm — intervene at the leading indicator, not after the cancel.
- Think of retention activities as **"regular to you, random to them"** — systematize cadence internally so it feels spontaneous to the customer.
- "It's work" is the moat — the Horsemen "seem obvious. But nobody does it. Because… it's work."

## Anti-patterns
- **Assuming you can out-sell churn**: "I always thought you could make up for one customer leaving by selling two more. And it worked… until it didn't." At 15% monthly churn a gym loses 83% of members in a year.
- **Panicking when churn spikes in Month 1**: that's expected "shaking the three," not failure.
- **Generic/store-bought cards or invites**: the value is that *you made it yourself*.

## Worked Example: "Shaking the Three" and the results curve
When all five Horsemen were rolled out across the gyms:
- **Month 1: churn UP ~50%** (10% → 15%) — "Spooky, but I'll explain."
- **Month 2: churn DOWN ~50%** (15% → 7%)
- **Month 3: churn DOWN another ~50%** (7% → 3%)

Why churn rises first ("shaking the three"): (1) people who would've canceled anyway, and (2) people who already churned *as consumers* but whose automated billing kept running — reminding them prompts the cancel. "All's well that ends well… they made more money, so it ends well." Net result framing: a drop from **9% to 3% churn is a 3.3x increase in lifetime value**. "I have yet to see a business with less than 3% churn that does not make great money." (For the full gym system, he points to his first book, *Gym Launch Secrets*.)

## Key Takeaways
1. Find your retention answers by interviewing the people already winning at retention and keeping only the **common factors**.
2. The 5 Horsemen (gyms): **track attendance, reach out 2x/week, handwritten cards, member events, exit interviews** — keep all five.
3. Behavior is a leading indicator: intervene at the first attendance drop, not at the cancel.
4. Expect churn to **rise first** ("shaking the three"), then fall month over month if you keep doing the work.

# Chapter 2: What Is Churn? — Price, Value, and Why Reducing It Matters

## Core Idea
**Churn = customers who leave over a specific period**, measured against the *original* pool only. Keep the **value customers get greater than the price they pay** and they stick. Reducing churn is the highest-return work in the business because existing customers are the cheapest, warmest buyers you have — and small churn drops compound into huge LTV and profit gains.

## Frameworks Introduced

- **The Churn Formula**: `Churn = people who left ÷ original customer pool`. Example: start 100, end with 95 → 5 left → 5/100 = **5% churn** (you retained 95%).
  - Critical rule: **churn only counts the first-timepoint pool.** New signups during the period don't change it. "You could sign up zero or 1,000 new clients… if you still lost five of the original one hundred, your churn is still five percent."

- **Price ↔ Value ↔ Churn (Warren Buffett frame)**: "Price is what you pay. Value is what you get." Keep value > price two ways: **(1) provide more value** (preferred) or **(2) lower the price**.

- **The "Churn 100% of My Customers" Inversion**: instead of "How do I retain customers?", ask **"What would make my customers leave?"** then do the exact opposite. His churn-everyone list → invert each:
  - Ignore them → **Talk with them**
  - Break promises → **Keep your promises**
  - Miscommunicate → **Communicate clearly**
  - Treat them poorly → **Treat them like royalty**
  - Set unrealistic expectations → **Set realistic expectations**
  - Hide progress/updates → **Give them status updates**
  - Keep them from other happy customers → **Connect them with other happy customers**
  - Make your stuff harder to use → **Make it as easy as possible to get the most value**

- **Provide On-Going Value To Get On-Going Customers**: "Think **value per second, not seconds of value**. Less but better is better than more and decent. **Overwhelm is the #1 reason for churn.**" Retention "comes down to making sure they CONSUME the value, not to overwhelm them with it." Decide on the **core 2–3 things** and make them really good. "There is wisdom in deletion."

## Key Concepts
- **Retained %** — the inverse of churn (95% retained = 5% churn).
- **The best price** — "the most sales at the highest LTV is the best price." Not necessarily the lowest churn.
- **Match model to pricing** — don't "create something recurring out of something that has one-time value." Match the business model to the pricing model.
- **Overwhelm** — the #1 churn driver; combat it by reducing **effort and sacrifice** and shrinking the offer.
- **Structural churn** — serving VSMBs (very small businesses) means customers go out of business; this makes you a high-churn business and "that's okay" — still decrease it (Shopify, Skool both serve small customers at scale).
- **Usage churn** *(named in Ch 3)* — still subscribed but no longer using; a leading indicator of cancellation.

## Mental Models
- Use **inversion**: design retention by listing everything that would make people leave, then doing the opposite.
- Think **"value per second"** — density of value, not volume of stuff.
- When customers **stop consuming, contact them more and more often** — non-consumption is the alarm.
- **Who > What**: improving a $100M business 10% can be "100x the value" of improving a $1M business 10% for the same work — serve better customers.

## Anti-patterns
- **Miscounting churn** by including new signups in the denominator. "People get this twisted. Don't be one."
- **Adding more to reduce churn**: the newsletter founder's lowest churn came from *one* Q&A call/month + *one* physical newsletter; "everything I added increased churn." (Gym Launch is on its 10th version — each shorter and simpler.)
- **Mispricing**: forcing recurring billing onto one-time-value offers.
- **Chasing more customers instead of keeping them**: 3.3x-ing new customers costs far more AND "you accelerate how quickly everyone in your market knows you suck."

## Worked Example: The Price/Churn Table (what "best price" means)
Selling something at $10 and testing price points (clicks held at 100):

| Price | Conv Rate | Sales | Churn | LTV | Total Return | Difference |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| $10 | 5% | 5 | 10% | $100 | $500 | — |
| $20 | 4% | 4 | 10% | $200 | $800 | +60% |
| $100 | 2% | 2 | 33% | $300 | $600 | +20% |

Best price here is **$20**: "If you can double your price and close 20% fewer deals, with all else staying the same… you should do it!" He'd then test **$39–$59** (where churn may stay low and profit peaks). Across Skool, "the higher the price, the higher the churn" — but conversion and churn don't rise *proportionally*, so **test, test, test**.

## Why It Pays (the returns case)
- **3.3x LTV**: cut churn 10% → 3% and a customer stays **33 months instead of 10**.
- **Existing > strangers**: your customers already trust you, got value, gave a card, have no acquisition cost, and are your ideal buyer — "easier to sell (and cost significantly less)."
- **5x–25x cheaper** to retain than acquire — budget **~1/5th of CAC** to keep a customer.
- **Harvard Business Review**: a **5% retention increase → 25%–95% profit increase**. "Monstrous."
- **Only path to compounding growth**: if **referrals > churn, you never stop growing** (10% churn vs 10% referrals = flat; lower either and you grow). Bonus: churn-reducing activities make happier customers who refer more.

## Key Takeaways
1. **Churn = leavers ÷ original pool** — new signups never change it.
2. Keep **value > price**; prefer adding value, and remember **overwhelm is the #1 churn driver** ("less but better").
3. Design retention by **inversion**: list what makes people leave, do the opposite.
4. The math is overwhelming: **3.3x LTV** from 10%→3% churn; **5%→25–95% profit** (HBR); retention is **5–25x** cheaper than acquisition.
5. **Referrals > churn = unstoppable compounding growth.**

# Chapter 2: My Rules For Raising Prices

## Core Idea
"People are petrified of raising prices because they fear losing *some* customers. But if they continue feeding this fear, it leads to losing *all* their customers because their business goes under." Internalize these rules **before** deciding to raise prices. Warren Buffett: *"If you've got the power to raise prices without losing business to a competitor, you've got a very good business. And if you have to have a prayer session before raising the price by a tenth of a cent, then you've got a terrible business."*

## Frameworks Introduced — My Rules For Raising Prices
Each is a standing rule; apply all that fit before a raise.

- **Don't grandfather existing customers. Don't do lifetime deals.**
  - Why: "Value depends on price. If your value goes up, so should your price." Locking in a price limits your future options — "you never know what you're going to want to do (or deliver) in the future." Not worth it.

- **Never sell a "one time price" for lifetime access** — unless it truly costs you $0 to fulfill.
  - Why: "forever lasts a lot longer than what they paid you once." You run out of the money they paid but still owe the commitment → upset customers. "Show me one large company with lifetime access for a one time payment… They don't exist. Model success not failure."

- **It's good to be insecure in the beginning because you probably suck (for now).**
  - How: as the product improves and the brand grows, the price should rise with it. "Don't be the entrepreneur who hates their customers" / resents early adopters paying early-adoption prices.

- **Raise prices at least once per year.**
  - Why: Buffett bought See's Candies and let managers control everything *except one decision — price*, sending product prices yearly. Over 50+ years he raised them ~**10%/year on average, up to 17%** some years. "If he made it the sole decision he was in charge of, you can bet it was probably a good investment of his time."

- **Test price raises on new customers before rolling out to your entire base.**
  - Why: (1) gives you data on whether it actually makes more money; (2) knowing others said yes without hesitation gives you confidence; (3) shows your base "you're not making up this increase in value… the marketplace has already agreed with you."

- **Meet with people if you raise prices more than 50%.**
  - How: talk to them directly (a big raise usually means a *big* early mistake) — **on top of** all the other steps in the RAISE letter (Ch 4).

- **Fear not** (price often matters less than you think).
  - The Shopify-competitor story: a software charged **$30/mo**; some customers earned $1M+/yr from it, but the biggest customers cost the most to fulfill — so the biggest customers **lost the most money**. Choice: fire them or raise prices. The CEO called all **300**, took prices from $30 to **$3,000 on average (a 100x increase)**, and lost exactly **one**. "If people see the value, they will stay."

- **If you can, pair a price raise within 90 days of a launch.**
  - How: best time is the same quarter as a new product/promo; frame as "early adopter pricing" to "draft off the momentum."

- **Do the math.**
  - How: "You should know what percentage of customers you can lose and still make money before raising your prices." If you've already tested the price on new customers, "no matter what, you'll make more money eventually."

## Key Concepts
- **Grandfathering** — keeping old customers permanently on their old (lower) price. Don't.
- **Early-adopter pricing** — framing a same-quarter-as-launch raise so new buyers feel they're getting in early.
- **Break-even loss %** — the share of customers you can lose and still come out ahead; know it before raising.

## Worked Example: Trey's Gym (the $49 trap → triple the price)
**2019.** Trey wanted everyone in his corner of North Carolina to afford fitness, so he priced memberships at **$49/month — "insanely cheap,"** assuming cheap customers would be the most grateful. The result was three problems: (1) "the absolute worst customers I've ever had" — a customer-service nightmare; (2) more customers but still losing money, savings gone; (3) couldn't afford paid ads, paying staff out of dwindling savings — "effed if I do and effed if I don't."

Hormozi's verdict: *"In 12 more weeks, you won't have a gym… It's do or die."* The plan: employees first (negotiate partial rent payments with the landlord, redirect the difference to payroll), then a price-raise plan. They crafted a letter; Trey turned it into an explainer video posted in his gym community **with comments turned off**, referring all questions to himself directly. The message: prices were going up — **not a little, triple** (all members, monthly rates tripled).

A week later Trey **did** lose members — but "not even close" to all. The business went **from bleeding $2,000/month to pocketing $4,000 in profit**, rolled out in about a week. "Don't be like Trey" (i.e., don't wait for disaster) — but his fix proves the playbook works.

## Mental Models
- Use a raise *now*, not when "the universe forces your hand." Distressed businesses just do reactively what healthy ones should do proactively.
- Treat **price as the one lever worth guarding personally** (Buffett at See's) — review it on a calendar, every year.
- Big raise (>50%) → **add** one-on-one conversations; never *replace* the letter steps with a meeting.

## Anti-patterns
- **Grandfathering / lifetime "pay once, deliver forever" deals** — "model success not failure."
- **Resenting early adopters** for paying low early prices instead of just raising the price as you improve.
- **Raising on the whole base before testing on new customers** — you fly blind on both economics and confidence.
- **Waiting until disaster** (the Trey mistake) before doing "stuff that will skyrocket profits in less than a week."

## Key Takeaways
1. Internalize the rules *before* raising: no grandfathering, no lifetime-for-one-payment, raise ≥ once/year, test on new customers first.
2. Buffett's See's model: price is the single decision worth controlling yearly — ~10%/yr, up to 17%.
3. For raises over 50%, meet people one-on-one in addition to the letter.
4. "Do the math": know your tolerable loss % first; if you tested on new customers, you win eventually.
5. Trey: $49 cheap rates bred the worst customers and losses; tripling prices flipped −$2k/mo into +$4k/mo in a week.

# Chapter 1: Why Raising Prices Is A Good Idea

## Core Idea
**"The easiest way to make more money is to charge more for the same stuff."** Most owners price by looking around and charging "somewhere in the middle" — but "the big secret… everyone else is BROKE." Raising prices starts a **virtuous cycle** of money-making; lowering prices (the "race to the bottom") starts a **vicious cycle**.

## Frameworks Introduced

- **The Vicious Price Cycle** — what happens as you DECREASE prices (what MOST businesses do):
  - When to use: recognize you're trapped in it; the cure is to raise, not cut.
  - The customer side: clients' **emotional investment DECREASES** → **perceived value DECREASES** → **results DECREASE** (less investment + lower perceived value) → clients **INCREASE their demandingness** ("the cheapest customers ask for the most") → clients get **LESS service** because you have less money to spend on them.
  - The business side: DECREASES in profit per customer · in perceived self-value · in ability to create results · in conviction in the sales process (you're no longer sure you can deliver) · in gratitude for customers (you feel like you're "giving away the farm").

- **The Virtuous Price Cycle** — what happens as you INCREASE prices:
  - When to use: this is the goal — raise price, then use the money to "make it worth the extra money."
  - The customer side: clients' **emotional investment INCREASES** → **perceived value INCREASES** → **results INCREASE** → clients **DECREASE their demandingness** ("the people who pay you the most are the most easygoing") → clients get **MORE service** because you have more money to spend on them (**race to the top**).
  - The business side: INCREASES in profit per customer · in perceived self-value · in ability to create results (you can reinvest in marketing and help more people) · in level of service per customer · in conviction in the sales process (confident you can deliver and even guarantee it).

## Key Concepts
- **"Poor people thinking"** — refusing to raise prices out of fear of angry messages, dropped sales, or not feeling worth it; long-term it becomes a race to the bottom.
- **Race to the bottom** — you and every other poor business owner competing to have the lowest price. "Avoid."
- **Race to the top** — the opposite: more money per customer reinvested into a better product, marketing, and service.
- **The reinvestment loop** — smart owners use the extra money to make "more and better advertisements, constant product improvements, and pamper their customers… make it worth the extra money. Everyone wins."

## Mental Models
- Think of price as the **input that sets everything else** — emotional investment, perceived value, results, demandingness, and your own conviction all move with it.
- Use a price raise when you want **"a permanent raise"** for yourself and **"a war chest of cash to ward off competitors."**
- Cheap customers are not grateful customers. Expect the *cheapest* rates to attract the *worst* behavior (Trey's lesson, Ch 2).

## Anti-patterns
- **Pricing in the middle of the market**: "everyone else is BROKE. So why copy them?"
- **Being the second-lowest price** (Dan Kennedy): "There's no strategic advantage to being the second lowest price in a marketplace. But there is for being the highest."
- **Cutting price to compete**: triggers the vicious cycle on both the customer and the business side.

## Key Takeaways
1. Charging more for the same thing is the easiest way to make more money — and almost no one does it.
2. Price drives perceived value, results, customer behavior, and your team's conviction; it is not a standalone number.
3. The link "the more you charge the more you make" only works if you **reinvest** the extra into product, marketing, and service.
4. Lowering price = vicious cycle (race to the bottom); raising price = virtuous cycle (race to the top).

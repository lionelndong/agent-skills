# Chapter 4: The Perfect Price Raise Letter (RAISE)

## Core Idea
How you *tell* existing customers determines whether a raise profits or backfires. Hormozi's price-raise letters have **five sections**, remembered with the acronym **RAISE**. Co-developed with Patrick Campbell (ProfitWell), who oversaw 100 price increases directly and analyzed 500+. Then: **"Actually. Send. This. Don't be a chicken."**

## Frameworks Introduced — RAISE (the 5-section letter)

- **R — Remind them of the value you provided them already.**
  - How: open with everything you do and the value they've gotten since becoming a customer. **Personalize as much as possible** — calls attended, deliverables used, revenue generated, profit increase. "Make it about them."

- **A — Address the price change directly.**
  - How: "Rip off the bandaid. Don't waste time. The rest of the letter will soften the blow." Usually a single sentence: *"For us to continue to invest in making [THE PRODUCT THEY USE] for you and the [YOUR COMPANY] team, we need to increase our prices."*

- **I — Invest in their future.**
  - How: frame the raise as value you'll *keep* providing — "this is what we're going to do (and you're gonna get) with the extra money." Use things you **already planned** to do. Pair each investment with **more good stuff** (more aligned with their outcome, faster, easier, risk-free) or **less bad stuff** (misaligned, slower, harder, riskier).
  - Investment categories: hiring better people · training current people · better equipment · better technology/software · facility upgrades · higher level of service.
  - Three rules: (1) **don't say things you won't do** (that's lying); (2) frame all investments **as value for them**; (3) **don't add expenses you didn't plan** — that "negates the benefits of the price raise to begin with."

- **S — Soften the news with a loyalty reward.**
  - How: "People are more 'okay' with a vanishing discount than they are a raise in price." Raise the price now, then **immediately offer a discount that expires 3 to 6 months out**. Bonus: show the **credit on their invoice** so "it almost feels like we're gifting them money/credit now, more than we are raising prices." Tell them they're **special** — new customers pay full price today; loyal ones get a discount today and a raise later.

- **E — Explain away their concerns.**
  - How: take concerns seriously and personally — "this is not the time for 'it's not scalable' talk." End with a **PS statement** offering a safety valve, which is why most won't reply negatively.
    - **B2C PS:** "if this will materially affect your ability to buy groceries, let me know and we'll work something out."
    - **B2B PS:** "if this materially impacts your business, let me know and we'll work something out."

- **The Three Types of People** (who you'll hear from):
  - **Type #1 — People who see the value.** They may not like it (nor should they), but they understand and comply because they get enough value.
  - **Type #2 — People actually affected.** They'll write sob stories; this gives you **optionality** — e.g., "Don't worry, we got you. Why don't we extend your discount another 6 months, then reach out then. Is that fair?"
  - **Type #3 — People who were going to cancel anyway.** Churn spikes month one, drops below normal month two, returns to baseline month three — you simply **"pulled forward" churn** (they cancel a month sooner), so you lose just one month of revenue from a tiny slice. (More in the *Churn Checklist Playbook*.)

## Worked Example: The Assembled Template Letter
> Dear [NAME],
> Over the past 12 months, we've added so much value to [THE PRODUCT/SERVICE YOU USE]:
> • We've made you X · • Feature A helped you do Y · • Feature B is great and you use it daily · • Etc. **(R)**
> For us to continue to invest in making [THE PRODUCT YOU USE] for you and the [CUSTOMER'S COMPANY NAME] team, we need to increase our prices. **(A)**
> With these extra resources, we're going to… • Expand Thing A you love · • Hire people to do B and make X better · • Invest in making this faster / new equipment · • Etc. **(I)**
> You've been insanely loyal to [YOUR COMPANY NAME], using us for the past X months/years. So, as of today we're raising prices on new customers, but since you've been so loyal we're going to keep you on your existing plan for the next [PICK: 3 TO 6 MONTHS]. So we are giving you a [$XXXX] credit as a way of saying thank you (after which we'll bump you up to the new price of $XXX). **(S)**
> Thank you for letting us be a part of the [COMPANY]'s mission. If you have any questions at all, let me know. All replies go directly to me.
> [YOUR NAME — sign in ink if physical mail]
> PS — [B2C] if this will materially affect your ability to buy groceries, let me know and we'll work something out. / [B2B] if this materially impacts your business, let me know and we'll work something out. **(E)**

## Key Concepts
- **Vanishing / expiring discount** — a loyalty credit that drops off in 3–6 months; customers tolerate a disappearing discount better than an announced raise.
- **Stair-step discount** — for a big raise, drop the discount in increments (e.g., "$200/mo off for 4 months, then $100/mo off, then $50/mo off") to "ease customers into their new price" — 2–3 mini jumps.
- **PS statement** — the explicit safety valve that routes anxious customers to you privately instead of to a public complaint thread.
- **Pulled-forward churn** — the month-one churn spike is mostly people who'd have canceled soon anyway.

## Mental Models
- **What to do next:** either **go all the way up** (if unprofitable and you need the move — "It worked for Trey") or do it in **1–3 increments** as a stair step.
- **Communities:** if members congregate somewhere, post a **video** (like Trey) and **turn off comments**; route questions to you via the PS — "You don't want a b*tch fest in the comments."
- **Incoming customers:** get new customers to the new price **immediately** — "They have no context." If they balk ("my friend paid less"): "That may feel like bad news but it's actually good news… we keep making the value better… 96% of our customers agreed… this is the cheapest it'll ever be." **"People moan, but they still buy. Just because someone wants something for less doesn't mean they won't buy it for more."**

## Anti-patterns
- **Burying the price change** — A means *rip off the bandaid* in one sentence; don't dance around it.
- **Promising investments you won't make**, or **adding new expenses** to justify the raise (kills the profit).
- **Leaving comments open** in a community announcement, inviting a public gossip mill.
- **Delaying the new price for incoming customers** — no reason to; they have no prior context.
- **Replacing the loyalty reward with nothing** — the vanishing discount is what makes the raise feel like a gift.

## Key Takeaways
1. Use the **RAISE** order every time: Remind → Address → Invest → Soften → Explain. Keep the letter short.
2. The **S** is the hinge: raise now, give an **expiring 3–6 month discount** (ideally shown as invoice credit).
3. The **PS** safety valve makes most concerns go away and routes the rest to you privately.
4. Expect the **Three Types**; treat Type #2 with discount extensions and read the month-one churn spike as **pulled-forward churn**.
5. Big raise → meet one-on-one and/or **stair-step** the discount; for incoming customers, charge full price now.
6. Then **send it** — "Be bold. You'll survive."

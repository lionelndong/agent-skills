# Patterns — $100M Playbook: Price Raise

## Pattern: Raise the Price (the whole playbook)
**When to use**: Profits are thin, customers are too cheap/demanding, or you simply haven't raised in over a year.
**How**: (1) Decide on the increase. (2) Test on **new customers** first. (3) Segment out "old" (pre-raise) customers. (4) Send the **RAISE** letter to the base. (5) Charge new customers the new price immediately.
**Trade-offs**: You'll lose *some* customers — "but often, not as much as you gain." A wrong move can lose customers and money; a right one gives you "a permanent raise" and a cash war chest.

## Pattern: Pick the Best Price (test the table)
**When to use**: Choosing what to charge or how far to raise.
**How**: Hold clicks constant; map Price → Conv Rate → Sales → Churn → LTV → **Total Return (Sales × LTV)**. Pick the highest Total Return you have data on, then test nearby prices (e.g., between the best two points — Hormozi tested $39–$59 between $20 and $100).
**Trade-offs**: Conversion and churn both worsen as price rises, **not always proportionally**. Higher isn't always better (the $100 point made *less* than $20). Decision rule: double price + lose ≤20% of deals ⇒ do it.

## Pattern: The RAISE Letter
**When to use**: Announcing a raise to existing customers.
**How**: **R**emind (personalized value they've gotten) → **A**ddress (one-sentence bandaid rip) → **I**nvest (already-planned investments framed as their benefit) → **S**often (expiring 3–6 month loyalty discount, shown as invoice credit) → **E**xplain (PS safety-valve statement; all replies to you). Keep it short.
**Trade-offs**: The investments must be real and already planned — don't lie, and don't add new expenses (that negates the profit).

## Pattern: Soften With a Vanishing Discount
**When to use**: Making the raise feel like a gift instead of a loss.
**How**: Raise the price now; immediately give a discount that **expires in 3–6 months**. Tell loyal customers they're special (new customers pay full price today). Bonus: show the credit on their invoice.
**Trade-offs**: "People are more 'okay' with a vanishing discount than a raise in price" — and mind even less when another appears later.

## Pattern: Stair-Step a Big Raise
**When to use**: The needed raise is large (often after a big early mispricing).
**How**: Position the discount as a stair step that drops off in increments (e.g., "$200/mo off for 4 months, then $100/mo off, then $50/mo off") for 2–3 mini jumps. Alternatively, just "go all the way up" if you're unprofitable and need the move.
**Trade-offs**: Easing in lowers backlash; going all the way up gets the cash fastest (worked for Trey).

## Pattern: Meet People on Big Raises (>50%)
**When to use**: Raising prices more than 50%.
**How**: Talk to affected customers one-on-one — **on top of** the RAISE letter, not instead of it. For key customers it truly affects, extend the discount term or make more, smaller jumps.
**Trade-offs**: Costs your time and call volume; only feasible if you can manage the volume.

## Pattern: Handle Incoming Customers
**When to use**: New leads/customers arriving during or after a raise.
**How**: Charge the **new price immediately** — "they have no context." If they balk: reframe ("good news — value keeps improving; 96% of customers agreed; this is the cheapest it'll ever be").
**Trade-offs**: None worth delaying for — "people moan, but they still buy."

## Pattern: Announce in a Community (video, comments off)
**When to use**: Your members congregate in a group/community.
**How**: Make a short explainer video (like Trey), post it, and **turn off comments**; route all questions to you directly via the PS.
**Trade-offs**: Prevents a public "gossip mill"; requires you to field questions privately.

## Pattern: Read the Three Types of People
**When to use**: Triaging replies after the letter lands.
**How**: Type #1 (see the value) → let them comply. Type #2 (actually affected) → extend their discount ("another 6 months, then reach out"). Type #3 (were going to cancel) → expect a month-one churn spike, then below-normal month two, baseline month three = **pulled-forward churn**.
**Trade-offs**: Most concern is noise; only Type #2 needs real accommodation.

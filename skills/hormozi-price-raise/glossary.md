# Glossary — $100M Playbook: Price Raise

**A — Address the price change directly** — The second RAISE section: rip off the bandaid in usually one sentence ("…we need to increase our prices"). (Ch 4)

**Break-even loss %** — The percentage of customers you can lose and still make money; "do the math" and know it before raising. (Ch 2, Ch 3)

**Churn rate** — Share of customers who cancel per period; tends to rise at much higher prices (the $100 jump tripled it to 33%). (Ch 3)

**Conversion rate** — Share of clicks that buy; tends to fall as price rises, but slower than price rises. (Ch 3)

**Don't grandfather** — Rule: never lock existing customers into a permanent old (lower) price; value depends on price. (Ch 2)

**E — Explain away their concerns** — The fifth RAISE section: take concerns seriously, end with a PS safety-valve statement. (Ch 4)

**Early-adopter pricing** — Framing a same-quarter-as-launch raise so new buyers feel they got in early. (Ch 2)

**Expiring (vanishing) discount** — A loyalty credit that drops off in 3–6 months; people tolerate a disappearing discount better than an announced raise. (Ch 4)

**Grandfathering** — Keeping old customers permanently on their old price. Hormozi says don't. (Ch 2)

**I — Invest in their future** — The third RAISE section: frame the raise as value you'll keep delivering; pair each (already-planned) investment with more good stuff / less bad stuff. (Ch 4)

**LTV (lifetime value)** — Per-customer revenue over their lifetime; rises with price even as customer count falls. (Ch 3)

**Loyalty reward** — The expiring discount given to existing customers to soften the raise (the S in RAISE). (Ch 4)

**More good stuff / less bad stuff** — How to pair each investment: more good = more aligned with their outcome, faster, easier, risk-free; less bad = misaligned, slower, harder, riskier. (Ch 4)

**My Rules For Raising Prices** — Hormozi's ground rules to internalize before raising (no grandfathering, raise ≥ once/year, test on new customers, meet if >50%, do the math, etc.). (Ch 2)

**Poor people thinking** — Refusing to raise prices out of fear; long-term it becomes a race to the bottom. (Ch 1)

**PS statement** — The closing line offering a safety valve ("if this materially affects your groceries/business, let me know") that routes anxious customers to you privately. (Ch 4)

**Pulled-forward churn** — The month-one churn spike is mostly people who'd have canceled anyway, choosing to do it a month sooner; churn drops below normal month two, baseline month three. (Ch 4)

**R — Remind them of the value** — The first RAISE section: open with the value they've already received; "make it about them." (Ch 4)

**RAISE** — The five-section price-raise letter: **R**emind, **A**ddress, **I**nvest, **S**often, **E**xplain. (Ch 4)

**Race to the bottom** — Competing to have the lowest price; "avoid." (Ch 1)

**Race to the top** — More money per customer reinvested into better product, marketing, and service. (Ch 1)

**S — Soften the news with a loyalty reward** — The fourth RAISE section: raise now, then immediately offer an expiring 3–6 month discount (ideally shown as invoice credit). (Ch 4)

**Stair-step discount** — For a big raise, drop the discount in increments (e.g., $200 → $100 → $50/mo off) for 2–3 mini jumps to ease customers into the new price. (Ch 4)

**Test on new customers first** — Try the new price on new customers before rolling out to the base; gives data and confidence and proves "the marketplace has already agreed." (Ch 2, Ch 3)

**Total Return** — Sales × LTV from a fixed number of clicks; the number you actually optimize when picking a price. (Ch 3)

**Three Types of People** — Who you hear from after a raise: #1 see the value (comply), #2 actually affected (extend their discount), #3 were going to cancel anyway (pulled-forward churn). (Ch 4)

**Vicious price cycle** — As you decrease prices: emotional investment, perceived value, results, and your conviction all fall; cheap customers get more demanding and worse service. (Ch 1)

**Virtuous price cycle** — As you increase prices: emotional investment, perceived value, results, service, and conviction all rise (the reinvestment loop). (Ch 1)

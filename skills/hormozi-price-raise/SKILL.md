## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

### The one idea
**"The easiest way to make more money is to charge more for the same stuff."** Most owners price in the middle of the market — but "everyone else is BROKE." A price raise loses *some* customers, "but often, not as much as you gain." A wrong raise can sink a business; a right one (like Trey's) is "a miracle." Then: **"Actually. Send. This. Don't be a chicken."**

### Why raise — the two cycles (price is the input)
- **Vicious Price Cycle** (lowering prices / race to the bottom): clients' emotional investment ↓ → perceived value ↓ → results ↓ → demandingness ↑ ("the cheapest customers ask for the most") → service ↓. Business: profit/customer, self-value, ability to get results, and sales conviction all ↓.
- **Virtuous Price Cycle** (raising prices / race to the top): emotional investment ↑ → perceived value ↑ → results ↑ → demandingness ↓ ("the people who pay you the most are the most easygoing") → service ↑. The catch: **reinvest the extra money** into product, marketing, and service to "make it worth the extra money. Everyone wins."

### My Rules For Raising Prices (internalize *before* raising)
- **Don't grandfather** existing customers; **no lifetime-for-one-payment** deals (unless $0 to fulfill) — "value depends on price."
- **Raise at least once a year** — Buffett at See's Candies: price was the *one* decision he controlled, ~10%/yr (up to 17%) for 50+ years.
- **Test on new customers before the whole base** — gives data + confidence, and proves "the marketplace has already agreed."
- **Meet people one-on-one if the raise is >50%** (on top of the letter).
- **Pair a raise within 90 days of a launch** ("early adopter pricing"); **do the math** — know the % of customers you can lose and still profit.
- **Fear not**: a software went $30 → $3,000 (a 100x raise) on all 300 customers and lost exactly **one**. "If people see the value, they will stay."

### How To Pick Your Price — test the table
"The best price is the price that makes you the most money." Hold clicks constant; map **Price → Conv Rate → Sales → Churn → LTV → Total Return (Sales × LTV)**. Both conversion and churn worsen as price rises, **but not proportionally**, so a raise often nets more. Worked example (per 100 clicks): $10 → $500; **$20 → $800 (+60%, the winner of the tested points)**; $100 → $600 (higher isn't always better). **Decision rule: "If you can double your price and close 20% fewer deals… you should do it!"** Then test nearby (Hormozi tried $39–$59).

### The RAISE letter (how to tell existing customers — keep it short)
- **R — Remind** them of the value they've already gotten (personalize: calls attended, deliverables used, revenue/profit generated). "Make it about them."
- **A — Address** the price change directly: "Rip off the bandaid," usually one sentence.
- **I — Invest** in their future: frame the raise as **already-planned** investments (better people, training, equipment, tech, facilities, service), each paired with **more good stuff / less bad stuff**. Rules: don't say things you won't do · frame every investment as *their* value · **don't add new expenses** (that negates the profit).
- **S — Soften** with a loyalty reward: "People are more 'okay' with a vanishing discount than a raise in price." Raise now + an **expiring 3–6 month discount** (ideally shown as invoice credit); tell loyal customers they're special.
- **E — Explain** away concerns: end with a **PS safety valve** ("if this materially affects your groceries/business, let me know and we'll work something out"); "all replies go directly to me."

### After you send — the Three Types of People
- **#1 See the value** → comply (may not like it; fine).
- **#2 Actually affected** → extend their discount ("another 6 months, then reach out").
- **#3 Were going to cancel anyway** → churn spikes month 1, dips below normal month 2, baseline month 3 = **pulled-forward churn** (lose ~1 month from a tiny slice).

### Rollout choices
**Go all the way up** if unprofitable and you need the move (Trey: −$2k/mo → +$4k/mo in a week), or **stair-step** a big raise (drop the discount in increments: "$200 → $100 → $50/mo off"). In a community, post a **video** with **comments off** and route questions to the PS. Get **incoming customers** to the new price **immediately** — "they have no context"; if they balk, "this is the cheapest it'll ever be."

# Cheatsheet — $100M Playbook: Price Raise

## The one line
**"The easiest way to make more money is to charge more for the same stuff."** You'll lose some customers — "but often, not as much as you gain." Then *send the letter*.

## The two cycles (price is the input)
| Lower price → **Vicious cycle** | Raise price → **Virtuous cycle** |
|---|---|
| Emotional investment ↓ · perceived value ↓ · results ↓ | Emotional investment ↑ · perceived value ↑ · results ↑ |
| Customers more demanding, get less service | Customers more easygoing, get more service |
| Profit/customer, self-value, conviction all ↓ (race to bottom) | Profit/customer, self-value, conviction all ↑ (race to top) |

## My Rules For Raising Prices (internalize first)
- **No grandfathering / no lifetime-for-one-payment** (unless $0 to fulfill) — "value depends on price."
- **Raise at least once a year** (Buffett at See's: ~10%/yr, up to 17%).
- **Test on new customers before the whole base** — data + confidence + "the marketplace already agreed."
- **>50% raise → meet people one-on-one** (on top of the letter).
- **Pair a raise within 90 days of a launch** ("early adopter pricing").
- **Do the math** — know the % of customers you can lose and still make money.
- **Fear not** — a $30→$3,000 (100x) raise on 300 customers lost exactly **one**. "If people see the value, they will stay."

## Pick your price — the table (per 100 clicks; optimize Total Return = Sales × LTV)
| Price | Conv | Sales | Churn | LTV | Total | vs. base |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| $10 | 5% | 5 | 10% | $100 | $500 | — |
| **$20** | 4% | 4 | 10% | $200 | **$800** | **+60%** |
| $100 | 2% | 2 | 33% | $300 | $600 | +20% |
**Read:** higher isn't always better — $100 made *less* than $20. Pick the best you have data on ($20), then test nearby ($39–$59). **Decision rule:** *double price + lose ≤20% of deals ⇒ do it.*

## The RAISE letter (5 sections, keep it short)
| | Section | Move |
|---|---|---|
| **R** | Remind | Personalized value they've already gotten — "make it about them" |
| **A** | Address | Rip the bandaid in ~1 sentence: "…we need to increase our prices" |
| **I** | Invest | Already-planned investments framed as their benefit (more good / less bad stuff) |
| **S** | Soften | Raise now + **expiring 3–6 month discount** (show as invoice credit) |
| **E** | Explain | PS safety valve; "all replies go directly to me" |
**Invest rules:** don't promise what you won't do · frame every investment as *their* value · **don't add new expenses** (negates the profit).

## PS statement (the safety valve)
- **B2C:** "if this will materially affect your ability to buy groceries, let me know and we'll work something out."
- **B2B:** "if this materially impacts your business, let me know and we'll work something out."

## The Three Types of People (after the letter)
- **#1 See the value** → comply (may not like it; that's fine).
- **#2 Actually affected** → extend their discount ("another 6 months, then reach out").
- **#3 Were going to cancel** → churn spikes month 1, dips below normal month 2, baseline month 3 = **pulled-forward churn** (lose ~1 month from a tiny slice).

## How big? / How to roll out
- **Go all the way up** if unprofitable and you need the move (worked for Trey: −$2k/mo → +$4k/mo in a week).
- **Stair-step** a big raise: drop the discount in steps ("$200 → $100 → $50/mo off") for 2–3 mini jumps.
- **Community?** Post a **video**, **turn comments off**, route questions to the PS.
- **Incoming customers:** new price **immediately** — "they have no context." Balk? "96% of customers agreed… this is the cheapest it'll ever be." "People moan, but they still buy."

## Price Raise Checklist (do, don't study)
Decide increase → test on new customers → segment out old customers → 1–5 bullets of current value (personalize) → tell them it happens now → 3 bullets of biggest (already-planned) investments → explain how investments benefit them → expiring loyalty discount → "I respond personally" → **sign in ink** → strong PS → do it 1-on-1 if ≥50% → stair-step if it's a lot.

# Chapter 3: Premium Promotions

*Source: "$100M Lost Chapters" — Section A: Attract. Subtitle: "Presenting Your Grand Slam Offer On Its Own."*

## Core Idea
A premium offer presents your Grand Slam Offer on its own at a high ticket. "While everyone buys traffic on their '$6.75/hr' budget, you buy traffic with your '$25,000/hr' budget." You don't need volume — far fewer, far more profitable clients. **The power of infinite returns**: "there is no cap on the upside, but you can only go down to zero when pricing."

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The two prerequisites for premium** — you must: (1) **Have something very valuable to provide** (your Grand Slam Offer), and (2) **Have a selling process that demonstrates that value.**
- **Pros of Premium Offers** (taught as a role-reversed dialogue):
  1. **Simplest math & fewest moving parts** — one core thing: pay $X for Y leads, close Z at $V.
  2. **Only "quality" customers / decreases operational drag** — you "skim … pay a premium for the cream instead of all the milk."
  3. **No discounts or incentives → high lead quality** — buyers come in expecting to be sold; transparent intentions both sides.
- **Cons of Premium Offers**:
  1. **Most expensive cost per lead / can take longer to break even** — less room for error.
  2. **Must be a good copywriter and understand your avatar well** — specificity wins ("not 'working hard,' say 'cleaning the bathrooms yet again'"). Free/discount give margin for error on copy; premium does not.
  3. **Must be good at sales** — it's a "good old fashioned sale."
  4. **Least efficient way to capture a marketplace** — more eyeballs per result.
  5. **Must have an extremely compelling offer of a result** — packed with bonuses, creative guarantees, premium support.

## Key Concepts
- **The power of infinite returns** — price has a floor at $0 and no ceiling; raise value to raise price.
- **Skim the cream** — premium marketing self-selects the most qualified buyers.
- **"If I Lost Everything And Had To Start Over"** — Hormozi would *not* start premium; he'd add a free or discount money model first, then restructure to premium once reputation/results exist.

## Mental Models
- **Premium math (the 9.5x):** Business #1 makes $50 profit/sale; #2 makes $9,500/sale. To match $9,500 profit, #1 must sell 190; #2 sells one. Offer those same 190 the $10,000 thing and close ~5%: 9.5 sales × $9,500 = $90,250 vs. $9,500 → **9.5x as profitable**, with ~10 clients instead of 190.
- Premium works well as the **"second" offer** after a free offer — *only* if the first offer demonstrated value.

## Anti-patterns
- **Leading premium with no cash and no proven process** — high cost per lead + no room for error = you run out of money learning.
- **Running a premium offer at "free-offer prices"** — see Ch 4's "Free Makes More Money": match the whole structure to one strategy.

## Worked Example: The first premium offer
Hormozi's offer after his original free offers: *"Apply & Book a call to see if you qualify for our services."* "Not. Attractive. At. All." — but the copy *before* the offer filtered for gym owners who understood it was expensive. Conclusion: with existing client flow, restructuring to premium can **3–5x your prices without changing your services**; if you're early or under-volumed, wrap premium in a free/discount front end first.

## Key Takeaways
1. Premium = fewest clients, highest profit per client, simplest math — but demands a great offer, great copy, great sales, and cash/patience.
2. Use the 9.5x logic: a high-value offer can be ~9–10x more profitable even at far lower volume.
3. New/under-volumed? Don't lead premium — wrap it in free/discount, prove results, then go premium.

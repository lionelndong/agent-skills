# Chapter 5: Discount Promotions

*Source: "$100M Lost Chapters" — Section A: Attract. Epigraph: "A discount turns wants into haves. Why pay more when you could pay less?"*

## Core Idea
"Free" and "discount" work similarly — both create a **perceived value discrepancy** that drives action. But skip "marginal" discounts (5–25% — just cuts margin); use **massive discounts (50%+)**, the kind that move "a population that wouldn't otherwise act." Most discount offers are a *piece* of the thing, not the whole thing.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Four Ways To Display Discounts** (same promotion, different feel — test all four; same $30/day bundle → first week $29 instead of $210):
  1. **Percentage Off** — "87% off first visit."
  2. **Absolute Amount Off** — "$181 Off First Week (Normally $210)."
  3. **Relative Equivalent Off** — "Save A Steak Dinner" (negative) or "Less Than Going Out To Lunch" (positive).
  4. **Simply the Discounted Price** — "$29 New Client Special."
- **Pros of Discount Offers**: (1) **Lots of cheap leads within the law** (where "free" with stipulations is forbidden); (2) **You actually collect some money** (helps liquidate acquisition cost — but never *the* way you fund marketing); (3) **People come in expecting to spend** (mostly a mental benefit for the team); (4) **The Two-Step Sale** (probably the biggest benefit — collect a card, eliminate no-shows); (5) **Discounts make upsells smooth** (card on file).
- **Cons of Discount Offers**: (1) **Giving away the farm** — never discount your *core* offer habitually (trains buyers to wait); "splinter" the offer and discount only a sliver; (2) **Bargain hoppers** — the Groupon problem; fix with intelligent offer structure (see Ch 7). "We shouldn't see people as customers if they buy the discount. We should see them as qualified leads."

## Key Concepts
- **The Two-Step Sale** — discounted front end → card on file → upsell the core offer (the "second sale," where the money is). Example flow: *Ad → Opt in → Phone call for $19 promo, set appt → Shows up → Gets value → Schedules $2,100 program → Shows up & is sold.*
- **Splinter the offer** — give a core *component* at a discount, not the whole farm.
- **It's easier to upsell than to sell** ("foot in the door") — any small purchase raises the odds of the next.

## Mental Models
- **Use absolute prices for well-understood products** — discounts work when people already know the cost (dentists, chiropractors, gyms, haircuts). "50% off agency retainer" fails (no reference price).
- **Add steps as price and complexity increase** — the bigger the plane, the longer the runway; spend more time before asking for the sale.
- People will **show up for things they pay for** (85–90%+) — discounts nearly eliminate no-shows.

## Anti-patterns
- **Marginal discounts (5–25%)** — not enough to change behavior; just margin loss.
- **Habitually discounting the core offer** — trains customers to only buy on sale.
- **Treating discount buyers as the customer** — they're *qualified leads* for the real (second) sale.

## Worked Example: The Proprietary 5-Minute Appointment Method
To avoid burning expensive expert time on unqualified leads, fix the first visit so a front-desk admin handles it (paperwork, X-rays, financing pre-approval). The expert (e.g., a doctor) pops in for 5 minutes to say hello and set the in-depth follow-up where the treatment plan is sold. This eliminates no-show cost, pre-qualifies everyone, and — by opening many more appointment slots — "improves the percentage of people who schedule more than just about anything else." (Hormozi: "feel free to swipe it.")

## Key Takeaways
1. Use big discounts (50%+) on a *splinter* of the offer, displayed four ways and tested.
2. The two-step sale (card on file → upsell) is the main reason to use discounts; it kills no-shows.
3. Discounts shine for well-understood services; treat buyers as qualified leads, not customers.

# Chapter 10: Section D — Expanded Employees Chapter (incl. Maker or Manager?)

*Source: "$100M Lost Chapters" — Section D. An expanded cut of the "Employees" chapter from $100M Leads ("twice the length of the final chapter"), plus the standalone "Maker or Manager?" chapter. Epigraph: "If you want to go fast, go alone. If you want to go far, go together."*

## Core Idea
To advertise more, you need more workers. **Getting employees is the same as getting customers** — "you have two types of customers: the customers who pay you and the customers you pay." Once you can reliably get people who get you leads (running the Core Four on your behalf), "you just need to do more." And: **"You get rich from what you make. You become wealthy from what you own"** — a business that runs without you becomes a sellable asset.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The Five Lead Stages**: `Uncontacted → Contacted → Engaged → Qualified → Sold`. Each stage is a reaction to something your business does: advertise → incentivize engagement → qualify (problems + money) → make an offer → collect money.
- **The Internal Core Four** — the $100M Leads Core Four, applied to hiring:
  - Warm Outreach → Asking Your Network · Cold Outreach → Recruiting · Post Content → Posting Job Openings · Paid Ads → Promoting Job Postings · Customer Referrals → Employee Referrals · Affiliates → Associations/Guilds/Listservs · Agencies → Staffing firms · Employees → Employees. "When you need more, you do more."
- **The 3Ds training model** — `Document → Demonstrate → Duplicate`:
  1. **Document** — write a checklist of the steps exactly as you do them (record yourself; can you do an A+ job following only your directions?).
  2. **Demonstrate** — do it in front of them, step by step; adjust the checklist where they slow you down.
  3. **Duplicate** — they do it in front of you following the checklist; fix the checklist until the outcome matches. "If you vanished tomorrow, could a stranger get your results following only your checklist?"
- **The Performance Diamond** — four reasons performance drops, with fixes:
  1. **Communication** — they don't know *THAT* you want it ("I didn't know you wanted me to do that"). You're the chief accountability officer; have them describe their job/priorities back.
  2. **Training** — they don't know *HOW* ("I don't know how to do that"). Don't ask "do you understand?" (they say yes from fear) — have them *show* you.
  3. **Motivation** — they don't *WANT* to. Three levers: reward (wrong time/frequency/intensity/source), aversion (work/environment/leadership/coworkers/customers), or outside life events (positive, negative, lifestyle).
  4. **Circumstances** — *something is stopping them* ("I can't — we're out of beef / my internet's too slow / the phone broke"). Owners expect employees to solve problems the owner should have prevented.

## Key Concepts
- **Maker or Manager?** (separate chapter; expands Paul Graham's essay) — two calendar styles:
  - **Managers** split the day into 10–20 small 30–60 min blocks; "an empty time slot is a lost opportunity"; objective: use up all the chunks.
  - **Makers** need large half/full-day blocks (only 1–2 a day) and work "open to goal" until quality drops; objective: **maximize blank calendar space**. A short meeting "costs makers 10x more" — it breaks one usable work unit in two unusable halves. The **Zeigarnik effect** (remembering open loops) makes a pending meeting eat a maker's whole morning.
- **The 3 reasons people don't want to do things** — reward problem, aversion, outside events (the Motivation lever above).
- **Competence vs. performance** — they can know exactly what to do and still not be good yet; that just needs practice ("slow then smooth then fast").

## Mental Models
- **You make wealth from what you own, not just what you make** — a $2M-profit business that runs without you can be worth $10M+ (an asset, not a job).
- **Replace yourself as soon as you can** — "everyone is replaceable, especially me." The old beliefs ("nobody can do it but me," "if you want it done right, do it yourself") are false and never made him money. New beliefs: "If you want it done right, get someone to spend all their time doing it"; "If I can do it, someone else can do it better."
- **Trade hours of doing for hours of managing** — 40 hours doing → 4 hours managing = 36 fewer hours, repeatable up the chain.
- **Diagnose CAC problems with one question**: *"Do my engaged leads have the problem I solve and the money to spend?"* No → advertising problem (unqualified). Yes but too few → advertising problem. Yes but not buying → **sales problem.** "Don't fire your sales guy if you've got advertising problems."

## Anti-patterns
- **Competing with your own employees** — "the more I tried to outcompete my employees, the more distracted I became, and the worse my business got."
- **Punishing mistakes during training** — "reward the good stuff … learning a new skill is punishing enough."
- **Fixing multiple steps at once** — give one piece of feedback at a time until it's right, then move on.
- **A manager keeping tabs on a slipping maker** — "the worse the maker's performance gets, the more the manager tries to keep tabs … lose-lose"; the manager *prevents* the work they check on.

## Worked Example: The hiring-bottleneck meeting
A sales team missed its cold-outreach goal two quarters running. Walking the funnel: reps churn every ~4 weeks; hire 1 of 4 candidates; HR sends ~1 candidate/week; ~1 qualified per 10 screening interviews → ~40 interviews per low-skill frontline hire. **The bottleneck was the one-on-one screening**, not the outreach method or offer. Fix: interview in groups, screen out "crazies," push everyone with work ethic + basic social skills to sales ("we can teach the rest"). Within six weeks hiring outpaced churn; cold-outreach sales doubled and became >half of total sales.

*(Returns math: `Total Payroll / Total Engaged Leads = cost per engaged lead`; × leads-per-customer = CAC; LTGP / CAC = the ratio. Aim CAC within 3x industry average, then raise LTGP. Pro Tip — Leila's feedback line: "Over the last [time] your [performance] has changed from the norm. What do you think has gotten in the way and how can I help?")*

## Key Takeaways
1. Getting employees = getting customers: same Five Lead Stages, same (Internal) Core Four — "when you need more, you do more."
2. Train with the **3Ds** (Document → Demonstrate → Duplicate); a stranger should get your results from the checklist alone.
3. Diagnose dropped performance with the **Performance Diamond** (Communication, Training, Motivation, Circumstances) — usually it's something *you* missed.
4. Protect makers' large time blocks; a "got a min?" can cost a maker a whole work unit.

# Chapter 1: Your First Avatar

*Source: "$100M Lost Chapters" — Intro Chapter (also published standalone as "The Lost Chapter: Your First Avatar," 2021). The Lost Chapters (2025) version adds the Pro Tips below.*

## Core Idea
Choosing the perfect avatar is a subset of picking the right market: you get nuanced about exactly who you serve — **and who you do not.** Survey your customers, find the top 20% (those you like most, who spent most, who stayed longest), find the fewest qualifiers they share, then speak only to them and re-engineer the buying process your best customers went through. "You make more because of who they are, not because of who you are."

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The Four-Step Avatar Process** (Hormozi uses it "with every company I advise"):
  1. **Survey your customers** — ask Demographics, Business Stats (before & current), Aspirations, and Buying Process. Make them prove completion to get a benefit.
  2. **Find your biggest spenders** — sort by who you like most / spent most / stayed longest; focus on the top 20%, ignore the rest.
  3. **See what they have in common** — read every answer and think; come up with the *fewest qualifiers they all share* (usually three to five). "Your competitors won't do it — easy advantage."
  4. **Execute** — (a) **Speak your new avatar**: be up front about customer requirements; get all advertising to speak directly to them; stop selling anyone who doesn't meet the ideal; (b) **Re-engineer the sales process**: reverse-engineer the buying process your best customers went through, then make it happen on purpose.
- **The Vista / VAM origin** — private-equity method: score customers by who stays longest and pays most, cut channels bringing low-value customers, double down on channels bringing the best. "Pareto's principle (80/20) on steroids" — replace the 80% with high spenders → grow ~5x. Became a pillar of Acquisition.com's **Value Acceleration Method (VAM)**.

## Key Concepts
- **The fewest qualifiers** — the minimum set of shared traits (3–5) that define your best customer.
- **Quality > Quantity** — every time they removed qualification steps, lead volume rose but they made *less* money. Merging marketing + sales into one **acquisition department** fixed the infighting.
- **Force multiplier** — getting the avatar right multiplies the value of everything else in the book; it raises both lead *quality* and *quantity* and average customer value.

## Mental Models
- Think of the ideal **buyer journey** as something to engineer, not a widget to spam. "Knowing your ideal buyer journey forces patience."
- Prefer a bigger, slower-but-richer bet: "I would rather pay $5,000 to acquire $45,000 than pay $1,000 to acquire $5,000."
- **Start with what you know** (no customers yet): pick the industry you know most about (friends, family, past jobs), create a narrow target, serve them first, re-run the analysis later.

## Anti-patterns
- **Accepting "anyone with a pulse and a credit card"** — drives high churn, high CAC, low retention, generic advice, lower satisfaction (the company making 70x less profit on the same number of sales).
- **Panicking and cutting qualification steps** to chase volume — increases leads, decreases money.

## Worked Example: Gym Launch's redefined avatar
After running steps 1–3 on Gym Launch customers, the top-20% profile was: **Demographics** — right-leaning/conservative, married, 25–45, male, gym owner, US-based; **Business Requirements** — signed lease, 1+ employees, $10,000+/mo revenue minimum at start, 30+ existing clients; **Aspirations** — $1M+ gym, work less, open more locations; **Buying Reasons** — not enough leads, "bad market," bad pricing, can't find good employees. On the buying side: **78% of top customers had consumed at least TWO pieces of long-form content before buying.** So they injected two long-form value pieces into every lead's journey and armed the sales team with a hand-picked "greatest hits" list — forcing strangers through the same buying process that produced the best customers. Result vs. a competitor: average competitor LTGP ~$6,000–$8,000; theirs north of **$45,000**.

## Pro Tips (added in the 2025 Lost Chapters version)
- **You Make More Because of Who They Are, Not Who You Are** — same work, more valuable customer = more value you can charge for (the 5%→7% sales-page lift is worth far more to a $10M/mo client than a $100k/mo one).
- **Sell To People Who Don't Stop Buying** — either improve the product so everyone keeps buying, or only sell to customers who historically keep buying. Enterprise clients get higher multiples partly because they keep buying.

## Key Takeaways
1. Survey → sort to top 20% → find the fewest shared qualifiers → speak to them and reverse-engineer their buying process.
2. Repel bad customers on purpose; it raises quality, quantity, and average value at once.
3. You make more because of *who the customer is* — sell to the highest-value people and stop selling everyone else.

# Section II: The Ad Assembly Process & Writing Hooks

## Core Idea
Don't *create* ads — **assemble** them. Mass production is easier in parts than whole: *"I wasn't really creating ads at all. I was assembling them."* Build a stock of **hooks**, **meat**, and **CTAs** separately, then combine. The hook earns ~80% of the effort because *"if someone doesn't make it through the hook, then nothing else matters."*

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The Ad Assembly Process / Assembly Line** — chunk ads into **Hook → Meat → CTA**, made in parts.
- **The Ad Equation** — `50 hooks × 3–5 meat × 1–3 CTAs = 150 to 750 ads per week`.
- **The Prep Split** — 80% Hooks / 20% Meat / ~0% CTAs.
- **Two Hook Types** — **Winner Hooks** (for immediate results) and **Expansion Hooks** (to enter new markets).
- **The 5 Hook Sources** — where to find high-converting hooks.

## Key Concepts

### The Ad Equation (assembly math)
Make the parts in bulk, in one session: **50 hooks**, **3–5 well-thought-out meat scripts**, **1–3 CTAs**. Combined: **150 to 750 ads per week**. Time cost is small — *"an hour or two to make the hooks, maybe an hour to record three to five 'meats' and five minutes to record a few CTAs"* — because the bulk of the work is **prep**, not recording.

### The Prep Split (where the time goes)
*"Six-Pack-Abs are made in the kitchen, not in the gym. And like six-packs, ads are made in research, not in recording."* Ninety percent of advertising is preparation. Allocate prep:
- **80% → Hooks**
- **20% → Meat**
- **~0% → CTAs**

### Two Hook Types
1. **Winner Hooks (immediate results)** — bread-and-butter hooks written off **previous winners**, made weekly. *"You'd be amazed how much runway one good hook can give you."*
2. **Expansion Hooks (new markets)** — written when you've **"capped"** your existing ad capacity (can't spend more profitably even after refreshing creative). You expand the **audience** by meeting people at their level of awareness.

> *"Once you get ads converting, it's about doing more/better far more than about doing something new."* Only write expansion hooks when you genuinely need a broader audience.

### The 5 Hook Sources (in order Hormozi uses them)
1. **Winning Hooks From Your Previous Ads** — literal past winners, reused. *(Skip if it's your first time running ads.)*
2. **Winning Hooks From Your Free Content** — chop a hook that worked on one platform (longs, shorts, tweets, emails) and use it in an ad. *Highest likelihood of converting.*
3. **Winning Hooks From Other People's Ads** — save ads you like; write down their hooks; reuse the broad ones. (He keeps a phone album of them.)
4. **Winning Hooks From Other People's Free Content** — find content with tons of views in your space; snag the hook.
5. **Platform-Specific Ad Libraries** — **last** place he looks. Pro: see endless ads from any business. Con: you can't tell which ones actually perform, so libraries are better for *ideas* than guaranteed winners. **Key point: winning hooks in one industry often work in others.**

### Expansion Hooks by Level of Awareness
If hooks focus only on your offer/proof, you convert a small slice of a large audience. To grab a bigger slice, **meet the audience where they're at**. The hook *type* maps to the awareness level:

| Awareness | Hook is typically… | Template |
|---|---|---|
| **Most Aware** | **offer**-driven | "50% off our best-selling XYZ product for the rest of the month." |
| **Product-Aware** | **proof**-driven | "Discover why 10,000 people chose XYZ to solve their [problem] last month" |
| **Solution-Aware** | **promise**-driven | "The fastest way to [achieve desired result] - introducing XYZ" |
| **Problem-Aware** | **pain**-driven | "Tired of [specific problem]? There's a better way" |
| **Completely Unaware** | **curiosity**-driven | "The hidden danger in your daily routine that's costing you [money/time/health]" |

### Pro Tip — The Widest Hooks Possible
**Memes or meme-like content** attract the largest percentage of your audience — *"A relevant meme for a specific audience works like a moth to a flame."* Narrower groups have their own culture-specific memes; advertise with those. *"If 90% of your hooks land in the 'Most aware' bucket, spread them out to capture a bigger slice. When in doubt, go a little broader."* You still catch the warm audience and attract some colder audience too.

### Frameworks, not magic recipes
National offer-driven hooks (e.g. the **$5 Foot Long** by Subway, run ~20 years) work only because the brand and product are already universally known — they've spent hundreds of millions on awareness, so they *"just need to make an offer."* A no-name with a product that needs education leading with an offer-driven hook to a national audience is *"the closest thing to burning money on fire."* Treat the awareness map as a set of tools, not stone.

## Worked Example: The Weight-Loss Shake "XFast" (B2C)
Same templates, translated into one B2C business — a weight-loss shake called **XFast**:
1. **Most Aware:** "XFast's new formula: Now with 25% more protein - Same great taste!"
2. **Product-Aware:** "Why 9 out of 10 XFast users reached their goal weight within 3 months"
3. **Solution-Aware:** "Lose 15 pounds in 30 days with our scientifically proven meal replacement system"
4. **Problem-Aware:** "Frustrated with crash diets that don't last? There's a sustainable way to shed pounds"
5. **Completely Unaware:** "The hidden hormonal imbalance that's making 1 in 3 Americans gain weight"

*(He gives a parallel B2B set for a marketing agency, "DigitalBoost," following the exact same Most-Aware→Unaware ladder.)*

## Mental Models
- **Assemble, don't create** — separate the parts so you can mass-produce combinations.
- **The hook is the gate** — 80% of effort, because nothing past it matters if the hook fails.
- **Cross-industry hooks travel** — a winner in one industry often wins in another.
- **Go a little broader when in doubt** — broader hooks still catch the warm audience.

## Anti-patterns
- Pouring effort into the meat or CTA while under-investing in hooks.
- Starting from platform ad libraries (you can't tell winners from losers there).
- Leading with an offer-driven hook to a cold, national audience as an unknown brand.
- Writing only offer/proof hooks and wondering why you can't reach a bigger audience.
- Letting 90% of hooks pile up in "Most Aware."

## Key Takeaways
1. Chunk every ad into **Hook → Meat → CTA** and produce the parts in bulk: 50 × 3–5 × 1–3 = **150–750 ads/week**.
2. Spend **80%** of prep on hooks; write off **previous winners** first, **expansion hooks** only when you need a broader audience.
3. Pull hooks from **5 sources** (your ads → your content → others' ads → others' content → ad libraries last).
4. Match hook type to awareness: **offer / proof / promise / pain / curiosity**; broaden with memes when in doubt.

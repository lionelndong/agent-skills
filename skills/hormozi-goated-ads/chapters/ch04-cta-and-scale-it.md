# Section IV: CTA (Call To Action) & Scale It

## Core Idea
The CTA tells the audience exactly what to do next while their motivation is high but brief: *"If your ad got them interested, then your audience will have huge motivation… for a tiny time. Take advantage."* The guiding rule is **Clear > Clever**. CTAs come **last** and take the least effort — *"a sound CTA's hasn't ever broken a campaign, but no CTA has."* Then you **scale** by making many variations and doubling down on winners.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Clear > Clever** — the CTA design principle.
- **Show AND Tell** — don't just tell them the next step; demonstrate it.
- **The 5 CTA Elements** — what a good CTA shows or tells.
- **The CTA Test** — how to A/B a CTA (change only the CTA).
- **The Scaling Loop** — make variations → find winners → double down → reuse hooks.

## Key Concepts

### Clear > Clever — spell it out
Tell them exactly what to do: *"Click this button. Call this number. Reply with 'YES.' Go to this website. Scan this QR Code."* So many ads still don't do this. *"Your audience can only know what to do if you tell them."* More people do what you want, more often, when you tell them to.

### Show AND Tell
Take it up a level: don't only *say* the next step — **demonstrate** it. Show what happens when they click the button, where they'll fill out their information, walk through clicking submit on the site or calling the number. When they click and *"get exactly what they expected,"* they follow through — *"the ultimate congruence."* Basic and obvious, yet almost no one does it, and *"it really works."*

### The 5 CTA Elements
A good CTA shows or tells: **(1) what to do, (2) how to do it, (3) when to do it, (4) what they get for doing it, (5) what happens next** *(optional — more important with lead magnets and multi-step sales processes)*. Worked sentence:
- **What to do:** "Take advantage of this great offer by"
- **How to do it:** "tapping the button on the bottom of your screen…"
- **When to do it:** "…before it expires…"
- **What they get:** "…and you'll get $1000 of free stuff."
- **What happens next (optional):** "…delivered straight to your inbox."

### The CTA Test
Make **1–3 CTAs per recording session**. To find a winner: *"run three identical hook + ad meat combinations, to the same audience, only changing the CTA."* Example for a 14-day free trial — three CTAs at the end of the same ad:
1. "Start free on the next page"
2. "Grab your 14-day free trial on the next page"
3. "Get started on the next page free."

Once you have a clear one that follows the steps, **stick with it.** You spend the least time here.

### Layer on persuasion tactics
Beyond the basics, apply **urgency, scarcity, guarantees, and bonuses** to get even more people to act — *"they apply here, and everywhere else you tell your audience to do something."*

### Scale It — the loop
*"New customers enter your market everyday… don't get bored repeating the same stuff. It'll still be the first time they see it."* Early on you can't know which ads will hit, so **make a lot of variations** to appeal to different market segments. A few will wildly outperform — **double down on what works** and **reuse the same winning hooks to make even more winners.** *"They're the key to unlocking your customer's wallet. This is what allows you to scale."*

> **The doubling logic:** *"If you double your leads, you double your business. The easiest way to double the amount of leads you get is better advertising. And the easiest way to double advertising, is to make more, better ads."*

## Worked Example: CTAs That "Show What Happens Next"
The playbook points to real ads where the CTA demonstrates the next step:
- **Gym Launch ad** — the CTA begins at **3:04** and shows what happens next (today he'd swipe through each screen live on a digital board).
- **Skool ad** — the **last 5 seconds** contain the CTA and what happens next.
- **Urgency bonus** — an ad that *"crushed,"* whose CTA is the last 5 seconds and brings the urgency.

## Mental Models
- **Clear beats clever** — confusion kills action; spell out the next step.
- **Show, don't just tell** — demonstrating the click creates congruence and follow-through.
- **CTAs are cheap insurance** — they never break a campaign; having none does.
- **Variation then concentration** — fan out many ads, then double down on the few winners.
- **Winners breed winners** — reuse winning hooks to manufacture more winners.

## Anti-patterns
- Clever, vague CTAs that don't say exactly what to do.
- Running no CTA (or assuming the audience will figure it out).
- Over-testing CTAs (it's the lowest-leverage chunk).
- Getting "bored" of a winning ad and killing it — new customers see it for the first time.
- Spreading spend evenly instead of doubling down on proven winners.

## Key Takeaways
1. **Clear > Clever:** spell out the next step, then **show AND tell** it.
2. A good CTA covers **what / how / when / what-they-get / what-happens-next**; make **1–3** and test by changing only the CTA.
3. **Scale** by making many variations, doubling down on winners, and **reusing winning hooks** to make more.
4. Double your leads → double your business; the lever is **more, better ads**.

# Chapter 8: Price-Profit Levers

## Core Idea
**"The easiest way to make more money is to simply charge more for the same level of service."** Higher prices trigger a **virtuous cycle**; lower prices a **vicious** one. Use the **Buying Curve** to price by *type of buyer*, avoid **No Man's Land**, and take billing **off monthly**.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The Big Secret**: "Everyone else is BROKE. So why copy them?" When most owners set prices, they average what competitors charge — but those competitors are broke. **"If you only do one thing after reading this book, RAISE YOUR PRICES."**
- **Virtuous vs. Vicious Price Cycle**: As you **decrease** price → emotional investment ↓, perceived value ↓, results ↓, demandingness ↑, service ↓ (race to bottom); business profit ↓, self-value ↓, sales conviction ↓. As you **increase** price → all of those reverse (race to the top). "Ever notice how the cheapest customers ask for the most?… the people who pay you the most are the most easygoing?"
- **Three Price Micro Levers**: #1 **Increase Profit Per Member** (charge more) · #2 **Membership Levels** (tiered by buyer type) · #3 **Billing Cycle** (off monthly).
- **The Buying Curve ("watermelon curve")**: price by **what the client is willing to pay**, not what the market charges or what it costs to fulfill. Four fulfillment zones:
  - **Low Price/Low Service ($0–$99)**: globo gyms; never compete here.
  - **Large Group (16+)**: $167–$225/mo (target ~$199). Avoid the $99–$167 zone.
  - **Medium Group (5–15) = No Man's Land**: $250–$399; **avoid at all costs**.
  - **Small Group / Semi-Private (1-on-1 to 1-on-4)**: $500–$700/mo (priced like personal training, 4x revenue per slot).
- **No Man's Land** (5–15 people, $250–$399): too high for the lower buying pocket (constant billing issues, drop-ins/outs) and too low to deliver high service — "the client is dissatisfied, struggling to pay for mediocre service." **Solution: split into two tiers (large group + small group).**
- **The Two-Tier Model + 80/20**: Lower tier $167–$225 (normal person/normal pain OR wealthy/low pain), Higher tier $500–$700 (high pain/need OR wealthy). 20% of customers will pay 3x → "add 60% to your business while serving one-fifth of them." Implementing two tiers → **3x–5x profit within a few months** and lower attrition.

## Key Concepts
- **Pain → Price quadrant** (income × pain/need): $199 pocket = wealthy/low-pain or average/medium-pain; $499+ pocket = wealthy/high-pain or average/high-pain.
- **Keep It Simple** — three reasons: easy for the customer ("a confused mind doesn't make wise decisions"; **never more than 3 membership levels**), easy for employees, and **target levels by type of buyer, not type of service**.
- **Billing Cycle problems with monthly**: 1) **Inflow vs. Outflow Inequality** — you pay payroll every 14 days (26 cycles = 13 "months") but collect 12 monthly inflows → one extra month of payroll out of pocket. 2) **Low Gross Margin** — industry avg ~12%/year. 3) **Devil Is In The Decimals** — there are **4.33 weeks per month**, so weekly billing pushes ~8% more revenue.
- **Sellability**: smaller time units sound cheaper ("less than $3.31 per day"). $49/week feels cheaper than $211/month, even though it's MORE. "You can shear a sheep for a lifetime, but you can only skin it once."
- **The combo move**: advertise rates **weekly** ($39–$49/wk) but bill on a **28-day cycle** → equal inflow/outflow, weekly sellability, same admin work as monthly, +8% gross revenue.

## Worked Example: Kirk Huggins Tripled Prices
Kirk had memberships as low as $49/month (and those people were his biggest PIAs). Couldn't make payroll. They sent a letter/text/video/email explaining the increase, settled at **$150/month — a tripling** in one of the country's most depressed economies, and pulled **every macro and micro lever at once** ("Cut once, cut deep"). In **one week** Kirk went from **losing $2,000/month to netting $4,000 profit.**

## Mental Models
- "Charge more so you can help more." Low prices leave no room to give amazing experiences; high prices fund the service that gets results (and the marketing to reach people).
- "You're going to have hard conversations either way — with your spouse/landlord about not making rent, or with customers about higher rates. Only one makes you more money."
- Buying curves are like accommodating strength curves: match your "resistance" (price) to the prospect's ability to spend.

## Anti-patterns
- Pricing by averaging competitors (who are broke) or by cost-to-fulfill.
- Living in No Man's Land (5–15 per session, $250–$399).
- Offering >3 membership levels or pricing by service instead of buyer type ("everyone winds up on a weird custom plan only the person who signed them up remembers").
- Billing monthly (loses the extra payroll month and the 8% sellability gain).

## Key Takeaways
1. Raise prices — the single highest-leverage move in the book; it starts the virtuous cycle.
2. Price by buyer type in two tiers ($167–$225 large group, $500–$700 semi-private); kill No Man's Land.
3. Sell in weekly amounts, bill on 28-day cycles — ~8% more revenue for one config change.

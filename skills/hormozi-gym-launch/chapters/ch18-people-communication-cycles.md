# Chapter 18: PEOPLE — Communication Cycles And Cadences

## Core Idea
"Business [is] a garden rather than a machine." The **communication cycle is the water/irrigation system** — three rigid, consistent cadences keep the team (and business) growing. "If you don't have a defined communication cycle, you are leaving your growth to chance."

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The Three Communication Cycles**: 1) **Daily Huddles** · 2) **Weekly Team Meetings** · 3) **Weekly One-on-Ones.** "Join the top 5% of businesses that actually do them." (Over time the operations manager runs these; the owner "rides shotgun.")
- **Daily Huddles**: ≤10 minutes. Cover the previous day's numbers (sales, cancels), missed sessions/attendance, client wins/losses, quick announcements, finish with "a little spicy motivation and appreciation." "The lifeblood of the business" — spot anything "off" and fix it before it festers.
- **Weekly Team Meetings**: "incredibly scripted," each person a defined time slot, covering last week's goals, this week's goals, and what they need. Sample agenda: trainers (community vibe, wins/losses, red-flagged members) → challenge manager (challengers' progress) → operations manager (numbers, billing, pull-asides, supplement goals) → owner (new internal promotion, content schedule, card follow-ups, praise). **End early if done** — "everyone loves meetings that end early."
- **Weekly One-on-Ones**: ~30 minutes. "To grow a business, you GROW PEOPLE." **YOU talk about nothing** — listen and ask leading questions so *they* figure it out (otherwise you never scale out of the role). The three questions: **"What's going well? What's not going well? How can I help you win more?"**

## Key Concepts
- **Clear Outcomes = Motivated Employees**: if someone seems vague about what they're doing, "YOU usually didn't do a good job of defining the role or giving them clear outcomes, guidelines, or training" — not that they're a leech.
- **Pruning the garden**: cutting weeds (bad employees/members) "can sometimes be painful in the short term, but is necessary for the health of the overall organism."
- **Who really grew the business**: Hormozi credits client-financed acquisition; his wife Leila (who "ACTUALLY grew our business") credits **weekly one-on-ones**.

## Worked Example: The Weekly Team Meeting Agenda
9:00–9:12 Trainers (community vibe, client wins, losses to address, red-flagged/missing people, workout stuff, music). 9:12–9:28 Challenge manager (all challengers' progress + issues + plans). 9:28–9:41 Operations manager (last week's numbers + this week's plans, billing issues, pull-asides, supplement goal). 9:41–10:00 Owner (new internal promotion + video/text release schedule, handwritten-card reminder, praise). As the team grows, each slot shortens and sharpens. "And yes, you pay people to attend this meeting."

## Mental Models
- "You want to not be needed? Then don't be needed" — hand the meetings to the ops manager and shut up.
- One-on-ones are coaching your team — "Who else could give you a positive ROI for your coaching time? YOUR TEAM."
- "Seek first to understand… most times mistakes happen because of miscommunication, not from lack of character."

## Anti-patterns
- Having no defined cadence (growth left to chance).
- Talking *at* people in one-on-ones instead of asking questions (you stay needed).
- Talking past your time slot in meetings ("No need to ever just talk because you have the floor").

## Key Takeaways
1. Run daily huddles, weekly team meetings, and weekly one-on-ones — rigidly, "as consistent as the sun rising."
2. In one-on-ones, listen and ask: What's going well? Not well? How can I help you win more?
3. Vague employees usually mean unclear outcomes — clarity from you fixes it.

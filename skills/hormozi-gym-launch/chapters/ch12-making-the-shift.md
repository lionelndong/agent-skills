# Chapter 12: Making The Shift

## Core Idea
The levers only work if you can **communicate the change** to the two parties who'll resist it — **trainers and clients** — without sparking a revolt. The whole thing is a sale; you want **buy-in**, not "my way or the highway." "You can never OVER-communicate."

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Telling The Trainers (3 steps)**: 1) **Frame the problem** (for them, the business, the customers) · 2) **Explain your thought process** (what you considered, pros and cons) · 3) **Explain the solution** (and what's in it for them). Talk to each trainer **individually first** — "This is 100% a sale… you want their buy-in." Tie everything to the **semis** coming soon (their path to $2,000–$6,000/month more via 25% rev-share). Present a **unified front**: "If any member of the team questions it — or worse, creates doubt in the members — it could make this really difficult."
- **Telling Your Members**: announce over **multiple mediums** — direct mail (with a stamp), email, social post w/ video (**comments turned off**), unlisted YouTube video, text blast. A full swipeable letter is provided ("What We Currently Do / What Is Coming"), framing the price increase as funding a **massive service upgrade** (3x/week appointments, smaller classes, accountability, events, specialty programs). Include a **Refer-a-Friend** offer (a free month per referral) to soften the $10–$15/week increase.
- **Dealing With The Loud Customers**: turn off comments on the video; expect loud customers for weeks but **recognize it as NOISE** ("It is in their best interest to convince you NOT to change"); **only discuss the business side IN PERSON** (no Messenger/DM/email/FB threads) — "0% chance of miscommunication."

## Key Concepts
- **The firing heuristic**: "I have never fired someone and not found someone better to replace them." "As soon as you think it, it's going to happen, so it might as well happen sooner."
- **Reframe for trainers**: "Don't think about how much workout time they're losing, but how much attention they're gaining."
- **The cost of leaving is high**: "The costs of leaving a community to find a new home, new friends, and a new routine are very high, much higher than $10–$15/week."

## Worked Example: The Member Letter
The swipeable letter opens with gratitude, lists current investments (great coaches, continued education, new equipment), then "What Is Coming" (facility improvements, member events, specialty programs), then the big reveal: switching from "come whenever you can" to "you now have an appointment with a trainer 3x/week," plus weekly billing ("an increase of about $10–$15/week… final result $39/week"). It addresses any prior lifetime-rate promise head-on ("I have not been very good at… understanding the financial realities of running a small business… I have recently invested in business coaches… it is simply impossible for us to grow and provide the level of service… without this change"), launches Refer-a-Friend, and forbids gossip-mill social posts. Signed by hand. "P.S. I love you all, seriously."

## Mental Models
- Both audiences boil down to a choice: "the guaranteed path to burnout or the potential to transform your entire business. Both have risk, but only one can get you what you want."
- People hate change "even if it's better and more convenient for them" — so **switch everything at once** and absorb the kickback in one window.

## Anti-patterns
- Framing the change as a dictate instead of a sale (kills buy-in).
- Letting members debate the "business side" publicly (gossip mill).
- Keeping a trainer who "acts like they own the place and second-guesses your every decision."

## Key Takeaways
1. Sell the change to trainers individually (frame → thought process → solution); tie it to their upside.
2. Announce to members across many channels with a service-upgrade frame + Refer-a-Friend cushion.
3. Loud pushback is noise; handle the business side in person only.

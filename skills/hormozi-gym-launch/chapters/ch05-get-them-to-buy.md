# Chapter 5: Get Them To Buy

## Core Idea
**Selling is serving.** Sales is three transfers (belief, confidence, conviction) that overcome a prospect's resistance to change. Never answer "How much is a membership?" with a number — and build a **consistent environment + consistent conversation** (a sales system) so anyone on your team can close.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The Three Transfers Of Sales**: 1) Transfer of **belief** in the product · 2) transfer of **confidence** that the person can reach their goals · 3) transfer of **conviction** that they can overcome their circumstances. "Without transferring these three things, you will not be able to help people." (Forged from **4,000 one-on-one closes**.)
- **The "How Much Is A Membership?" Problem**: never answer the price question with a number. The real answer is **"It depends on your goal."** Consumers ask price only because they don't know what else to ask; the salesperson's job is to **educate, provide context, and break beliefs**.
- **The Sales Greeting, Waiting Area & Office** (a consistent *environment*):
  1. Greet emphatically **by name within 10 seconds** (team should run).
  2. **Stacked sign-in sheet** (make up names / have current clients sign).
  3. **Crazy testimonial wall/room** as the waiting area (before/after photos).
  4. Have them fill out the **pre-sell intake questionnaire**.
  5. Escort to a sales area where you **NEVER sit across from the prospect — sit shoulder to shoulder, same side**; keep lots of chairs and a **big stack of signed contracts** in view.
  - The setup must shout: you're in demand, an authority, lots signed up recently, scarcity (almost full) + urgency (program starts soon).

## Key Concepts
- **Selling is serving**: "If you don't like selling, it means you don't like helping people make decisions to help themselves."
- **A sales system = leverage + scale**: make all conversations the same to get a consistent outcome and stack the chips in your team's favor when you're not there.
- **You have to suck before you get good** — "it's only a lack of practice, not a lack of inclination, that makes it hard."

## Worked Example: The Walk-In Catastrophe
A walk-in asks Josh about "memberships and pricing." Josh hands him a clipboard with a **pricing sheet**, says "let me know if you have questions," and walks back to his office. The guy looks at the sheet, looks around, sets it down, and **leaves, never to be seen again.** CRASH. "Like watching a car accident in slow motion." The lesson: handing over a price sheet (a number) with no context, no pain discovery, no relationship = a lost sale.

## Mental Models
- Squat down to **eye level** and read the questionnaire to them — "people appreciate when someone inconveniences themselves." Details matter.
- The reason everyone tells you they're "broke" or you're "too expensive" is that **you answered the price question with a number** before giving context.

## Anti-patterns
- Answering "how much?" with a number (no pricing context → price comparison).
- Sitting across a table from the prospect (perceived distance/adversarial).
- Having no sales system, so outcomes depend on who happens to be working.

## Key Takeaways
1. Sales = transferring belief, confidence, and conviction — and that's serving.
2. Answer price with "It depends on your goal," then educate and break beliefs.
3. Engineer a consistent environment (10-sec greeting, testimonial wall, shoulder-to-shoulder, social proof) so the room sells for you.

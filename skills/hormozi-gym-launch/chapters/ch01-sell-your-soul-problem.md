# Chapter 1: The Broken "Sell Your Soul" Acquisition Problem

## Core Idea
The standard microgym acquisition model — a cheap low-barrier offer (LBO) like "$21 for 21 Days" — **nets zero growth** because the weeks spent running the campaign let your normal attrition eat the new members. You spend money and time to stay exactly where you started.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The "Sell Your Soul" Problem**: give away services/time/money on the front end, hope to make it up on the back end. Only works with deep pockets (the big-box gyms are owned by private-equity groups who can wait a year for ROI).
  - The math: spend $1,500 → 100 leads → 30 trials at $21 → keep 50% (15 EFTs). But over the 6 weeks of selling (14 days) + fulfillment (21 days) + tracking/resell (7 days), **10%/month churn runs ~1.5x**, so 100 starting EFTs → 85. The 15 new EFTs merely offset attrition. **Net growth: zero. Cost: ~$1,199 + your time.**
- **LBO (Low Barrier Offer)**: a cheap front-end offer ($21/21 days). The thing to STOP doing.
- **EFT (Electronic Funds Transfer)**: a recurring monthly member on auto-pay. The unit you're trying to grow.

## Key Concepts
- **A Different Set Of Rules**: big-box gyms (24 Hour Fitness, LA Fitness) are PE-owned; they play a game you can't win by their rules. "If we are trying to play their game by their rules, we are going to lose. Every. Single. Time."
- **Churn / attrition**: industry average ~10% per month — the silent force that nullifies cheap-offer campaigns.
- **Trial-to-membership conversion**: industry average is 35% (this book's gyms beat it).

## Mental Models
- Think of cheap front-end offers as **a treadmill**: lots of motion (leads, trials, sign-ups) but no forward progress once churn catches up.
- Whenever you say "this won't work for my business," swap it for **"how will I make this work?"**

## Anti-patterns
- **Competing on price / racing to the bottom**: "There are no winners in this race, only frustrated and broke people."
- **Running cheap LBOs without the capital to wait** for back-end payback — that's the PE model, and you aren't PE.

## Key Takeaways
1. The conventional cheap-offer model produces zero net growth for a bootstrapped gym — churn eats the gains during the campaign window.
2. You lose by playing the big-box game; you need a different model (client-financed acquisition, Ch 2–3).
3. The fix is to stop competing on a cheap commodity price and decommoditize with an irresistible high-ticket offer.

# Chapter 10: The Overhead Lever

## Core Idea
"It's not what you make, it's what you keep." The smallest macro lever, but **~90% of gyms over-fulfill** — too many half-empty sessions. Use the **80% service margin** standard and the margin math to cut sessions to what you actually need.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The Three Overhead Micro Levers**: 1) **Number of sessions** · 2) **Total work hours** · 3) **Cost of those hours** (compensation). "I don't like to adjust compensation unless it is absolutely essential."
- **The 80% Service Margin** (the gold standard): **(Revenue − Cost of Trainers) ÷ Revenue ≥ 80%.** "As soon as we get a gym past that point, it is profitable and self-sustaining." (Exception: very high rent → needs higher.)
- **The Empty Session Problem**: ~90% of gyms over-fulfill — not "too good" sessions, just **too many** of them. Happens when a client says "Can you do 7 o'clock?" and the owner thinks "another $120/month, sure" until the schedule is packed with empty sessions. "Only open new sessions when you are certain you can fill them."
- **Cut Sessions To Maximize Efficiency**: compute how many sessions you actually need (members ÷ capacity ÷ attendance) and cut to it. "You need to become BETTER before you become BIGGER."
- **Calibrate Trainer Pay**: standardize pay across trainers ("calibration"). Hormozi has found phenomenal trainers at **$15/hour** (~$18.30 with payroll tax). "I HATE decreasing pay… but if the option is keeping the doors open or closing them, you need to feed the business first. That is your obligation as the owner."

## Worked Example: 66% → 84.6% Margin
A bootcamp: 100 EFTs @ $118, 38 sessions/week = 163/month. Revenue $11,800; revenue/session = $11,800 ÷ 163 = **$72**. Trainer cost $20/hr × 1.22 payroll = $24.40/session → 163 × $24.40 = $3,977. Margin: ($72 − $24.40) ÷ $72 = **66%** ("just 14% off"… but the industry runs on 12% margins, so 14% may be your *entire* take-home).
**Fix:** 100 members ÷ 2 (3x/week) = 50/day → at 85% attendance = 42.5/day → could run **3 sessions/day**, but cut to **4** for headroom. 4×5 + 3 Sat = 23/week × 4.3 = **99 sessions/month** (a 61% reduction). New: revenue/session = $11,800 ÷ 99 = **$119**. Calibrated pay $15 × 1.22 = $18.30 → margin = ($119 − $18.30) ÷ $119 = **84.6%** (or **79.5%** if you keep the $24.40 pay). Cutting sessions alone added **$1,561/month ($18,732/year)**; calibrating pay added another **$7,200/year**.

## Mental Models
- "There's no secret sauce… It's just about making mathematical decisions within the context of human psychology."
- A client coming 20×/month at $120 is paying you **$6/session** — at 6 people/session you barely break even on what's supposed to be your profit center.
- Being "only" 7–14% under 80% margin can equal **half your net income** in a low-margin industry.

## Anti-patterns
- Adding a session every time one client asks for a new time → packed schedule, empty sessions, creeping payroll.
- Letting trainers out-earn the owner without recalibrating.
- Ignoring the margin math because "14% isn't a big deal" — "a naive entrepreneurial move."

## Key Takeaways
1. Know your margin math; target the 80% service margin.
2. Cut unneeded sessions (drop to 3x/week) — doing less often earns more.
3. Calibrate trainer pay only if needed, but feed the business first.

# Patterns — $100M Playbook: Proof Checklist

Concrete, reusable techniques from the playbook. Format: When to use / How / Trade-offs.

## Claim Your Proof (data-first claims)
**When to use**: before writing any advertising claim.
**How**: collect your data first → make only claims the data already supports → "claim your proof," don't "prove your claim." State how and when the data was collected.
**Trade-offs**: caps your claims at what's true (the point); requires real measurement before marketing.

## The Top-Ads Audit (bucketing)
**When to use**: overwhelmed by years of assets, or trying to find what actually works.
**How**: rank all assets by performance → take the top performers → split them into theme buckets → the winning pattern "sticks out like a sore thumb" and becomes a repeatable framework.
**Trade-offs**: needs a database of past performance; takes more than fifteen minutes.

## Get the Result Before They Buy
**When to use**: highest-stakes persuasion; whenever you can afford it.
**How**: deliver the maximum achievable result pre-purchase via live demonstration or working for free (teeth-whitening swish-and-spit; cleaning a stain on the doorstep).
**Trade-offs**: costs time/money up front; not always feasible at scale — but it's the most compelling proof there is.

## Fly-In Customers for Events (In-Person / Live)
**When to use**: you have a big event and strong customers.
**How**: fly best customers in to tell their stories live; spotlight them as close to your pitch as possible.
**Trade-offs**: travel cost — offset by selling far more units while they're there.

## Build Capture Into the Customer Journey
**When to use**: always; proof is fleeting.
**How**: record as many client experiences as possible so breakthroughs are pre-captured (e.g., always film the final weigh-in); add testimonial prompts at key milestones; teach customers how to structure a testimonial; give incentives if they don't comply.
**Trade-offs**: operational overhead — but "may be one of the most profitable things you ever do."

## Show, Don't Tell (before/after artifacts)
**When to use**: any visual transformation.
**How**: capture spliced before/after in the same room and lighting (unboxing-style) so prospects see exactly what to expect, rather than hearing it described.
**Trade-offs**: requires consistent capture conditions; weaker categories may have no visual to show.

## Personal-Pain Interviewing
**When to use**: collecting testimonials.
**How**: ask probing questions to surface specific, vivid before/after detail ("why was this so important?"; "describe the conditions that forced you to act") so prospects think "this is me."
**Trade-offs**: more interview effort; generic answers must be re-probed.

## Avatar-Matched Proof
**When to use**: you have one or several customer types.
**How**: deliberately map the demographics you attract (age, gender, ethnicity) and represent each in proof; one avatar → all testimonials from that type; many avatars → show each.
**Trade-offs**: needs a diverse-enough proof library to match each segment.

## Volume Plays: Proof Page / Reel / Wall of Results
**When to use**: conquering a platform or sales room with sheer quantity.
**How**: build a proof page, a stitched testimonial video, and/or a wall of results; add a call-to-action button every three-to-five testimonials (Hormozi's page had 5,000+).
**Trade-offs**: long pages can be heavy — but people read them all night; "Proof works. Use it."

## Local Proof Tip (floor-to-ceiling reviews)
**When to use**: physical lobby or waiting room.
**How**: print every 5-star review from Google/Yelp/etc., cheap-frame them, line the lobby floor to ceiling; keep a physical or digital notebook of proof for customers to scroll while they wait — especially proof from customers just like them.
**Trade-offs**: only applies to in-person/local businesses.

## Borrowed Third-Party Verification
**When to use**: your platform is less trusted than another where you have proof.
**How**: move proof from a trusted site onto the less-trusted one (Amazon reviews on a non-Amazon page); add BBB endorsement, NSF certification, industry awards. "You don't need to believe me, believe them."
**Trade-offs**: depends on the third party being genuinely credible; awards take effort to win.

## Numbers + Visualization
**When to use**: any claim you can quantify.
**How**: collect milestone data (sign-up, onboarding, 30-60-90 days); state numbers plainly with how + when collected; visualize with charts/graphs ("the right graph can be more compelling than a page of numbers"); if data sucks, fix the product.
**Trade-offs**: requires data infrastructure; honesty cap (hold nothing back).

## Two-or-Three Metaphors per Mechanism
**When to use**: explaining complex unique mechanisms / proprietary process / product tech.
**How**: build two or three metaphors connecting the unknown to something the audience already knows; teach them to sales staff; use in advertising; match the metaphor to the audience (car stuff for car people).
**Trade-offs**: a mismatched metaphor confuses; needs audience knowledge to land.

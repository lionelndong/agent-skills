# Chapter 3: The Proof Checklist

## Core Idea
Some proof works better than other proof. This is the checklist of **13 head-to-head comparisons** (each written as `Better > Worse`) that make any given piece of proof more compelling. Whenever possible, push every testimonial toward the left-hand side of each rule.

## The 13 Comparison Rules (exact names — preserve `>` form)

### 1) In-Person > Virtual
Whenever possible, make the proof **in-person**. The extreme: walk up to a customer's door and clean a stain on their pavement in front of them.
- **Make It Happen**: if you have a big event, fly your best customers in to tell their stories — you'll make up the cost by selling more units. Spotlight them as close to your pitch as possible.

### 2) Live > Recorded
A customer telling their transformation story **live** (on stage or live webinar) beats playing a video of the exact same testimonial. Wherever possible, use **live testimonials** over recorded ones — they are literally "living proof."

### 3) Raw > Processed
**Raw** (unedited) proof beats polished, edited proof.
- **Make It Happen**: teach customers how to structure a testimonial; provide bonuses/incentives for them to proactively send them; remind them at key milestones; **build testimonial prompting into your customer journey.**

### 4) Show > Tell
**Showing** the result visually beats **telling**. A spliced before/after video (same room, same lighting, a month apart) beats someone *describing* the change. This is why unboxing videos are so compelling — they show exactly what to expect.
- **Make It Happen**: record as many client experiences as possible so breakthrough moments are already captured (e.g., always capturing the final weigh-in of weight-loss challenges). Ask customers to document key moments; if they won't, give an incentive.

### 5) Other People Saying Your Stuff's Great > You Saying Your Stuff's Great
No one cares what *you* think about your product — you're the most biased person in existence. People care what **other customers** think.
- **Make It Happen**: put a step in your customer journey to ask people to leave reviews on review sites.

### 6) Identical to Them > Opposite of Them
Show proof from people **identical** to the prospect. If you have multiple avatars, show results from each; if you have only one type, all testimonials should be from that type.
- **Make It Happen**: deliberately think about the demographics you attract (age, gender, ethnicity) and represent each in your advertising and proof. (Dr. Kashey helps all ages get in shape — so his testimonials show all ages doing exactly that.)

### 7) Personal > Generic
**Specific, personal** pain and benefit beat **generic** language. "I was fat. Then I got skinny" is boring; the vivid version (chafing thighs, oversized clothes, a sexless marriage → heels, dresses, a promotion) makes the prospect think **"this is me."**
- **Make It Happen**: in interviews, keep asking probing questions ("why was this so important to you?"). Ask customers to be specific about the pain before and the relief after.

### 8) Big Results > Small Results > No Results
A **big** result beats a small one, which beats none. There's a place for small results (always better than bad or zero), but if you have a big one, use it as much as you can and capture it in as many formats as possible.
- **Make It Happen**: reach out to your longest-tenured, biggest-result customers; pay money, invest time, do whatever it takes to capture their result — it pays dividends for years.

### 9) Newer Proof > Older Proof
**Recent** proof beats stale proof. If your testimonials aren't up to date, people assume you haven't had a good result since. (Investment-services example: a competitor with testimonials from *during and after* a market crash beats your pre-crash testimonial.) Note: review sites have no "sort by oldest" — because no one cares.

### 10) More Proof > Less Proof
**More** proof beats less. Quality can be limited by platform, so conquer with volume.
- **Make It Happen**: build a proof page, a video of all testimonials together, a **wall of results** — one or all. (Hormozi ran a page with over **5,000 testimonials** and a call-to-action button every three-to-five testimonials; people stayed up all night reading it. "Proof works. Use it.")

### 11) Third Party Verification > Zero Verification
Accreditations, industry awards, certifications, and public approval by a **trusted third party** make your existing proof more compelling — like a brand endorsement: "you don't need to believe me, believe them."
- **Make It Happen**: tie in platforms where you have trusted reviews; apply for industry awards. Take proof from a trusted site onto a less-trusted one (Amazon reviews on a non-Amazon page; BBB endorsement; NSF certified).

### 12) Proof With Numbers > Proof Without Numbers
People can't easily argue with **understandable numbers** laid out plainly. Always say **how** you collected the data and **when**; hold nothing back; state facts and tell the truth. If your data sucks — fix your product. Make numbers more compelling by "showing not telling" with charts and graphs.
- **Make It Happen**: collect all the customer milestone data you can (sign-up, onboarding, 30-60-90 days). "This may be one of the most profitable things you ever do."

### 13) Metaphors > Technical Jargon
Use **metaphors** to connect what people don't know with what they do, instead of technical jargon for your unique mechanisms / proprietary process / product technology. Come up with two or three metaphors, teach them to your sales staff, and use them in advertising.
- **How**: match the metaphor to what the audience already understands — for car people use car stuff; for sewing people use sewing stuff.

## Worked Examples (from the doc)
- **Cale's testimonial (Big Results)**: took his gym from a handful of members to completely full with **200+ members**, fully outsourced, and sold it within **18 months for $250k**. His testimonial converted more than any other.
- **The Rollover/Winback note → Numbers, real claims** (page 20): the proof must match the claim:
  - Claim: help people start a business online → Proof: **54.1%** of people who start a paid community on Skool make money.
  - Claim: help gym owners run more profitable businesses → Proof: in their first complete year, the average **Gym Lord adds over $100,000 in profit** to their gym.
  - Claim: if we buy your company, we'll make it more valuable → Proof: the average Acquisition.com portfolio company increased its **EBITDA by 3.1x in their first 12 months.** "Data matters."
- **SEO metaphor (Metaphors)**: "Ads are like working for commissions — you only get paid on what you put in, and if you stop, so does the pay. SEO is more like an investment account — in the beginning you keep putting money in and very little comes out, but over time it compounds until it's your main source of income."

## Anti-patterns
- **Polishing proof until it looks like an ad**: over-editing kills the "raw," believable quality (violates Raw > Processed).
- **Describing instead of showing**: a testimonial that only narrates the result is weaker than one that shows it.
- **Letting proof go stale**: old-only testimonials signal you haven't succeeded recently.
- **Hiding your data collection method**: numbers without "how and when" lose trust.

## Key Takeaways
1. Run every testimonial through the checklist and push it toward the left of each `>` rule.
2. The highest-leverage rules: In-Person/Live, Show, Other-People, Personal, Big Results, and Numbers (with method + date).
3. Build the capture into your customer journey so breakthrough moments are recorded before they happen.

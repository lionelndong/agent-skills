# Chapter 2: Three Models Of Pricing + My Rules For Pricing

## Core Idea
**Price on value, not on cost or on competitors.** "People usually price by simply looking at what everyone charges. This is the recipe for break even and burnout." Value-based pricing lets you charge 2–5x market rates (or more) and keep raising as you add value. Then a set of hard-won pricing *rules* tells you when and how far to push.

## Frameworks Introduced

- **The Three Big Pricing Models**:
  1. **Cost Plus Pricing** — your costs plus an arbitrary margin. *Pro:* simple, covers costs. *Con:* you miss everyone who'd pay more; costs change and aren't fully known; **"your customers have no idea what it costs you, so your costs don't matter to them."**
  2. **Competitor Based Pricing** — the average of what everyone else charges. *Pro:* simple, maybe near market WTP. *Con:* "You're a copycat… based on their business and their customers." **"Be Cinderella, not the stepsisters."**
  3. **Value Based Pricing** ← the goal. Price on the customer's **willingness to pay (WTP)**, not the competition's willingness to charge. *Pro:* charge multiples of market rate; **continuously raise prices if you continuously add value**; forces you to improve the product and talk to customers. *Con:* more (and different) work — focuses on value per customer over raw customer count.
  - When to use: always aim for value-based; the other two are fallbacks/sanity checks.

- **Three Metrics To Determine Value-Driven Pricing**: to adjust a price, look at (1) **how many buy at the current price** (conversion rate), (2) **how many times they buy / how long they stay** (churn). For non-luxury goods, raising price means people buy *less often*. **"The perfect price is the one that makes you the most money (not gets you the most customers)."**
  - How: build a table — `Price | Clicks | Conv Rate | Sales | Churn | LTV | Total Return | Difference` — and **pick the row with the highest Total Return** (highest lifetime gross profit), not the most sales.

## Key Concepts
- **Willingness To Pay (WTP)** — what a customer will actually pay; the basis of value-based pricing.
- **Customer Surplus (goodwill)** — the gap between value delivered and price charged; dictates word-of-mouth and repurchase.
- **Total Return** — lifetime gross profit per price point; the number you optimize in the metrics table.
- **NPS score** — how you objectively measure whether you've raised price too far ("value for the cost").
- **The Lookback Window of Value** *(Author Note theory)* — customers judge your value within their **last billing cycle**, not the whole relationship; longer cycles → less churn.

## Mental Models
- **Use value-based pricing when you can add value over time** — it lets you raise prices forever and pulls you closer to customers.
- **Think of a premium price as a tool, not a goal**: "having a premium price so that I have the gross profit to make a far superior product… resulting in a customer surplus for them and net profit for me."
- **Bill on the longest increment you can; display on the smallest.** "This costs $5 per day" but bill $1,825/year.

## Anti-patterns
- **Cost-plus / competitor pricing**: leaves money on the table and shapes your business around someone else's.
- **Mixing one-time and on-going value into one price**: info gets underpriced, accountability overpriced — "Pricing should be tied to value created." Separate them (one-time access fee + smaller recurring fee).
- **Caving at the first "no"**: raising price *means* hearing more nos; reverting to get more yeses optimizes for yeses, not money.
- **Billing too frequently**: "The more often you bill the more often people cancel."

## Worked Example: The Metrics Table
| Price | Clicks | Conv Rate | Sales | Churn | LTV | Total Return | Difference |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| $10 | 100 | 5% | 5 | 10% | $100 | $500 | – |
| $20 | 100 | 4% | 4 | 10% | $200 | $800 | 60% |
| $100 | 100 | 2% | 2 | 33% | $300 | $600 | 20% |

Doubling $10→$20 dropped conversion only 5%→4% but **Total Return rose to $800 (the winner)**. Jumping to $100 cut sales and raised churn, so Total Return fell to $600. **Pick the $20 row** — highest total lifetime gross profit, not the most sales.

## Hormozi's Rules of Pricing (the foundation)
- **Raising prices makes money two ways:** more gross profit per customer AND lower delivery cost (fewer customers). *Gym example: tripled price $99→$299, lost 30% of customers (200→140); revenue more than doubled while costs dropped 30%.*
- **High close rate = prices too low.** Consistently closing >50%? Raise prices.
- **Full / fixed capacity = prices too low.** Sold out (e.g. artist capped at 10 paintings/mo)? Raise prices.
- **Keep raising until extra money from new sales no longer offsets lost sales.** "Hear more nos, make more money." 9 times out of 10 you profit more than you lose.
- **Raising price usually means raising value** — bigger increase, bigger value bump.
- **Hearing "no" more ≠ making less money** — owners cave to get yeses instead of profit; don't.
- **How to know you went too far:** sales stop, or people complain about value-for-cost (measure with NPS).
- **Multiple customer types → multiple prices.** You likely have 2–3 avatars; one may have 5–10x the WTP of another. Name tiers after an **aspirational title the customer wants for themselves**, and make sure each knows which tier is theirs.
- **Believe in your price** — "if not, make it better."
- **Match how you bill to how you provide value** — separate one-time from on-going.
- **Bill as much as you can while keeping customers happy** (preserve customer surplus).

## Key Takeaways
1. Aim for value-based pricing (WTP), not cost-plus or competitor pricing — it lets you charge multiples and raise forever.
2. The perfect price maximizes **Total Return** (lifetime gross profit), not sales count — build the metrics table and pick the highest row.
3. High close rate or full capacity are tells that price is too low.
4. Different avatars need different prices; name tiers aspirationally.
5. Bill on the longest cycle you can (less churn), display on the smallest increment (lower perceived price).

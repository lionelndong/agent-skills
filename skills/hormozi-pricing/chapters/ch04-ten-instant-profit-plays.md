# Chapter 4: The 10 Instant-Profit Pricing Plays

## Core Idea
Ten specific, low-effort pricing plays you can implement almost overnight — each designed to raise revenue with **minimal or zero impact on conversion**. Together they provide a **26.8%–63.8%** price increase. Each play follows the same structure: how Hormozi learned it, how it works, examples, and how to use it. **You only need one.**

## The Ten Plays (exact names preserved)

### Play #1 — Monthly to 28-Day Billing Cycles → **+8.3%**
Monthly billing = 12 cycles/year; billing **every 28 days = 13 cycles**. That extra cycle is pure profit, and people convert at the same rate → instant, permanent **8.3%** revenue lift (a 20%-margin business goes to 26.1% margins, +41.5% net profit). *Learned from a gym owner: "I program everything in 4-week meso cycles. My payroll is every two weeks. So I might as well bill that way."*
- **How:** change new contracts immediately; set a date for everyone else; be upfront ("call it a price increase"); explain as reinvestment.
- **Advice:** bill every **4 weeks** (weekly/bi-weekly caused pause-related billing hassles). **Display the price weekly** (lowest perceived price) but **bill every 4 weeks** (or 12 weeks+).

### Play #2 — Processing Fees & Second Form of Payment → **+3–4%**
After the customer agrees to price, ask **"How did you want to pay?"** then **"Great, it's just a 3.99% card processing fee."** *("I've never seen anyone lose a sale because of adding a processing fee.")* The clever add-on: if they hesitate, offer to **waive the fee for a second form of payment** — "if a card declines we use resources to get a new one, and about 7% of cards do every month. If you save us time and effort, we pass those savings to you."
- **Why it really matters:** recurring payments suffer **1.2%–1.7% monthly *involuntary* churn** from card changes (can be 24–34% of total churn at 5% churn). A backup card recovers that. *Example: $100/mo, 5% churn → adding a 2nd card cuts churn to 3.3–3.8% → LTV $2,000 → $2,631–$3,030 (+31% to +51%).*
- **How:** change scripting; authorize both cards; re-run failed transactions next day on the backup.

### Play #3 — Sales Tax → **+0–10%**
Stop eating sales tax. "If you have 20% margins and have to pay 5% sales tax, you're giving away 25% of your profit." *Learned from a Canadian marketer who invoiced $25,000 → $27,000 (8% provincial tax): "I don't cover taxes. My price is what I get."*
- **Rule:** **You cannot charge taxes you do not pay.** But if you *do* pay them, pass them on.
- **How:** get agreement on price first; put on the invoice as a **separate line item before the total**, dry/matter-of-fact: *"State tax code [1030a.o] mandates that personal services are subject to 6% sales tax."* If they balk, **"get in the angry boat with them"** — be more annoyed than they are ("I hand it right over to Uncle Sam").
- **Pro tips:** consider incorporating in a **state with no sales tax** (keep the profit without charging customers; check with lawyers). On a menu, add a line: *"all prices subject to sales tax and an additional 4% processing fee."*

### Play #4 — Annual Price Increases (CPI) → **+3–10%**
Build a **fixed annual price increase into contracts** so you don't watch margins erode to inflation. *Learned from a real-estate friend on rent increases (avg ~3.18%/yr).* "If you charged $100 in 2017 and never changed it… you'd have no profit only seven years later."
- **Examples:** 5%/yr → $100 → $122 over 4 yrs (+22%). 12%/yr → $100 → $157 (compounding).
- **Fun Fact:** **Warren Buffett raised See's Candies prices 50+ times** in 51 years (up to 17% in a year) → $1B+ profit.
- **How:** pick **5–15%**; add to new contracts. Script: *"…we keep our prices standard with the CPI, so we don't have any incentive to cut on the quality of what you get as the price of goods goes up."* Bring it up while filling out paperwork, **not during the sale.** Few balk under 15%.

### Play #5 — Annual Billing → **+10–15% (churn/LTV)**
Less frequent billing = less churn. ProfitWell data: **1x/yr = 2% monthly churn → 5.35x LTV vs monthly; 4x/yr = 5% churn → 2.14x; 12x/yr = 10.7% churn.** You can **5x LTV by getting customers to pay annually.** Fewer convert (price is 12x the monthly), so test whether it drops conversion *more than 5x* (usually not). *Learned from Patrick Campbell, who sold ProfitWell for $250M.*
- **Pro Tip (high conversion + annual):** sell a short term first (6–12 weeks), build trust, then **upsell into prepaying a full year.**
- **Selection rates:** annual option on a page → 10–15% pick it; as the *default* → 30%; over the phone → 35–40%. At 30% prepay with 16% discount, average first transaction jumps to **$370**.
- **How / Always start with the highest price:** offer full annual ($1,200) first → ask if they'd like a discount → prepaid annual −17% → if no, prepay quarter −8% → if still no, standard monthly. *"The first number that comes out of your mouth anchors the entire conversation."* Position prepay as a *benefit*, not monthly as a *penalty*. **"0 risk more money play."**

### Play #6 — Round Up → **+1–3% (real-world 4–11%)**
Change prices ending in **7 → 9**, and add **.99**. No change in conversion, meaningful revenue. *Discovered after a grocery-store "round up to donate" prompt, while billing weekly and losing 10% to weekly pauses.*
- **Pro Tip — when NOT to:** **luxury** items end on round numbers (0 or 5) because luxury buyers *don't* want a deal. **"Premium does not mean luxury"** — premium price reflects product value; luxury value comes *from* the price. Everything else: .99 works wonders. Test it.

### Play #7 — Annual Renewal Fee On Top of Monthly → **+10%**
Advertise a low monthly, then add a once-a-year renewal fee. *Learned from mentor John's tanning-salon chain (sold to LA Fitness):* "people cared a lot about the monthly… but never looked at the annual price." Contracts had a **start-up fee + monthly rate + annual renewal fee.** *$39/mo × 12 = $468; add a $99 annual fee → $567/yr = effective $47/mo (+20%).* You advertise $39 but effectively get $47 — low price for sales, high price for profit.
- **How:** pick a renewal fee **1–3x the monthly rate** (0.5x = +4.15%, 1x = +8.3%, 2x = +16.6%, 3x = +24.9%). Charge it on the contract anniversary (day-one or year-end both work).
- **Reason-why script:** *"You pay this for rate protection. If you don't agree to the fee, you'll be subject to any price changes we make."* Alt: lets them go month-to-month after year 1 with no cancellation fee. Have them **initial next to both** the rate and the fee (call it "rate protection" or "cancellation fee prepayment").
- **Pro Tip:** have **both a setup fee and a renewal fee** so you can waive one to get the other.

### Play #8 — Automatic Continuity → **+10%**
Create the most **paired-down, zero-work, high-margin version** of your thing, price it at **5–20% of the main offer**, and **bolt it onto the end of every front-end purchase** as the default — it recurs automatically. *("Continued Access" — a workshop speaker made $750k/yr "from one line on my contract"; Hormozi made $150k/month of pure profit, so passive his COO didn't know what the line item was.)*
- **Mechanism:** **sunk-cost fallacy** — "they already spent X, they might as well pay 5–10% to keep it." Like **"storage" for services**: they don't want to use it, but don't want to lose it.
- **Worked math:** main thing $2,000/mo × 4 mo × 70% = $5,600 LTV. Add continuity at **$200/mo (10%), 90% margins, 20 months, 50% take rate → +$1,800 LTV ($5,600 → $7,400, +32%)**; profit up 25–100%.
- **Don't be a sneak:** must be **agreed up front** — not undisclosed/forced continuity. People like knowing there's a cheaper version "at the end of the rainbow."
- **How:** for each front-end product, find a high-value/low-cost feature → price it 5–20% → bolt on, earned only after X time (helps stick rate) → remarket to that pool. Works even for one-time-transaction businesses.

### Play #9 — Ultra High Ticket Anchor → **+10–15%**
Add something **10x+ more expensive** than your core offer (the "mac daddy"). It anchors the price so your core offer looks affordable — **and some people buy the expensive thing.** *Learned from a tailor: a $16,000 suit (almost an audible gasp) made the $2,000 suit feel like relief, even though Hormozi had budgeted $500.*
- **Worked math:** before — 80% take $500 + 20% take $200 downsell = **$440 LTV.** Add a **$5,000 option at 10% take** (others shift to 70%/$500 + 20%/$200) → **$890 LTV** (doubled). Bonus: the anchor closes *more* people on the $500.
- **How:** think of the most absurd version of your thing at a price you'd happily deliver for; **offer it first** to "get the gasp," then bring out the main offer. "Make sure you are actually willing to do the thing… if you feel stressed when people buy it, keep raising the price until it makes you smile."

### Play #10 — Guarantee & Warranty Upsells → **+5–20%**
After the person agrees to buy, offer a **guarantee/warranty for 5–30% of the price.** You're **buying and selling risk** — "People hate risk and will pay for you to take it for them." *Learned from Apple (AppleCare → billions/yr, formerly free); replicated by selling an existing 10-year warranty for 10% of product price.*
- **Never-lose pricing:** set the guarantee price = your **cost of goods** (e.g. COGS 10% → guarantee 10%) so you can re-deliver instead of refund and never lose a dollar. Want guarantee revenue to **exceed the cost of fixing** the product.
- **Worked math:** $1,000 thing, $100 guarantee, $100 replacement cost, 1 in 20 buys it → (20 × $100) − $100 = **$1,900 pure guarantee profit.**
- **Pro Tips:** works at *any* price (cf. shipping insurance); can **cover ~half your sales commissions** (if half take a 10%-of-price upsell, you recoup 5% of revenue).
- **Script (post-purchase):** *"You just want the standard warranty on that?"* → "it's for anyone who likes that extra bit of peace of mind… A lot of people do it." Check at claim time whether they bought it. You can still refund non-buyers (be the nice guy) — "might as well make a buck on it." Works especially well with **'main street' / traditional** businesses.

## Mental Models
- **Use the play with the highest impact ÷ least work/risk first** — not all ten at once.
- **Anchor with the highest number first** (Plays #5, #9): "the first number that comes out of your mouth anchors the entire conversation."
- **Sell risk and 'invisible' products** (Plays #8, #10) — high-margin add-ons bolted to an existing sale are the closest thing to free money.
- **Separate display price from billing** (Plays #1, #7): show the small number, collect the big one.

## Anti-patterns
- **Undisclosed / forced continuity** (Play #8): "Don't be a sneak" — must be agreed up front.
- **Charging tax you don't pay** (Play #3): you legally cannot.
- **Adding .99 to luxury goods** (Play #6): luxury value comes from round, premium-feeling prices.
- **Mentioning the annual CPI increase *during* the sale** (Play #4): raise it at paperwork, not at the close.

## Key Takeaways
1. Ten plays stack to **26.8%–63.8%** more revenue; each engineered to barely move conversion. You only need one.
2. Billing mechanics (28-day cycles, annual billing, renewal fee) and anchoring (highest price first) are the biggest low-risk levers.
3. Bolt-on, high-margin "invisible" products — automatic continuity and priced guarantees/warranties — are near-free profit.
4. Round 7s to 9s and add .99 everywhere except luxury.
5. Pass through processing fees and sales tax instead of eating them; a backup card recovers involuntary churn.
